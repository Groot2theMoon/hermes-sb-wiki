# The Shifted Boundary Method for Embedded Domain Computations. Part I: Poisson and Stokes Problems.

A. Main<sup>a,\*</sup>, G. Scovazzi<sup>a</sup>

<sup>a</sup>Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Duke University, Durham, North Carolina 27708

## Abstract

We propose a new finite element method for embedded domain computations, which falls in the category of surrogate/approximate boundary algorithms. The key feature of the proposed approach is the idea of *shifting* the location where boundary conditions are applied from the true to the surrogate boundary, and to appropriately modify the *shifted* boundary conditions, enforced weakly, in order to preserve optimal convergence rates of the numerical solution. This process yields a method which, in our view, is simple, efficient, and also robust, since it is not affected by the small-cut-cell problem. Although general in nature, here we apply this new concept to the Poisson and Stokes problems. We present in particular the full analysis of stability and convergence for the case of the Poisson operator, and numerical tests for both the Poisson and Stokes equations, for geometries of progressively higher complexity.

**Keywords:** Embedded methods; immersed boundary method; small cut-cell problem; approximate domain boundaries; weak boundary conditions; finite element method.

## 1. Introduction

One main advantage of embedded and immersed boundary methods over geometrically conformal computational methods (i.e., methods in which numerical grids conform to the geometrical shape of the physical domains of interest) is that the task of mesh generation becomes much easier (and, often times, trivial). However, this ease in mesh generation contrasts with the additional difficulties in the enforcement of boundary conditions, from both the mathematical and data structure perspectives.

Broadly speaking, immersed and embedded methods employ one of two techniques to enforce the boundary conditions. In the first approach, the flow equations are discretized continuously both inside and outside the physical domain, and a forcing function is introduced on the physical boundary, with the purpose of imposing the boundary conditions. It was observed in [21] that immersed methods can be interpreted in the context of variational formulations as penalty methods. In the second approach, the equations are discretized and solved only on the physical domain, and outside regions are excluded from the computation. For the sake of clarity, in what follows, the former class will exclusively be referred to as *immersed* boundary methods, and the latter as *embedded* boundary methods. Confusingly, these two terms are used interchangeably in the literature for either discretization method, despite the fact these techniques are quite distinguishable from one another.

Of the two approaches to non-conformal boundaries, immersed boundary methods are older, dating to the seminal paper of Peskin [33]. Immersed boundary methods were applied in a finite element context in [8, 9, 10]. While immersed boundary methods are generally easy to implement, they suffer from two shortcomings: first, they spread (or diffuse) the action of the interface over a few grid cells, and second, they typically are only first order accurate. In general this is a consequence of the utilization of a continuous approximation of the pressure across the immersed interface, when in fact the pressure is discontinuous. A number of attempts were made in the past to improve the order of convergence [20, 19, 27, 28, 29], and although generally successful from a mathematical perspective, these strategies had often resulted in complicated implementations.

Instead, embedded boundary methods generally do not have these problems, as they employ a “sharp interface approximation.” Within a finite element context, this is typically done by means of weak enforcement of boundary conditions through Nitsche’s method [31] in combination with XFEM strategies, to construct the solution approximation spaces [21]. In contrast to a simple penalty approach, the benefit of the Nitsche method is that it provides a mechanism to weakly and consistently enforce boundary conditions. Although the Nitsche method also introduces a quadratic penalty term, this can be more appropriately interpreted as a stabilization operator for the overall variational problem [14]. Dimensional analysis indicates that the penalty stabilization must scale like the inverse of the mesh size, and, consequently, the associated penalty parameter need not be particularly large. Hence, unlike in a pure penalty approach, enforcing boundary conditions weakly via Nitsche’s method does not adversely affect the conditioning of the discretized problem.

Unfortunately, the standard Nitsche-XFEM method suffers from instabilities on so-called small-cut elements. These elements are cut by the interface in such a way that only a small fraction of them remains inside the physical domain, and consequently their effective mesh size becomes extremely small. As a consequence, these areas of small support require an arbitrarily large penalty parameter, thereby destroying the good conditioning properties of Nitsche’s method.

A variety of clever techniques have been attempted to circumvent this difficulty. Mourad *et al.* [30] introduced bubble functions on the cut elements for stabilization, and demonstrated their technique on a model problem for the Poisson equation. A different method was attempted in [2, 3] for interface problems. In their approach, the interface boundary conditions are weighted so that, while maintaining consistency, the resulting variational form is stable without the need for the penalty parameter to be arbitrarily large. It is not clear, however, how to extend this innovation outside the context of interface problems, in the sense that the special weighting technique in [2, 3] requires materials to lie on both sides of the interface (consider, as a counterexample, the case of fluid over a rigid body).

For single material problems, Burman [11] introduced the *ghost penalty method*, in which the variational form is stabilized by introducing an additional penalty term on the jump of the solution gradient at the interface separating cut and uncut elements. This method was applied to the Stokes problem in [12]. It was further utilized for two and three dimensional Navier-Stokes calculations in [38], and for two-phase flow in [37]. The drawback of these methods is two-fold. First, the introduction of a fourth-order operator in the ghost penalty term may have a delicate implementation in the nonlinear case, and negatively affect the overall condition number of the ensuing algebraic system of equations. Second, and perhaps more importantly, the introduction of the ghost penalty term increases the stencil size when using low-order finite elements, with additional complications in the case of parallel computing.

A third approach introduced for B-spline finite elements known as *Extended B-splines* [22, 23] involves eliminating via an extrapolation procedure those cut B-splines with small support. This technique was applied to the Navier-Stokes equations for moving boundary problems in [35] and [36].

Unfortunately all of these methods require the geometric construction of the partial elements cut by the embedded boundary. This is a complex computational geometry calculation, which for general interfaces is both algorithmically complicated and computationally intensive. Furthermore, integrating the variational forms on the characteristically irregular cut cells which result may also be difficult. Typically some sort of adaptive quadrature is used [32, 16]. Accordingly, it is typical that a non-negligible portion of the overall wall-clock time for a simulation is spent handling the embedded boundary. A similar challenge exists for embedded boundary methods of finite volume type, which can suffer from a similar though technically different version of the small cut cell problem.

A manner in which this problem can be circumvented within the context of finite volume methods is through the introduction of *approximate domain* methods, in which the true domain is replaced by an approximation, which, in what follows, we term the *surrogate domain*. Boundary conditions are then accounted for along the frontier of the surrogate domain. The surrogate domain is appropriately chosen so that the integration and application of boundary conditions become much simpler (e.g., by constructing it only with whole cells of the background mesh). The problem then reduces to determining effective (i.e., accurate and robust) boundary conditions on the surrogate boundary – a major challenge in approximate boundary methods and still a topic of intense research.

One of the earliest approximate domain methods was proposed in [34] for inviscid multiphase compressible flow. Later on, this original method was referred to as the Ghost Fluid Method (GFM) and versions of it were applied to multiphase compressible flow in [18, 1], to compressible fluid-structure interaction in [25], and to multiphase fluid/structure interaction in [41]. An approximate domain approach has also been utilized for viscous incompressible flow in [26], where, however, the approximated domain concept is only harnessed for the continuity equation.

Although without strong theoretical foundations, the Ghost Fluid Method proved very effective for the simulation of practical problems, due to its ease of implementation and avoidance of the small cut cell instability.

In this work, we propose a new approach that falls in the category of embedded finite element methods and leverages a surrogate/approximate boundary strategy. The key feature of the proposed approach, which, to the authors knowledge, is also new in this field of research, is the idea of *shifting* the location where the boundary conditions are applied from the true to the surrogate boundary, and, most importantly, of appropriately modifying the *shifted* boundary conditions in order to avoid a reduction in the convergence rates of the overall formulation. In fact, we show that if the boundary conditions originally applied to the true domain are not appropriately modified, only first order convergence can be expected. The appropriate (modified) boundary conditions are then applied weakly, using, for example, a Nitsche strategy (other weak enforcement strategies are also possible). Because of its specific structure, we name this new method *Shifted Boundary* (SB) method. This process yields a method which is simple, robust, accurate and efficient. Although general, we specifically apply this concept to the Poisson and Stokes flow problems. We present in particular the full analysis of stability and convergence for the case of the Poisson operator, and numerical tests in multiple dimensions for the Poisson and Stokes equations, in a battery of progressively more complicated geometries.

## 2. Preliminaries: General notation, the true domain, the surrogate domain and maps

Consider the domain  $\Omega$ , an open set in  $\mathbb{R}^{n_d}$  with Lipschitz boundary  $\Gamma = \partial\Omega$ , where  $n_d$  is the number of space dimensions. Let  $\mathcal{T}_h$  be a shape-regular triangulation (in the sense of Ciarlet) constituted by a family of non-overlapping  $n_d$ -partitions/elements  $\Omega_e$  of  $\Omega$  (triangles/squares for  $n_d = 2$  or tetrahedra/cubes for  $n_d = 3$ ) such that  $\bar{\Omega} = \bigcup_{e=1}^{n_{el}} \bar{\Omega}_e$  (where  $n_{el}$  is the total number of elements). We denote by  $h_e = h_e(\Omega_e)$  the diameter of element  $e$  and  $h = \max_{\Omega_e \in \mathcal{T}_h} h_e$ . Denoting by  $\omega \subset \Omega$  a portion of  $\Omega$  (e.g., an element domain  $\Omega_e$ ), and by  $\gamma$  a portion of  $\Gamma$ , we define with

$$(v, w)_\omega = \int_\omega v w \quad \text{and} \quad (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})_\omega = \int_\omega \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} \quad (1a)$$

the  $L^2(\omega)$ - and  $(L^2(\omega))^{n_d}$ -inner products on the interior of  $\omega$  and with

$$\langle v, w \rangle_\gamma = \int_\gamma v w \quad \text{and} \quad \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \rangle_\gamma = \int_\gamma \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{w} \quad (1b)$$

boundary functionals on  $\gamma$ . In particular, the space  $L^2(\mathcal{E})$  refers to the set of functions whose traces are square integrable on the interior or exterior boundary set  $\mathcal{E}$ . Let  $\|v\|^2 = (v, v)_\omega$ , and let  $W_j^k(\Omega)$  be the Sobolev space with norm

$$\|v\|_{W_j^k(\Omega)} = \left( \sum_{\alpha \leq k} \|D_x^\alpha v\|_{L^j(\Omega)}^j \right)^{1/j}, \quad 1 \leq j < \infty. \quad (2)$$

For  $j = 2$ , let  $H^k(\Omega) = W_2^k(\Omega)$  and set  $\|v\|_{W_2^k(\Omega)} = \|v\|_{k, \Omega}$ . By extension, let  $L^2 = H^0(\Omega)$ , so that  $\|v\|_{L^2(\Omega)} = \|v\|_{0, \Omega}$ .

Consider now an embedded discretization, in which the computational grid does not conform to the boundary, but is actually overlapped with it (see Fig. 1). The computational grid intersects the true boundary  $\Gamma$  of the domain  $\Omega$ . We can then introduce a *surrogate boundary*  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  composed of the edges/faces of the mesh that are the closest to the true boundary  $\Gamma$ .  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  can be constructed, for example, by computing the intersections of the grid and the true boundary  $\Gamma$  and using closest-point projection algorithms to detect the closest face/edge of  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  to  $\Gamma$ . Other choices are of course possible, as long as the overall topology of  $\Gamma$  and  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  are close to each other, that is if  $\Gamma$  has a certain number of holes, the same number of holes needs to also be present also in  $\tilde{\Gamma}$ . To a certain extent, the construction of  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  from  $\Gamma$  is a computational geometry problem, and several techniques can be borrowed from this field. The surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  encloses the *surrogate domain*  $\tilde{\Omega}$ . In particular,  $\tilde{n}$  indicates the unit outward-pointing normal to the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$ , to be distinguished from the outward-pointing normal  $n$  of  $\Gamma$ . We now define the map

$$M : \tilde{\Gamma} \rightarrow \Gamma, \quad (3a)$$

$$\tilde{x} \mapsto x, \quad (3b)$$

**Remark 1.** Note that the closest-point projection, in spite of the segmented/faceted nature of the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  is actually a smooth map from points in  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  to points in  $\Gamma$ , if the boundary  $\Gamma$  is sufficiently smooth (e.g. Lipschitz), and this is the primary reason why we propose it as the back-bone of the map  $M$ .

In particular, it will become very important to characterize the map  $M$  though a distance vector function

$$d_M(\tilde{x}) = x - \tilde{x} = [M - I](\tilde{x}). \quad (4)$$

**Remark 2.** Other strategies in the definition of the map  $M$  and distance vector  $d_M$  are possible, among which a level set description of the true boundary, in which  $d_M$  is defined by means of a distance function. Most importantly, the methods that we are about to introduce do not depend on how the map  $M$  is constructed (e.g., analytically, by solving an Eikonal-type equation, or by explicitly computing intersections of the grid with triangulated surfaces). In our case, for simple shapes we calculate  $M$  analytically, while for more complex shapes we intersect triangulated boundary surfaces with given computational grids using tools from the library developed by [40].

In what follows, for the sake of simplicity, the subscript in the definition of  $d_M$  will be omitted and we will simply write “ $d$ .” If the closest-point projection is used, the vector  $d$  is aligned with  $n$ . We also state an important assumption:

**Assumption 1.** *The distance vector is defined as  $d = \|d\|n$ , where the normal  $n$  to the true boundary and the normal  $\tilde{n}$  to the surrogate boundary satisfy*

$$n \cdot \tilde{n} \geq 0. \quad (5)$$

The condition  $n \cdot \tilde{n} \geq 0$  means that we require that  $\tilde{n}$  lies on the half-plane identified by the normal  $n$ , a situation that is always verified in practice.

**Remark 3.** It may be tempting to interpret condition (5) as a smoothness condition. In reality, it is easy to observe that it is realized in the case a polygonal boundary curve/surface with obtuse angles (concave). Therefore it is not clear whether this interpretation is the most appropriate. Possibly, a better interpretation is to consider (5) as a condition on geometric resolution. If details of the true geometry have scales much finer than the computational grid, then it is easy to break condition (5). Note also that the angle between  $n$  and  $\tilde{n}$  can easily reach  $45^\circ$ , as is the case, for example, of a circular boundary immersed in a uniform, logically structured grid of squares.

Through the map  $M$ , it is possible to define the extension  $\tilde{\psi}$  on  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  of a function  $\psi$  initially defined only on  $\Gamma$ , as

$$\tilde{\psi}(\tilde{x}) \equiv \psi(M(\tilde{x})) . \quad (6)$$

For example, the unit normal vector  $n$  and unit tangential vectors  $\tau_i$  ( $1 < i < n_d - 1$ ) to the boundary  $\Gamma$  can be extended to the boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  as follows:

$$\tilde{n}(\tilde{x}) \equiv n(M(\tilde{x})) , \quad (7a)$$

$$\tilde{\tau}_i(\tilde{x}) \equiv \tau_i(M(\tilde{x})) . \quad (7b)$$

In what follows, with the purpose of simplifying the notation, we will omit the bar from the expressions of  $\tilde{n}$  and  $\tilde{\tau}_i$ , whenever this does not cause confusion. Therefore when we write  $n(\tilde{x})$  we actually mean  $\tilde{n}(\tilde{x})$ , and similarly for  $\tau_i(\tilde{x})$  and  $\tilde{\tau}_i(\tilde{x})$ . We can also introduce the derivative of a function  $\psi$  along the directions  $\tilde{n}$  and  $\tilde{\tau}_i$  at a point  $\tilde{x} \in \tilde{\Gamma}$  as

$$\psi_{,\tilde{n}}(\tilde{x}) = \nabla\psi(\tilde{x}) \cdot \tilde{n}(\tilde{x}) = \nabla\psi(\tilde{x}) \cdot n(M(\tilde{x})) , \quad (8a)$$

$$\psi_{,\tilde{\tau}_i}(\tilde{x}) = \nabla\psi(\tilde{x}) \cdot \tilde{\tau}_i(\tilde{x}) = \nabla\psi(\tilde{x}) \cdot \tau_i(M(\tilde{x})) . \quad (8b)$$

These constructions are particularly useful if we consider the solution  $u$  to a partial differential equation and, for example, we desire to build an extension of the Dirichlet boundary condition  $u_D$  from the true boundary  $\Gamma_D$  to a surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ , and write

$$\tilde{u}_D(\tilde{x}) = u_D(M(\tilde{x})) , \quad (9a)$$

$$\tilde{u}_{D,\tilde{\tau}_i}(\tilde{x}) = \nabla u_D(\tilde{x}) \cdot \tilde{\tau}_i(\tilde{x}) . \quad (9b)$$

In particular, the following Taylor expansion formula centered at  $\tilde{x} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_D$  holds for  $x = M(\tilde{x}) \in \Gamma_D$ :

$$\begin{aligned} 0 &= u(\tilde{x}) + \nabla u(\tilde{x}) \cdot (x - \tilde{x}) - u_D(x) + O(\|x - \tilde{x}\|^2) \\ &= u(\tilde{x}) + \nabla u(\tilde{x}) \cdot (M(\tilde{x}) - \tilde{x}) - u_D(M(\tilde{x})) + O(\|M(\tilde{x}) - \tilde{x}\|^2) \\ &= u(\tilde{x}) + \nabla u(\tilde{x}) \cdot d(\tilde{x}) - \tilde{u}_D(\tilde{x}) + O(\|d(\tilde{x})\|^2) , \end{aligned} \quad (10)$$

where we have used the fact that  $u(x) = u_D(x)$ . The last expression in the chain of equalities can be used to develop a new strategy for the imposition of Dirichlet boundary conditions in the context of embedded methods. Intrinsically, this approach is at most second-order accurate, unless additional terms in the Taylor expansion are included.

**Remark 4.** Note that the closest-point projection, in spite of the segmented/faceted nature of the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  is actually a smooth map from point in  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  to points in  $\Gamma$ , and this is the primary reason why we propose it as the backbone of the map  $M$ . In our experience, the closest-point projection algorithm performed robustly even for geometries with sharp edges (as the one shown in Figure 12). The tests shown in this article seem to corroborate this argument in the general sense and an extensive testing campaign, beyond the scope of the present work, is under way.

## 3. Introducing the Shifted Boundary Method: The Poisson problem with Dirichlet boundary conditions

The key aspects of the proposed embedded boundary method are probably best understood in the case of the Poisson problem with (non-homogeneous) Dirichlet boundary conditions. We take then this simple problem as a point of departure and we will progressively increase the level of complexity later on, with a discussion on the Stokes equations.

### 3.1. Strong, weak and Nitsche variational forms of the Poisson problem on conformal grids

We consider first the case in which the computational grid conforms to the boundaries of the geometric domain of interest, so that no embedded technique is necessary. The strong form of the Poisson problem with non-homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions amounts to finding the solution  $u \in C^2(\Omega)$  of

$$-\Delta u = f , \quad \text{on } \Omega , \quad (11a)$$

$$u = u_D , \quad \text{on } \Gamma_D = \partial\Omega . \quad (11b)$$

The weak statement of (56) requires less regularity on  $u$  and reads

Find  $u \in H^1(\Omega)$  such that,  $\forall w \in H_{0,D}^1(\Omega)$ ,

$$(\nabla u, \nabla w)_\Omega = (f, w)_\Omega, \quad (12)$$

where  $H_{0,D}^1(\Omega)$  is the space of functions in  $H^1(\Omega)$  with zero trace on  $\Gamma_D$ . In [31, 4, 15], Nitsche introduced a consistent weak formulation in which Dirichlet boundary conditions were enforced weakly. Introducing a discrete approximation subspace  $V^h(\Omega) \subset H^1(\Omega)$  (typically given by continuous piecewise polynomial functions), the Nitsche weak form reads:

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\Omega)$  such that,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\Omega)$ ,

$$(\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_\Omega - \langle w^h, \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} - (\nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n}, u^h - u_D)_{\Gamma_D} + \langle \alpha/h^\perp w^h, u^h - u_D \rangle_{\Gamma_D} = (w^h, f)_\Omega, \quad (13)$$

where  $\alpha$  is a so-called penalty parameter and  $h^\perp$  is a characteristic length. In computations  $h^\perp$  is typically chosen to be the characteristic length of the elements in the direction orthogonal to the boundary and it is bounded above by  $h$ . Nitsche [31] showed that the solution to (13) converges to the solution of (12) in the limit as  $h^\perp \rightarrow 0$ .

**Remark 5.** The nature of the Nitsche method becomes clearer if we consider the Euler-Lagrange equations associated with (13), which can be derived using integration by parts, provided  $u^h$  is replaced by the exact solution  $u$  (sufficiently smooth, e.g.,  $u \in H^2(\bar{\Omega})$ ):

$$-(w^h, \Delta u + f)_\Omega - (\nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n} - \alpha/h^\perp w^h, u - u_D)_{\Gamma_D} = 0, \quad \forall w^h \in V^h(\Omega). \quad (14)$$

Hence, the Nitsche method enforces weakly both the partial differential equation on  $\Omega$  and the boundary condition  $u = u_D$  on  $\Gamma_D$ .

### 3.2. The Shifted Boundary Method for embedded domains (unsymmetric form)

The weak Nitsche boundary conditions are quite effective in the case of conformal/body-fitted grids, but they unfortunately pose challenges when applied at embedded boundaries, for the following main reasons:

- The presence of cut elements of small size can render Nitsche's method numerically unstable and/or produce poor condition numbers in the ensuing algebraic system.
- Integrating the Nitsche variational form on cut elements is computationally expensive and difficult to implement.

Because both of these issues emanate from the mere existence of cut elements near the boundary, one idea could be to exclude them altogether from the simulation. Excluding these elements has the effect of moving the boundary  $\Gamma$  of the computational domain to the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$ . Of course, if Dirichlet boundary conditions are naïvely applied on the surrogate boundary, then an  $O(h)$  error is introduced.

We develop next a new embedded finite element formulation, in which the key idea is *not* to try and apply the boundary conditions on the true boundary  $\Gamma$ , but, rather, to *shift* their point of application to the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$ . The map  $M$  defined in (4) and the Taylor formula (10) are instrumental in imposing on  $\tilde{\Gamma}$  *shifted* boundary conditions that are second-order accurate approximations to the exact boundary conditions on  $\Gamma$ . This is the reason why we have termed the proposed method *Shifted Boundary Method* (or, with an acronym, SB method). Let us introduce the discrete space of piecewise-linear continuous functions:

$$V^h(\tilde{\Omega}) = \{w^h \mid w^h \in C^0(\tilde{\Omega}) \cap \mathcal{P}^1(\Omega_e)\}. \quad (15)$$

Then, the SB method can be defined by means of the following associated variational statement:

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  such that,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,

$$\begin{aligned} (\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle w^h, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{n}, u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot d - \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot d), u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot d - \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, u_{,\tau_i}^h - \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} = (w, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}}. \end{aligned} \quad (16)$$

Also in this case, the Euler-Lagrange equations can help in the understanding of the variational form. Integrating by parts, we obtain

$$-(w^h, \Delta u + f)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{n} - \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot d), u + \nabla u \cdot d - \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, u_{,\tau_i} - \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} = 0. \quad (17)$$

Hence the variational form in the SB method enforces the partial differential equation on the interior of  $\tilde{\Omega}$  and the boundary condition  $u + \nabla u \cdot d = \bar{u}_D$  on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ . In addition, the last term in (16) and (17), namely  $\langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, u_{,\tau_i} - \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}$ , enforces a matching condition between tangential derivatives on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ , that is  $u_{,\tau_i} = \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i}$ . Here  $\beta \in \mathbb{R}^+$  is a positive scalar parameter  $O(1)$  that has to be appropriately chosen.

**Remark 6.** The term  $\langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, u_{,\tau_i}^h - \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}$  is not properly consistent, but produces only a second-order consistency error (i.e.,  $O(\|d\|^2) = O(h^2)$ ), of the same order of the consistency error associated with the boundary condition  $u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot d = \bar{u}_D$  on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ .

The tangential derivative penalization is introduced with a twofold purpose: a) deriving a simple proof of numerical stability, as shown in Section 3.4.2; and b) improving the stability of the overall formulation in the case when the computational grid is under-resolved with respect to the details of the geometry.

From a general standpoint, it is arguable whether the tangential gradient stabilization is truly necessary. For example, in the case of the symmetrized SB formulation to be discussed next in Section 3.3, this term is not necessary to prove stability and is omitted. This fact seems to suggest the possibility of a different way of proving stability in the case of the variational formulation (16). This argument is further corroborated by the fact that all computational tests presented here had been performed setting  $\beta = 0$ , with the exception of one last test for the Stokes flow problem in a very complex geometry and with a relatively coarse grid resolution.

For completeness we rewrite the variational statement (16) using the classical bilinear and linear form notations:

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  such that,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,

$$a_u^h(u^h, w^h) = l_u^h(w^h) \quad (18a)$$

$$\begin{aligned} a_u^h(u^h, w^h) &= (\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot d, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{n}, u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot d \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \nabla w^h \cdot d, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\quad + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot d), u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot d \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, u_{,\tau_i}^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \end{aligned} \quad (18b)$$

$$l_u^h(w^h) = (w^h, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{n}, \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot d), \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau_i}^h, \bar{u}_{D,\tau_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}. \quad (18c)$$

The bilinear form  $a_u^h(u^h, w^h)$  in the variational formulation is not symmetric, because of the presence of the unsymmetric term  $\langle \nabla w^h \cdot d, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}$ .

### 3.3. The shifted Boundary Method in symmetric form

It is possible to introduce a  $O(\|d\|^2)$  perturbation to the variational formulation (18) and obtain a symmetric bilinear form. With this purpose in mind, let us consider the following decompositions and recall that  $d = \|d\|n$ :

$$\nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} = \left( (\nabla u^h \cdot n)n + (\nabla u^h \cdot \tau_i)\tau_i \right) \cdot \tilde{n}, \quad (19a)$$

$$\nabla u^h \cdot d = \nabla u^h \cdot n \|d\|. \quad (19b)$$

Substituting (19b) into (19a), we obtain

$$\nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{n} = (n \cdot \tilde{n}) \|d\| \nabla u^h \cdot d + (\nabla u^h \cdot \tau_i) \tau_i \cdot \tilde{n}. \quad (20)$$

So far the derivations are exact. Consider now the following first-order approximation on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ :

$$\nabla u^h \cdot \tau_i \approx \nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \tau_i. \quad (21)$$

This approximation entails a certain level of boundary smoothness: It can be applied if the boundary is a collection of smooth segments/area patches, as is typically the case in design shapes, in which smooth surfaces are separated by edges and corners. Substituting (21) into (20) gives

$$\nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \approx (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) / \|d\| \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} + (\nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i) \boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, \quad (22)$$

which can be further substituted into (18b) to yield the symmetric variational form

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  such that,  $\forall w \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,

$$a_s^h(u^h, w^h) = l_s^h(w^h) \quad (23a)$$

$$a_s^h(u^h, w^h) = (\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ + \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) / \|d\| \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \quad (23b)$$

$$l_s^h(w^h) = (w^h, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, (\nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i) \boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}, \quad (23c)$$

where we have removed the term  $\langle \beta h^\perp w_{\tilde{\Gamma}_i}^h, u_{\tilde{\Gamma}_i}^h - \bar{u}_{D, \tilde{\Gamma}_i} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}$  since in the symmetric case it is not needed for the proof of stability. The Euler-Lagrange equations are again helpful in the understanding of the variational statement. Namely:

$$\begin{aligned} & -(w^h, \Delta u + f)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ & \quad - \underbrace{(\nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - \mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) / \|d\|}_{\|d\| \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n}} \underbrace{\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - (\boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i}_{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} = \\ & -(w^h, \Delta u + f)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ & \quad - \underbrace{(\|d\| \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n}, \nabla u \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} - (\boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i)}_{O(h)} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} = 0. \end{aligned} \quad (24)$$

This result shows that the symmetrization procedure introduces a consistency error of the same order of the consistency error due to the Taylor expansion used for the surrogate boundary conditions.

### 3.4. Analysis of the Shifted Boundary Method for the Poisson problem

We start by introducing the “broken” spaces  $H^k(\mathcal{T}_h) = \prod_{\Omega_e \in \mathcal{T}_h} H^k(\Omega_e)$  with norms  $\|\cdot\|_{k, \mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})} = \sum_{\Omega_e \in \mathcal{T}_h} \|\cdot\|_{k, \Omega_e}$ . We recall the following *discrete* trace inequalities, which are proved in [5] and will be used throughout.

**Lemma 1.** For any element  $\Omega_e \subset \tilde{\Omega}$  with an edge  $\gamma_e \subset \tilde{\Gamma}$  and any unit vector  $\mathbf{v}$  such that  $\mathbf{v} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} > 0$ ,

$$\|\sqrt{h^\perp} w\|_{0, \gamma_e}^2 \leq C_l (|w|_{0, \Omega_e}^2 + h^2 |w|_{1, \Omega_e}^2), \quad \forall w \in H^1(\Omega_e), \quad (25a)$$

$$\|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{v}\|_{0, \gamma_e}^2 \leq C_l (|w|_{1, \Omega_e}^2 + h^2 |w|_{2, \Omega_e}^2), \quad \forall w \in H^2(\Omega_e), \quad (25b)$$

where  $|w|_{1, \Omega_e}^2$  and  $|w|_{2, \Omega_e}^2$  are the  $H^1$ - and  $H^2$ -seminorms, respectively. Summing over all the elements with at least one of their edges on the boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}$ , we obtain

$$\|\sqrt{h^\perp} w\|_{0, \tilde{\Gamma}}^2 \leq C_l (|w|_{0, \mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})}^2 + h^2 |w|_{1, \mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})}^2), \quad \forall w \in H^1(\mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})), \quad (26a)$$

$$\|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{v}\|_{0, \tilde{\Gamma}}^2 \leq C_l (|w|_{1, \mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})}^2 + h^2 |w|_{2, \mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})}^2), \quad \forall w \in H^2(\mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})), \quad (26b)$$

For a function  $w^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ , that is piecewise linear and globally continuous function over the mesh  $\mathcal{T}(\tilde{\Omega})$ , inequality (26b) reduces to

$$\|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{v}\|_{0, \tilde{\Gamma}}^2 \leq C_l \|\nabla w\|_{0, \tilde{\Omega}}^2, \quad (27)$$

#### 3.4.1. Coercivity (symmetric form)

In this section we will address the stability of the symmetric form, using a coercivity argument for the bilinear form  $a_s^h$ :

$$a_s^h(u^h, u^h) = \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 - 2\langle u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \alpha \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \|\sqrt{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})/\|\mathbf{d}\|} \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2, \quad (28)$$

where we have applied condition (5) since  $\mathbf{d} = \|\mathbf{d}\|\mathbf{n}$ . The  $\epsilon$ -inequality (see Appendix A) yields

$$2\langle u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \leq \frac{1}{\epsilon} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \epsilon \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2, \quad (29)$$

where  $\epsilon \in \mathbb{R}^+$  is a positive real number that can be taken arbitrarily small. We can now use the inverse inequality (27) with  $\mathbf{v} = \tilde{\mathbf{n}}$ , to obtain

$$a_s^h(u^h, u^h) \geq (1 - \epsilon C_I) \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 + \left(\alpha - \frac{1}{\epsilon}\right) \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \|\sqrt{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})/\|\mathbf{d}\|} \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2. \quad (30)$$

Under the condition that (5) holds, stability is concluded by first choosing  $\epsilon < 1/C_I$  and subsequently choosing  $\alpha > 1/\epsilon$ . Hence:

$$a_s^h(u^h, u^h) \geq C_{\text{SB}} \|u^h\|_{\text{SB}}^2, \quad (31a)$$

where, noticing that  $\sqrt{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})/\|\mathbf{d}\|} \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} = \sqrt{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})} \|\mathbf{d}\| \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n}$ ,

$$\|u^h\|_{\text{SB}}^2 = \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 + \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \|\sqrt{(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})} \|\mathbf{d}\| \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2, \quad (31b)$$

$$C_{\text{SB}} = \min \left( 1 - \epsilon C_I, \alpha - \frac{1}{\epsilon} \right). \quad (31c)$$

#### 3.4.2. Coercivity (unsymmetric form)

In the case of the unsymmetric form, we have instead

$$\begin{aligned} a_u^h(u^h, u^h) &= \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 - 2\langle u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\quad + \alpha \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \beta \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2, \end{aligned} \quad (32)$$

where we have used the abbreviated notation  $u_{,\tau}^h = u_{,\tau}^h \tilde{\tau}_i$  implying  $\|u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n_u-1} \|u_{,\tau_i}^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2$ . Making use again of (19a)–(19b) and defining  $\partial_n u^h = \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n}$  and  $\partial_\tau u^h = \nabla u^h \cdot \tau_i$ , we obtain

$$\begin{aligned} \langle \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} &= \langle (\partial_n u^h \mathbf{n} + \partial_\tau u^h \tau_i) \cdot \mathbf{d}, (\partial_n u^h \mathbf{n} + \partial_\tau u^h \tau_j) \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &= \underbrace{\langle (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{d})(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_n u^h, \partial_n u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}}_{=\|\mathbf{d}\|} + \underbrace{\langle (\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{d})(\tau_j \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_n u^h, \partial_\tau u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}}_{=\|\mathbf{d}\|} \\ &\quad + \underbrace{\langle (\tau_i \cdot \mathbf{d})(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_\tau u^h, \partial_n u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}}_{=0} + \underbrace{\langle (\tau_i \cdot \mathbf{d})(\tau_j \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_\tau u^h, \partial_\tau u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}}_{=0} \\ &= (\|\mathbf{d}\| \langle (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_n u^h, \partial_n u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \|\mathbf{d}\| \langle (\tau_j \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \partial_n u^h, \partial_\tau u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}). \end{aligned} \quad (33)$$

Recalling that  $\mathbf{d} = \|\mathbf{d}\|\mathbf{n}$  and  $\|\mathbf{d}\| \leq h^\perp$  and using then (5), we obtain

$$\begin{aligned} \langle \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} &\geq \|\sqrt{\|\mathbf{d}\|(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})} \partial_n u^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 - |\langle (\partial_n u^h \mathbf{n}) \cdot \mathbf{d}, (\partial_\tau u^h \tau_j) \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}| \\ &\geq -\|\sqrt{h^\perp} \partial_n u^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\geq -\left(\frac{\epsilon}{2} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2 + \frac{1}{2\epsilon} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D}^2\right), \end{aligned} \quad (34)$$

where also  $\epsilon_1 \in \mathbb{R}^+$  is a positive and otherwise arbitrary scalar. We can apply again the inverse inequality (27), with  $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{n}$ :

$$\langle \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} \geq -\frac{\epsilon_1}{2} C_I \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 - \frac{1}{2\epsilon_1} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D}^2 \quad (35)$$

and we obtain

$$a_u^h(u^h, u^h) \geq \left(1 - \left(\epsilon + \frac{\epsilon_1}{2}\right) C_I\right) \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 + \left(\alpha - \frac{1}{\epsilon}\right) \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D}^2 + \left(\beta - \frac{1}{2\epsilon_1}\right) \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D}^2. \quad (36)$$

Stability requires to select  $\epsilon$  and  $\epsilon_1$  such that

$$\left(\epsilon + \frac{\epsilon_1}{2}\right) C_I < 1, \quad (37)$$

and to subsequently select  $\alpha$  and  $\beta$  such that

$$\alpha > \frac{1}{\epsilon}, \quad \beta > \frac{1}{2\epsilon_1}, \quad (38)$$

and, consequently

$$a_u^h(u^h, u^h) \geq C_{\text{SB}_u} \|u^h\|_{\text{SB}_u}^2, \quad (39a)$$

with

$$\|u^h\|_{\text{SB}_u}^2 = \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 + \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D}^2 + \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D}^2, \quad (39b)$$

$$C_{\text{SB}_u} = \min \left(1 - C_I \left(\epsilon + \frac{\epsilon_1}{2}\right), \alpha - \frac{1}{\epsilon}, \beta - \frac{1}{2\epsilon_1}\right). \quad (39c)$$

#### 3.4.3. Boundedness (continuity)

The bilinear forms associated with the symmetric and non-symmetric forms of the SBM methods are continuous (*bounded*). In fact, using Lemma 1 and the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality, we have, for the non-symmetric form:

$$\begin{aligned} a_h^h(u^h, w^h) &= \langle \nabla w^h, \nabla u^h \rangle_{\Omega} - \langle w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} + \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \langle \alpha h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\Gamma_D} + \langle \beta h^\perp w_{,\tau}^h, u_{,\tau}^h \rangle_{\Gamma_D} \\ &\leq \|\nabla w^h\|_{0,\Omega} \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega} + \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n}\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}\|_{0,\Gamma_D} + \alpha \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \beta \|\sqrt{h^\perp} w_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\leq (1 + C_I^2) \|\nabla w^h\|_{0,\Omega} \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega} \\ &\quad + C_I \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\nabla u^h\|_{0,\Omega} + C_I \|\nabla w^h\|_{0,\Omega} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \alpha \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\quad + \beta \|\sqrt{h^\perp} w_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \|\sqrt{h^\perp} u_{,\tau}^h\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \\ &\leq C_{b_u} \|w^h\|_{\text{SB}_u} \|u^h\|_{\text{SB}_u}, \end{aligned} \quad (40a)$$

that is, the non-symmetric bilinear form is continuous (bounded) with continuity constant  $C_{b_s} = \max \{1 + C_I^2, \alpha, \beta\}$ . Analogous derivations for the symmetric form lead to

$$\begin{aligned}
a_s^h(u^h, w^h) &= (\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_{\bar{\Omega}} - \langle w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, \nabla u^h \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}}, u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}, (\mathbf{n} \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}}) \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\quad + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\leq (1 + C_I^2) \|\nabla w^h\|_{0, \bar{\Omega}} \|\nabla u^h\|_{0, \bar{\Omega}} \\
&\quad + C_I \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0, \bar{\Gamma}_D} \|\nabla u^h\|_{0, \bar{\Omega}} + C_I \|\nabla w^h\|_{0, \bar{\Omega}} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0, \bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\quad + \alpha \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0, \bar{\Gamma}_D} \|\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u^h + \nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{d})\|_{0, \bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\leq C_{b_s} \|w^h\|_{\text{SB}_s} \|u^h\|_{\text{SB}_s},
\end{aligned} \tag{40b}$$

where  $C_{b_s} = \max \{1 + C_I^2, \alpha\}$ .

#### 3.4.4. Stability (or well-posedness, in the sense of Hadamard)

Both the symmetric and non-symmetric forms are continuous (bounded) and coercive. Then, for each of them, we can apply Lax-Milgram's theorem (see, e.g., [17] p. 83) to derive that there exist a unique discrete solution, which depends continuously on the data. This is the well-posedness statement in the sense of Hadamard.

#### 3.4.5. Asymptotic consistency (consistency error estimates)

The SB method is a so-called *asymptotically* consistent method, meaning that it does not satisfy the classic Galerkin error orthogonality/consistency condition, but rather we have

$$a_s^k(e, w^h) = O(h^k), \tag{41}$$

where  $e = u - u^h$ ,  $a_s^k(v, w^h) = a_s^k(v, w^h)$  or  $a_s^k(v, w^h) = a_s^k(v, w^h)$  and  $k = 2$  for linear polynomial interpolation. We give next the argument for the estimate (41) in the context of both the unsymmetric and symmetric formulations. In order to proceed in the analysis, let us define by  $u|_{\bar{\Gamma}_D}$  the exact solution to (12) on the surrogate boundary  $\bar{\Gamma}_D$ . This value is well defined since  $u \in H^1(\Omega)$  has sufficient regularity and well-posed trace operators exist on interior interfaces and exterior boundaries. Integration by parts yields that the subset  $\bar{\Omega}$ , the exact solution satisfies,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\bar{\Omega})$ ,

$$\tilde{a}^h(u, w^h) = \tilde{P}^h(w^h), \tag{42a}$$

$$\tilde{a}^h(u, w^h) = (\nabla w^h, \nabla u)_{\bar{\Omega}} - \langle w^h, \nabla u \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \tag{42b}$$

$$\tilde{P}^h(w^h) = (w^h, f)_{\bar{\Omega}}. \tag{42c}$$

Comparing now with the unsymmetric formulation (18), we have that

$$\begin{aligned}
a_s^h(u, w^h) - \tilde{a}^h(u, w^h) &= \underbrace{\tilde{a}^h(u, w^h) - \tilde{P}^h(w^h)}_{=0} - (\sqrt{h^\perp} \nabla w^h \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}}, \underbrace{\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D)}_{O(h^2)})_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\quad + \langle \alpha \sqrt{1/h^\perp} (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), \underbrace{\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D)}_{O(h^2)} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \beta \sqrt{h^\perp} w_{,\bar{\tau}}^h, \underbrace{\sqrt{h^\perp} (u_{,\bar{\tau}} - \bar{u}_{D,\bar{\tau}})}_{o(h^{3/2})} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\
&\leq \|w^h\|_{\text{SB}_s} O(h^{3/2}),
\end{aligned} \tag{43a}$$

where we have used (27), the error estimate on the Taylor expansion approximating the boundary condition on the surrogate boundary  $\bar{\Gamma}_D$  and the definition of the norm  $\|\cdot\|_{\text{SB}_s}$ . Analogously, in the case of the symmetric formulation

(23), we have that

$$\begin{aligned}
a_s^h(u, w^h) - I_s^h(w^h) &= \underbrace{\tilde{a}^h(u, w^h) - \tilde{I}^h(w^h)}_{=0} - \underbrace{\langle \nabla w^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D \rangle}_{O(h^2)} \tilde{\Gamma}_D \\
&\quad + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w^h + \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{d}), \underbrace{u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D}_{O(h^2)} \tilde{\Gamma}_D \\
&\quad - \langle \sqrt{|d|} \nabla w^h \cdot \mathbf{n}, \underbrace{\sqrt{|d|} (\nabla u \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} - (\tau_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \tau_i)}_{O(h^{3/2})} \rangle \tilde{\Gamma}_D \\
&\leq \|w^h\|_{\text{SB}}, O(h^{3/2}) .
\end{aligned} \tag{43b}$$

#### 3.4.6. Accuracy (convergence)

We are now in the position at this point to prove convergence by means of the second Strang's lemma (see Lemma 3 in the Appendix and [17] for more details). In order to do so, we introduce the space

$$V_*(h) = V^h(\tilde{\Omega}) + H_0^1(\tilde{\Omega}) \cap H^2(\tilde{\Omega}) \subset H^2(\mathcal{T}_h),$$

where the asterisk in the subscript stands for “u” or “s”, for the unsymmetric and symmetric version of the SB method, respectively. The corresponding norm for a function  $w \in V_*(h)$  is  $\|w\|_{V_*(h)}$  defined as

$$\|w\|_{V_*(h)}^2 = \|w\|_{\text{SB}}^2 + \sum_{\Omega_e \in \mathcal{T}_h} h_e^2 |w|_{2, \Omega_e}^2.$$

Note that for  $w^h \in V^h$ , by definition, we have that  $\|w\|_{V_*(h)} = \|w\|_{\text{SB}}$ , and for  $w \in H^2(\Omega)$ , there exist a constant  $c$  such that  $\|w\|_{V_*(h)} \leq c \|w\|_{2, \tilde{\Omega}}$  (i.e.,  $H^2(\tilde{\Omega})$  is continuously embedded in  $V_*(h)$ ). This last inequality is due to the classical Poincaré-type inequality [5, 6] below:

**Lemma 2.** *For all  $u \in H^1(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,  $\exists C_P > 0$ , independent of  $h$  and  $u$ , such that:*

$$\|u\|_{0, \tilde{\Omega}} \leq C_P \tilde{L} \left( \|\nabla u\|_{0, \tilde{\Omega}} + \sqrt{1/h^\perp} \|u\|_{0, \tilde{\Gamma}} \right), \tag{44a}$$

where  $\tilde{L} = \text{meas}(\tilde{\Omega})^{1/n_d}$  is a characteristic length of the domain  $\tilde{\Omega}$  that does not depend on  $h$ .

This Lemma also implies that  $\|\cdot\|_{\text{SB}}$  is a norm in the proper sense. It is also important to observe that the continuity of the SB bilinear forms holds also when we consider the trial space  $V_*(h)$  in place of  $V_{\text{SB}}^h$  (the proof is immediate, by definition), that is,

$$a_s^h(u, w^h) \leq C_b \|u\|_{V_*(h)} \|w^h\|_{\text{SB}}.$$

Concluding, all the hypotheses of the second Strang's lemma are verified (see in particular the Appendix and, for complete details, [17]) and we obtain the following estimate:

$$\begin{aligned}
\|u - u^h\|_{\text{SB}} &\leq \|u - u^h\|_{V_*(h)} \\
&\leq \left( 1 + \frac{\|a_s^h\|_{V_*(h) \times V^h(\tilde{\Omega})}}{C_{\text{SB}}} \right) \underbrace{\inf_{v^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})} \|u - v^h\|_{V_*(h)}}_{O(h)} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{C_{\text{SB}}} \sup_{v^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})} \frac{|l^h(v^h) - a_s^h(u, v^h)|}{\|v^h\|_{\text{SB}}}}_{O(h^{3/2})} \\
&\leq Ch,
\end{aligned} \tag{45}$$

where either  $C_{\text{SB}} = C_{\text{SB}}$ , or  $C_{\text{SB}} = C_{\text{SB}}$ , and  $\|a_s^h\|_{H^1(\tilde{\Omega}) \times V^h(\tilde{\Omega})} \leq C_b$ , the continuity constant equal to either  $C_b$ , or  $C_{b_s}$ . Hence, the SB method, either in its unsymmetric or symmetric form, is proved first-order accurate with respect to the natural norms for which we proved coercivity and boundedness.

For the symmetric form of the SB method, estimates in the  $L^2$ -norm can be obtained using a duality argument (the so-called Nitsche-Aubin trick, see [4, 17] and references therein). In the case of the proposed SB method, the analysis

is (considerably) complicated by the fact that the SB method is only asymptotically consistent, that is, the consistency error goes to zero as the mesh size is reduced and this needs to be taken into account. Consider the solution of the problem

$$\begin{cases} -\Delta\psi = u - u^h, & \text{in } \tilde{\Omega}, \\ \psi = 0, & \text{in } \tilde{\Gamma} = \partial\tilde{\Omega}. \end{cases} \quad (46)$$

In order to prove  $L^2$ -error estimates, we will need to use elliptic regularity estimates for  $\psi$  and for this reason we will assume that both  $\Omega$  and  $\tilde{\Omega}$  are convex and that  $u \in H^2(\Omega)$  (and consequently,  $u \in H^2(\tilde{\Omega})$ ), as is customary in the case of duality arguments [6, 7]. Note since  $\Omega$  is in the first place convex, it is feasible (although a little cumbersome) to generate a set of discrete mesh refinements such that  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$  stays convex for every mesh size  $h$ . Under this conditions, elliptic regularity implies that  $|\psi|_{2,\tilde{\Omega}} \leq c \|u - u^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Omega}}$  for a positive constant  $c > 0$ . By definition,  $\psi$  also satisfies a weak statement of the previous equations, that is,  $\forall w \in V(h)$ ,

$$0 = (\nabla w, \nabla \psi)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle w, \nabla \psi \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}} - \langle \nabla w \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, \psi - 0 \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}} + \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w + \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{d}), \psi - 0 \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}} - (w, u - u^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}}, \quad (47)$$

that is

$$0 = a_s^h(\psi, w) + \langle \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{n}, \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \nabla w \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{n}, (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ - \langle \alpha/h^\perp (w + \nabla w \cdot \mathbf{d}), \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{d} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - (w, u - u^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}}. \quad (48)$$

Taking now  $w = u - u^h$  and introducing the nodal interpolate  $\psi_I$  of  $\psi$  (such that  $\psi_I = 0$  on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ ), we obtain

$$\|u - u^h\|_{0,\tilde{\Omega}}^2 = a_s^h(\psi - \psi_I, u - u^h) + a_s^h(\psi_I, u - u^h) + R_c(\psi, u - u^h), \quad (49)$$

where

$$\begin{aligned} R_c(\psi, u - u^h) &= \langle \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \mathbf{n}, \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \mathbf{n}, \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\quad - \langle \alpha/h^\perp (u - u^h + \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \mathbf{d}), \|d\| \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &= \langle \sqrt{\|d\|} \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \mathbf{n}, \sqrt{\|d\|} \nabla \psi \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \langle \sqrt{\|d\|} (\tau \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \tau, \sqrt{\|d\|} \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\quad - \langle \alpha \sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u - u^h + \nabla(u - u^h) \cdot \mathbf{d}), \|d\| \sqrt{1/h^\perp} \nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D}. \end{aligned} \quad (50)$$

One by one, the terms on the right hand side of (49) can be bounded. Using continuity of the bilinear form, the classic interpolation estimate and the elliptic regularity estimate, we have that

$$a_s^h(u - u^h, \psi - \psi_I) \leq C_b \|u - u^h\|_{\text{SB}}, \|\psi - \psi_I\|_{\text{SB}} \leq C_b C_{\text{int}} \|u - u^h\|_{V_c(h)} h |\psi|_{2,\tilde{\Omega}}, \quad (51)$$

where  $C_{\text{int}}$  is the constant in the interpolation estimate. The term  $R_c(\psi, u - u^h)$  can be bounded with the help of the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality and the trace-type inequality  $\|\nabla \psi \cdot \mathbf{v}\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \leq C_{\tilde{\Gamma}} |\psi|_{2,\tilde{\Omega}}$  with  $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{n}$ , or  $\mathbf{v} = \tilde{\mathbf{n}}$  (recall also the assumption that  $\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} > 0$ ):

$$R_c(\psi, u - u^h) \leq C_{\tilde{\Gamma}} \|u - u^h\|_{\text{SB}} h^{1/2} |\psi|_{2,\tilde{\Omega}}. \quad (52)$$

The term  $a_s^h(u - u^h, \psi_I)$  requires a little extra care, since the SB method is not Galerkin consistent. In view of the symmetry of  $a_s^h$ , the Cauchy-Schwartz inequality and the consistency error estimate (43b), we have, recalling also that  $\psi_I = 0$  on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_D$ ,

$$\begin{aligned} a_s^h(\psi_I, u - u^h) &= a_s^h(u - u^h, \psi_I) \\ &= a_s^h(u, \psi_I) - l_s^h(\psi_I) \\ &= \langle \alpha \sqrt{1/h^\perp} \nabla \psi_I \cdot \mathbf{d}, \underbrace{\sqrt{1/h^\perp} (u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D)}_{O(h^2)} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \sqrt{1/h} \nabla \psi_I \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, \underbrace{\sqrt{h} (u + \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{d} - \bar{u}_D)}_{O(h^2)} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\quad - \langle \sqrt{h} \nabla \psi_I \cdot \mathbf{n}, \underbrace{\sqrt{1/h} \|d\| (\nabla u \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} - (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} - (\tau_I \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) \nabla \bar{u}_D \cdot \tau_I)}_{O(h^2)} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ &\leq C'_{\text{int}} |\psi|_{2,\tilde{\Omega}} O(h^{3/2}), \end{aligned} \quad (53)$$

where the terms in  $\psi_I$  have been bound with the seminorm  $|\psi|_{2,\bar{\Omega}}$  using the discrete trace inequalities, since, in general,  $\|\nabla\psi_I \cdot \nu\|_{0,\bar{\Gamma}_D} \leq \|\nabla(\psi_I - \psi) \cdot \nu\|_{0,\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \|\nabla\psi \cdot \nu\|_{0,\bar{\Gamma}_D} \leq (C_{int}h + C_\Gamma) |\psi|_{2,\bar{\Omega}}$ . Substituting (51), (52) and (53) into (49), we have

$$\begin{aligned} \|u - u^h\|_{0,\bar{\Omega}}^2 &\leq \left( C_b C_{int} h \|u - u^h\|_{SB_s} + C_\Gamma h^{1/2} \|u - u^h\|_{SB_s} + C'_{int} O(h^{3/2}) \right) |\psi|_{2,\bar{\Omega}} \\ &\leq C_{NA} h^{3/2} \|u - u^h\|_{0,\bar{\Omega}}, \end{aligned} \quad (54)$$

which implies, after simplification of  $\|u - u^h\|_{0,\bar{\Omega}}$ ,

$$\|u - u^h\|_{0,\bar{\Omega}} \leq C_{NA} h^{3/2}, \quad (55)$$

where  $C_{NA}$  is independent of  $h$ , but can be a function of  $\Omega$  and  $|u|_{2,\Omega}$ .

**Remark 7.** The bound on the error that we obtained is suboptimal, since a conformal body-fitted Nitsche discretization would yield quadratic convergence in the  $L^2$ -error. However, it is not clear at the moment if the above estimate is sharp, since in computations we always experience optimal, second-order convergence rates.

**Remark 8.** If higher-order elements are used, then, in order to obtain correspondingly higher convergence rates, higher-order Taylor expansions need to be used to reconstruct the Dirichlet boundary condition on the surrogate boundary  $\bar{\Gamma}$ .

## 4. An important note on Neumann boundary conditions

Within the scope of the Stokes and Navier-Stokes equations, certainly the imposition of Dirichlet boundary conditions is most critical for an embedded method. However, in some cases it may be desirable to *accurately* apply Neumann boundary conditions of embedded type. The general form of the Poisson problem in this case is

$$-\Delta u = f, \quad \text{on } \Omega, \quad (56a)$$

$$u = u_D, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_D, \quad (56b)$$

$$\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} = t_N, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_N, \quad (56c)$$

where  $\bar{\Gamma}_D \cup \bar{\Gamma}_N = \partial\Omega$  and  $\Gamma_D \cap \Gamma_N = \emptyset$ . The convergence estimate (45) obtained in the case of pure Dirichlet boundary conditions implies that the  $H^1$ -seminorm of the solution converges with first-order rate. From the duality estimate for the  $L^2$ -norm we have that the consistency error needs to be kept second order, or it will not be possible to prove that the overall error is higher than first order. Hence, a naïve approach to the imposition of Neumann boundary conditions leads to only first-order convergence of the consistency error and ultimately if the  $L^2$ -norm of solution error. Hence, we propose next a more accurate approach to Neumann-type boundary conditions, that showed better overall performance.

### 4.1. Unit normal vectors: $\mathbf{n}$ versus $\bar{\mathbf{n}}$

In a conformal mesh approach, Neumann boundary conditions of the type  $\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} = t_N$  are imposed weakly. For example, we can have the variational statement

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\Omega)$  such that,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\Omega)$ ,

$$(\nabla w^h, \nabla u^h)_\Omega = (w^h, f)_\Omega + \langle w^h, t_N \rangle_{\Gamma_N}, \quad (57)$$

to which corresponds, after integration by parts, the following Euler-Lagrange equations:

$$-(w^h, \Delta u + f)_\Omega + \langle w^h, \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} - t_N \rangle_{\Gamma_N} = 0. \quad (58)$$

Now in the case of the SB method, the integration-by-parts procedure yields a boundary term  $\langle w^h, \nabla u \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_N}$  rather than  $\langle w^h, \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\Gamma_N}$ . In order to approximately enforce the boundary condition  $\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} = t_N$ , we first recall (19a) and write

$$\langle w^h, \nabla u \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_N} = \langle w^h, (\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n}) \mathbf{n} \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_N} + \langle w^h, (\nabla u \cdot \tau_i) \tau_i \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_N}, \quad (59)$$

where we have decomposed the surrogate normal as  $\tilde{\mathbf{n}} = (\tilde{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{n})\mathbf{n} + (\tilde{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i)\boldsymbol{\tau}_i$ . At this point we proceed in the same manner as for the Dirichlet boundary conditions and adopt the approximation

$$\left( \nabla u^h + \nabla(\nabla u^h) \mathbf{d} \right) \cdot \mathbf{n} \Big|_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N} = \tilde{\tau}_N .$$

where  $\tilde{\tau}_N$  is the extension of  $\tau_N$  to the surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}_N$  via the map  $\mathbf{M}$  and  $\tilde{\tau}_N - \nabla(\nabla u^h) \mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}|_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N}$  replaces  $\nabla u^h \cdot \mathbf{n}|_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N}$ . Overall, we can then make the following substitution into the weak form:

$$\langle w^h, \nabla u^h \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_N} \leftarrow \langle w^h, (t_N - \nabla(\nabla u^h) \cdot \mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}) \mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_N} + \langle w^h, (\nabla u^h \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i) \boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\Gamma_N} . \quad (60)$$

The entire variational form for Neumann boundary conditions (with a unique solution up to a constant) is:

Find  $u^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  such that,  $\forall w^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,

$$\langle \nabla w^h, \nabla u^h \rangle_{\tilde{\Omega}} + \langle w^h, (\nabla(\nabla u^h) \cdot \mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{n}) \mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N} - \langle w^h, (\nabla u^h \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}_i) \boldsymbol{\tau}_i \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N} = (w^h, f)_{\Omega} + \langle w^h, (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}) t_N \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N} , \quad (61)$$

to which correspond the following Euler-Lagrange equations:

$$- (\nabla w^h, \Delta u + f)_{\Omega} + \langle w^h (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}}), (\nabla u + \nabla(\nabla u) \mathbf{d}) \cdot \mathbf{n} - t_N \rangle_{\Gamma_N} = 0 . \quad (62)$$

Note that the Neumann condition is tested against  $w^h (\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})$  and not  $w^h$  (as was the case for the conformal grid formulation), where the multiplier  $(\mathbf{n} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{n}})$  is always positive by condition (5). Unfortunately the expansion (60) requires the second derivative of the solution  $u^h$ , which is not readily available when employing linear elements. It becomes useful at this point to build nodal-based gradients are estimated using the scheme shown in Figure 2. Hence the troublesome Hessian  $\nabla(\nabla u^h)$  is estimated using a gradient recovery technique. At each node, the gradient is estimated using the gradient in the element away from the interface, that is, for a node with location  $\mathbf{x}$  and the distance vector  $\mathbf{d}$ , the nodal gradient is assigned to be the gradient calculated from the element that contains the point  $\mathbf{x} - \epsilon \mathbf{d}$ , where  $\epsilon$  is a small number. Having obtained the nodal gradients, the Hessian can be calculated in the standard manner in an element using the gradients of the shape functions.

Because this term does not fit neatly into the normal finite element stencil, we do not compute it as part of the stiffness matrix directly; rather we handle it in a matrix free approach. To put it concretely, if our entire linear system including this correction is

$$\mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} ,$$

we decompose  $\mathbf{A}$  as

$$\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}_0 + \mathbf{A}_{\text{corr}} ,$$

where  $\mathbf{A}_{\text{corr}}$  is the portion of the stiffness matrix corresponding to the second-order correction term (61). We form the rest of the stiffness matrix  $\mathbf{A}_0$  in the normal manner (and store it, e.g. in compressed row storage) and we use it to compute the preconditioner. Then forming matrix-vector products is simple:

$$\mathbf{A} \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{A}_0 \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{A}_{\text{corr}} \mathbf{p} .$$

## 5. Numerical results for the Poisson problem

In what follows, we present a number of tests to evaluate the performance of the proposed SB method. In particular, we focus on the convergence properties of the algorithm, and also on the trend of the condition number of the related algebraic system as the grid is refined. We compare the solution obtained with the SB method with a reference solution obtained with what we call the “conformal method,” that is with a standard Nitsche method on a grid that conforms to the true boundary (i.e., a body-fitted grid). In all the tests that follow we choose the penalty parameter  $\alpha = 20$  (for both the embedded and conformal methods) and  $\beta = 0$ .

### 5.1. Convergence test

#### 5.1.1. Dirichlet boundary conditions

To test the method for Poisson's equation with Dirichlet boundary conditions, we consider the Poisson's problem on a two-dimensional disk of radius  $R = 0.2$ , with a unit source term ( $f = 1$ ) and homogenous Dirichlet boundary conditions ( $u = 0$  over the entire  $\Gamma$ ). An exact solution exists in this case, of the form

$$u(r, \theta) = \frac{1}{2} (R^2 - r^2) ,$$

where  $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ . Figure 3 shows the convergence of the  $L^2$ - and  $H^1$ -norms of the solution for this test. The convergence of the solution is first order for the  $H^1$ -norm and second order for the  $L^2$ -norm. Comparing in Figure 3a the SB method with the reference Nitsche method on conformal grids, we observe that the former converges with optimal rates, although with  $L^2$ -errors about three times the magnitude of the latter. We argue that this is actually a small price to pay when weighted against the ease of implementation and simplicity of the method, if compared with strategies that require tedious integration along the true boundary of the computational domain. Instead, considering Figure 3b, the  $H^1$ -errors of the SB and reference method are nearly indistinguishable. #### 5.1.2. Neumann boundary conditions

To test the method for Neumann boundary conditions, we consider Poisson's equation on an annulus of inner radius  $a = 1$  and outer radius  $b = 3$ . On the outer boundary, we apply a strong (conformal) Dirichlet boundary condition, and the inner boundary is handled weakly with the shifted boundary method. As before, the source term is set to be  $f = 1$ , and the Neumann and Dirichlet boundary conditions are calculated from a chosen manufactured solution:

$$u(x, y) = \frac{1}{4} \left( 9 - x^2 - y^2 - 2 \ln 3 + \ln(x^2 + y^2) \right) + \frac{1}{4} \sin x \sinh y$$

Figure 5 shows the convergence of the  $L^2$ - and  $H^1$ -norms of the solution for this test, both with and without the correction proposed in Section 4. It is easy to appreciate that with the correction term, the convergence of the solution is first order for the  $H^1$ -norm and roughly second order for the  $L^2$ -norm. There is little difference between the errors in the  $H^1$ -norm, but, without the correction term, the convergence appears to be only first order. Adding the correction term significantly reduces the  $L^2$ -error. Over the full range of meshes tested, the average convergence rate in the  $L^2$ -norm is 1.87. The unevenness of the convergence rate is likely due to the simplicity of the gradient approximation described in Figure 2. A more sophisticated gradient recovery technique would likely improve the regularity of the convergence rate.

### 5.2. Poisson's equation in an irregular domain

In this second test, we compute the solution of the Poisson equation, again with  $f = 1$ , but this time on a domain in which multiple shapes are embedded, both smooth and non-smooth. Specifically, we consider the rectangular domain  $[-2, 2] \times [-1, 1]$ , with a circular hole of radius 0.4 and center  $(-1, -0.4)$ , and a rectangular hole of width 0.8, height 0.7, and centered at  $(1.1, 0.35)$ . The circular hole is obviously a smooth shape, (but the computational grid cuts through it), while the rectangular hole includes corners, which might pose problems of robustness and accuracy to embedded boundary methods.

On the boundary of the circle and rectangle, a homogenous Dirichlet boundary condition is applied ( $u = 0$ ), while on the external boundaries of the domain a non-homogenous Dirichlet condition ( $u = 1$ ) is applied. Only the rectangular and circular holes are embedded, while the external boundaries of the domain are handled with a standard conformal Nitsche approach.

## 6. The Shifted Boundary Method for the Stokes flow equations

This section is devoted to the mixed system of the Stokes flow equations. This case is instrumental in developing the theoretical framework that can later be applied, for example, to the Navier-Stokes equations, and other related systems of equations. In what follows, for the sake of simplicity but without lack of generality, we will assume that the Neumann conditions are imposed on conformal surfaces (i.e.,  $\bar{\Gamma}_N = \Gamma_N$ ). If this is not the case, then the extension described in Section 4 in the case of the Poisson problem can be applied. The Stokes flow system of equations, with Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions, is given by

$$\nabla \cdot (2\mu \epsilon(\mathbf{u}) - p\mathbf{I}) = \mathbf{f}, \quad \text{in } \Omega, \quad (63a)$$

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0, \quad \text{in } \Omega, \quad (63b)$$

$$\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}_D, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_D, \quad (63c)$$

$$(2\mu \epsilon(\mathbf{u}) - p\mathbf{I}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{t}_N, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_N, \quad (63d)$$

where  $\epsilon(\mathbf{u}) = 1/2(\nabla \mathbf{u} + \nabla \mathbf{u}^T)$  is the velocity strain tensor (i.e., the symmetric gradient of the velocity),  $p$  is the pressure,  $\mathbf{f}$  a body force,  $\mathbf{u}_D$  the value of the velocity on the Dirichlet boundary and  $\mathbf{t}_N$  the normal stress on the Neumann boundary. The Stokes flow represents a prototype for the application of the SB method to systems of differential equations in mixed form. We introduce next the discrete spaces  $V^h(\bar{\Omega})$  and  $Q^h(\bar{\Omega})$ , for the velocity and the pressure. We assume that a stable and convergent base formulation for the Stokes flow exist for these spaces in the case of conformal grids. For example, if we consider the piecewise linear spaces

$$V^h(\bar{\Omega}) = \{v^h | v^h \in (C^0(\bar{\Omega}))^{nd} \cap (\mathcal{P}^1(\Omega_e))^{nd}\}, \quad (64)$$

$$Q^h(\bar{\Omega}) = \{w^h | w^h \in C^0(\bar{\Omega}) \cap \mathcal{P}^1(\Omega_e)\}, \quad (65)$$

the stabilized formulation of Hughes *et al.* [24] will satisfy these assumptions. For the sake of simplicity, we will use this formulation in what follows, but alternative choices are possible, such as, for example, discontinuous Galerkin

The following SB weak form of (63) can be expressed using an unsymmetric form for the velocity strain and an unsymmetric form for the mixed pressure gradient terms. Namely:

Find  $\mathbf{u}^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  and  $p^h \in Q^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  such that,  $\forall \mathbf{w}^h \in V^h(\tilde{\Omega})$  and  $\forall q^h \in Q^h(\tilde{\Omega})$ ,

$$\begin{aligned} 0 = & (\boldsymbol{\epsilon}(\mathbf{w}^h), 2\mu \boldsymbol{\epsilon}(\mathbf{u}^h))_{\tilde{\Omega}} - (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{w}^h, p^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - (q^h, \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}^h)_{\tilde{\Omega}} - \langle \mathbf{w}^h \otimes \tilde{\mathbf{n}}, 2\mu \boldsymbol{\epsilon}(\mathbf{u}^h) - p^h \mathbf{I} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + (\mathbf{w}^h, \mathbf{f})_{\tilde{\Omega}} \\ & - \langle 2\mu \boldsymbol{\epsilon}(\mathbf{w}^h) - q^h \mathbf{I}, (\mathbf{u}^h + (\nabla \mathbf{u}^h) \mathbf{d} - \tilde{\mathbf{u}}_D) \otimes \tilde{\mathbf{n}} \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} + \alpha \langle 2\mu/h^\perp (\mathbf{w}^h + (\nabla \mathbf{w}^h) \mathbf{d}), \mathbf{u}^h + (\nabla \mathbf{u}^h) \mathbf{d} - \tilde{\mathbf{u}}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} \\ & + \beta \langle 2\mu h^\perp \nabla_\tau \mathbf{w}^h, \nabla_\tau \mathbf{u}^h - \nabla_\tau \tilde{\mathbf{u}}_D \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_D} - \langle \mathbf{w}^h, \mathbf{t}_N \rangle_{\tilde{\Gamma}_N} + (\text{stabilizing terms [24]}) . \end{aligned} \quad (66)$$

The variational statement (66), without considering stabilizing terms, can be equivalently expressed as

$$a(\mathbf{u}^h, \mathbf{w}^h) + b(p^h, \mathbf{w}^h) = l_f(\mathbf{w}^h) , \quad (67a)$$

$$b(q^h, \mathbf{u}^h) + \hat{b}(q^h, u^h) = l_g(q^h) , \quad (67b)$$

where

$$a(u^h, w^h) = (\epsilon(w^h), 2\mu \epsilon(u^h))_{\hat{\Omega}} - (w^h \otimes \bar{n}, 2\mu \epsilon(u^h))_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} - (2\mu \epsilon(w^h), (u^h + (\nabla u^h)d) \otimes \bar{n})_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\ + \alpha \langle 2\mu/h^\perp (w^h + (\nabla w^h)d), u^h + (\nabla u^h)d \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \beta \langle 2\mu h^\perp \nabla_\tau w^h, \nabla_\tau u^h \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D}, \quad (68a)$$

$$b(p^h, w^h) = -(\nabla \cdot w^h, p^h)_{\hat{\Omega}} + \langle w^h \cdot \bar{n}, p^h \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D}, \quad (68b)$$

$$\hat{b}(q^h, u^h) = \langle q^h d \otimes \bar{n}, \nabla u^h \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D}, \quad (68c)$$

$$l_f(w^h) = -\langle w^h, f \rangle_{\hat{\Omega}} - \langle 2\mu \epsilon(w^h), \bar{u}_D \otimes \bar{n} \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \alpha \langle 2\mu/h^\perp (w^h + (\nabla w^h)d), \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} \\ + \beta \langle 2\mu h^\perp \nabla_\tau w^h, \nabla_\tau \bar{u}_D \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D} + \langle w^h, t_N \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_N}, \quad (68d)$$

$$l_g(q^h) = \langle \bar{u}_D \cdot \bar{n}, q^h \rangle_{\bar{\Gamma}_D}. \quad (68e)$$

## 7. Numerical results for the Stokes flow equations

Also in the case of the Stokes flow problem, we will compare the performance of the proposed SB method against a reference Nitsche method on a conformal grid. The resolution of the grids for the SB and reference methods are similar. In all test cases we chose the penalty parameters  $\alpha = 20$  (for both the embedded and conformal methods) and  $\beta = 0$ , unless otherwise noted.

### 7.1. Convergence test: Stokes flow in an annulus

The first test case considered is Stokes flow in an annular region. The geometry of the problem is best stated in polar coordinates, where  $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$  is the radius (measured from the origin of the coordinate system) and  $\theta$  is the angle (positive counterclockwise). The domain is  $\Omega = \{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid x = r \cos \theta \text{ and } y = r \sin \theta, \text{ with } r \in (a, b), \theta \in [0, 2\pi)\}$ . The viscosity is set to 1. A stream function for this problem can be derived as

$$\psi(r, \theta) = [Ar^3 + Br \ln r + Cr + D/r] \sin \theta$$

so that the corresponding velocity components and pressure are

$$u_r(r, \theta) = [Ar^2 + B \ln r + C + D/r^2] \cos \theta, \quad (69)$$

$$u_\theta(r, \theta) = -[3Ar^2 + B(1 + \ln r) + C - D/r^2] \sin \theta, \quad (70)$$

$$p(r, \theta) = \left[ -\frac{2B}{r} + 8Ar \right] \cos \theta. \quad (71)$$

These expressions can be derived from Stokes' paradox (showing that there is no solution to the Stokes equations around a cylinder [13]). On the inside boundary of the annulus ( $r = a$ ) we apply a Dirichlet condition on the velocity. In the context of the proposed exact solution of Stokes flow, there is some arbitrariness on the boundary conditions on the outer edge of the annulus ( $r = b$ ). At this boundary we apply a stress condition, so as to uniquely fix the pressure. For convenience we choose

$$D = -Ab^4, B = -4Ab^2$$

which implies the following condition on the exterior boundary

$$\epsilon(u) \cdot n = 0.$$

In particular, we set  $a = 1$ ,  $b = 3$ , and  $A = 1$ . In all simulations, the computational grids conform to the exterior boundary. Instead, and just for the case of the SB method, the interior boundary is embedded. For both the SB and reference methods, we tested on four unstructured triangular meshes of decreasing characteristic size:  $h = 0.32, 0.16, 0.08, 0.04$ . For a given resolution, the corresponding grids for the SB and reference methods have similar element distribution, and node locations differ significantly only near the inner boundary.

### 7.2. Flow around circle/rectangle

As a second test, we compute the flow over a pair of analytical shapes - a circle and a rectangle. As in the case of the Poisson problem discussed earlier, the purpose of this example is to exhibit the performance of the method in the presence of a smooth boundary (a circle) and a non-smooth boundary (a rectangle). The problem domain is the rectangle  $\Omega = [-2, 2] \times [-1, 1]$ , in which a circular hole is present, of radius 0.4 and center  $(-1, -0.4)$ , as well as a rectangular hole of width 0.8, height 0.7 and center  $(1.1, 0.35)$ . The viscosity is set to 1. A constant velocity in the x direction,  $U = 1$  is applied at the left side of the domain, and an open boundary condition with  $p = 0$  is applied on the right. A slip (no penetration) boundary condition is applied on the top and bottom edges. On the boundary of the circular and rectangular holes (which are embedded in the case of the SB method), a no slip boundary condition is applied.

Figure 9 shows the flow streamlines for both methods, overlapped with contours of the velocity magnitude. The velocity profiles and streamlines for the two methods are nearly identical, with very few observable differences. In particular, even in the neighborhood of the corners of the rectangle, where the boundary is non-smooth, the SB method produces good quality and smooth results. In summary, consistent with the results from the Poisson problem, there are no observable oscillations in the velocity field, even in regions of sharp geometry features.

A comparison of the pressure elevation plots is shown in Figure 10. Qualitatively, the solutions are again very close. An overlay of the results from the two methods is shown in Figure 11, where the SB method is shown as a black wireframe and the conformal result as a solid surface. The results are practically matched. In Table 1, we show the flow forces, in the x and y directions, acting on the union of the surfaces of the square and circular shapes of Figure 9, for two different mesh resolutions. The base grid is associated with the solution contours in Figure 10 and we also considered one level of refinement, in which we halved the mesh size. In the case of the SB method, the forces have been computed integrating the stresses on the true surface rather than using the surrogate surface. This postprocessing stage is not expensive *per se*, since the location of the true surface can be obtained by means of the mapping  $M$  and the distance vector  $d$ . We compare the results obtained with the SB method and with a conformal Nitsche method on grids of similar resolution. For a given resolution, the computed force on the embedded and conformal simulations are very close to each other. In fact, the change in force values as the mesh resolution changes, going from coarse to fine, is significantly larger than the discrepancy between the forces obtained from the conformal and embedded methods at the same resolution. This indicates that the numerical error in the force computation with the SB method is negligible compared to the overall numerical discretization error.

## 8. Summary

We have proposed a new embedded boundary method that eliminates the small cut-cell problem by recasting the partial differential equation problem on a surrogate boundary of edges/faces that are the nearest neighbors of the true boundary. We have provided analysis for the method in the context of the Poisson problem and demonstrated its performance on a series of convergence and robustness tests for the Poisson and Stokes problems.

## Appendix A. Some general results in the analysis of non-conformal finite elements

We start by recalling an ubiquitously used inequality in numerical analysis, Young’s inequality: Given two numbers  $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ , we have that  $ab \leq 1/2(a^2 + b^2)$ . From this inequality it is possible to derive the so-called  $\epsilon$ -inequality  $ab \leq 1/2(\epsilon a^2 + b^2/\epsilon)$ , for any  $\epsilon \in \mathbb{R}^+$  (a positive real number). This last inequality can be derived substituting  $\tilde{a} = \sqrt{\epsilon}a$  and  $\tilde{b} = b/\sqrt{\epsilon}$ , and applying Young’s inequality to the product  $\tilde{a}\tilde{b}$ . This last inequality can be then combined with Cauchy-Schwartz inequality to yield  $\langle v, w \rangle \leq \|v\| \|w\| \leq 1/2(\epsilon \|v\|^2 + \|w\|^2/\epsilon)$ , where  $v$  and  $w$  are members of a Hilbert space with norm  $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$  expressed in terms of the inner product  $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ .

We recall now a few definitions and theorems in numerical analysis that are used in what follow. We utilize the notation from [17], where more details can be found. Consider the variational problem

Find  $u \in W$  such that,  $\forall v \in V$ ,

$$a(u, v) = l(v) , \quad (\text{A.1})$$

where  $W$  and  $V$  are infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces with norms  $\|\cdot\|_W$  and  $\|\cdot\|_V$ . Consider now finite dimensional approximating spaces  $W^h$  and  $V^h$  associated with  $W$  and  $V$  respectively, and the discrete version of (A.1),

Find  $u^h \in W^h$  such that,  $\forall v^h \in V^h$ ,

$$a^h(u^h, v^h) = l^h(v^h) . \quad (\text{A.2})$$

We also introduce the following extension  $W(h) = W + W^h$  of the space  $W^h$  to infinite dimensions, equipped with a norm  $\|\cdot\|_{W(h)}$  such that:

- (i)  $\|w^h\|_{W(h)} = \|w^h\|_{W^h}$ ,  $\forall w^h \in W^h$ , and
- (ii)  $\|w^h\|_{W(h)} \leq c \|w^h\|_W$ , that is,  $W$  is continuously embedded in  $W(h)$ .

**Lemma 3** (Strang's second lemma). *Assume that:*

- (i)  $\exists \alpha^h > 0, \inf_{w^h \in W^h} \sup_{v^h \in V^h} \frac{a^h(w^h, v^h)}{\|w^h\|_{W^h} \|v^h\|_{V^h}} \geq \alpha^h$ .
- (ii)  $a^h$  is bounded (continuous) on  $W(h) \times V^h$ .

Then the following error estimate holds:

$$\|u - u^h\|_{W(h)} \leq \left( 1 + \frac{\|a^h\|_{W(h) \times V^h}}{\alpha^h} \right) \inf_{w^h \in W^h} \|u - w^h\|_{W(h)} + \frac{1}{\alpha^h} \sup_{v^h \in V^h} \frac{|l^h(v^h) - a^h(u, v^h)|}{\|v^h\|_{V^h}} . \quad (\text{A.3})$$

**Remark 9.** If  $a^h$  is coercive, then condition (i) in Strang's second lemma is automatically satisfied.
