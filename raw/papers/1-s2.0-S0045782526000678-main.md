## ABSTRACT

We propose and mathematically analyze a new Shifted Boundary Method for the treatment of Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions, with provable optimal accuracy in the  $L^2$ - and  $H^1$ -norms of the error. The proposed method is built on three stages. First, the distance map between the SBM surrogate boundary and the true boundary is used to construct an approximation to the geometry of the gap between the two. Then, the representations of the numerical solution and test functions are extended from the surrogate domain to the such gap. Finally, approximate quadrature formulas and specific shift operators are applied to integrate a variational formulation that also involves the fields extended in the gap. An extensive set of two- and three-dimensional tests demonstrates the theoretical findings and the overall optimal performance of the proposed method.

## 1. Introduction

This article explores a reimagined approach for the treatment of Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions with the Shifted Boundary Method. Although this approach applies to both, the major impetus for this topic stemmed from the pursuit of optimal convergence rates for Neumann conditions in a primal formulation. What follows is a general overview of motivations, historical background, and key ideas that will be explored in depth throughout the present article.

Proper enforcement of Neumann boundary conditions is critical across a wide range of engineering and applied science applications, particularly in computational solid mechanics and heat transfer, where traction and flux boundary conditions are ubiquitous. The analyses performed in these areas rely on computational representations of increasingly complex geometry to the extent that traditional conforming Finite Element Methods may no longer be sufficiently practical. These limitations are further exacerbated in situations where meshing must be performed iteratively, as in geometrically demanding scenarios like shape optimization, digital twins, and additive manufacturing.

Addressing the demand for less labor-intensive design algorithms, recent advancements have been made in immersed (or embedded, or unfitted) computational methods. In the last two decades, much attention has been given on reducing the design cost for problems involving complex geometrical features, described in standard formats (i.e., CAD) and non-standard formats (i.e., STL, level

sets, etc.). Indeed, immersed/embedded/unfitted methods have shown the potential to drastically reduce the pre-processing time involved in the acquisition of the geometry and the generation of the computational grid. An (incomplete) list of these developments in the context of finite element methods include the Immersed Boundary Finite Element Method (IB-FEM) [1,2], the cutFEM [3–22], the Finite Cell Method [5,6,23,24], Immerso-Geometric Analysis [19], B-spline immersed methods [7,8] and similar earlier methods. Many of these approaches require the geometric construction of the partial elements cut by the embedded boundary (cut-cells) to form the solution space, and typically employ Nitsche's method for consistent weak boundary enforcement.

CutFEM relies on data structures that are considerably more tedious to implement with respect to corresponding fitted finite element methods. Furthermore, integrating the variational forms on the characteristically irregular cut cells may also be difficult and advanced quadrature formulas might need to be employed [23,24]. Additionally, small-cut cells can induce poor matrix conditioning and even numerical instabilities, which need to be addressed with appropriate stabilization operators [25,26] or element aggregation techniques [3].

The Shifted Boundary Method was proposed as an alternative unfitted method that remedies the so called "small cut cell problem" by removing cut cells entirely. Instead, the location where boundary conditions are applied is *shifted* from the true to an approximate (surrogate) boundary composed of facets belonging to fully intact cells (the surrogate domain). This shift can be thought of as a modification/correction of the boundary conditions by way of Taylor expansions in the direction of the closest distance between corresponding points on the true and surrogate boundaries. These shifted conditions are enforced weakly, using Nitsche's method, leading to a relatively simple, robust, accurate, and efficient algorithm. Indeed, the computational infrastructure based on distances instead of cut cells can be less algorithmically burdensome in regards to implementation.

The Shifted Boundary Method (SBM) was introduced in [27] and belongs to the more specific class of approximate domain methods [28–37], along with  $\phi$ -FEM [38–43] albeit with some key differences. The work in Main et al. [27] demonstrated the viability of the SBM for Poisson and Stokes flow problems. Soon after, the method was generalized in [44] to the advection-diffusion and Navier-Stokes equations, and later to hyperbolic conservation laws in [45]. An analysis of the stability and accuracy of the SBM for the Poisson, advection-diffusion, and Stokes operators was also included in [27,44,46], respectively. A high-order version of the SBM was proposed in [47], applications to solid and fracture mechanics problems were presented in [48–52] and simulations of static and moving interfaces were developed in [53,54]. Most recently, the SBM was extended to contact problems in solid mechanics in Li et al. [55].

Until now, Dirichlet and Neumann conditions were handled differently due to the certain challenges posed by the limitations of the Taylor expansions. That is to say, the solution and its gradient are available within piecewise-linear interpolation spaces (P1), however, the higher-order terms in the Taylor expansion are not. This means that Neumann boundary conditions, if shifted naively, will result in a loss of one order of convergence in  $L^2$ . Earlier work in Atallah et al. [49,56] addressed this challenge via a mixed shifted formulation strategy (e.g. solving for strains and displacements in solid mechanics). Although mixed formulations increase computational cost, it was shown that it is only necessary to solve the mixed variation along the strip of elements adjacent to the Neumann boundary while enforcing continuity of the normal stress component between the primal and mixed domains. This approach successfully recovered second order accuracy for Neumann Boundary conditions with only a meager increase in computational burden.

Naturally, the potential for a SBM that accurately enforces Neumann boundary conditions without a mixed formulation remained an open research question. This current work demonstrates the viability of a newly conceptualized SBM that optimally enforces both Neumann and Dirichlet boundary conditions, which requires no mixed formulation strategy, and retains the classic Taylor expansion shift operator paradigm.

The key idea in the proposed SBM variant, named here Gap-SBM, is to construct a geometric approximation to the gap between the surrogate and true boundaries, and then devise approximate quadrature formulas to integrate a modified variational formulation, which includes the extension of the solution and test functions from the surrogate domain to the approximate true boundary.

Although the integration of the variational form in the gap between surrogate and true boundaries is a prerogative of cutFEM approaches, the proposed method remains conceptually an SBM, because the construction of the approximation to the gap geometry is done via distance vectors/maps, and the numerical integration is performed on the surrogate boundary using special quadratures that do not involve any cut cells. Because these geometric constructions and integration formulas do not involve cut cells, the method is inherently of SBM type. Specifically, the proposed method needs only the finite element infrastructure that already exists on the original background mesh: no additional degrees of freedom, cut-cells, or ghost penalization are needed.

In the following derivations, analysis, and numerical experiments, the proposed method is shown to be stable and have optimal error convergence rates (in the  $L^2$ - and  $H^1$ -norm of the error). We emphasize that, to the best of our knowledge, it is for the first time in the literature that optimal convergence in the  $L^2$  norm can be proven theoretically for a method acting on unfitted grids without using advanced quadrature formulas on cut cells. Indeed, previous analyses of different SBM and  $\phi$ -FEM variants had to contend with a half-order sub-optimality, which was never observed in practice, but was persistent theoretically. Here, the fully optimal convergence is proven, at least for the symmetric variant of the Gap-SBM.

The rest of this article is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces the SBM notation, Section 3 derives the new proposed SBM for the Poisson problem with Dirichlet and Neumann conditions, Section 4 derives stability results and error estimates in the  $H^1$ - and  $L^2$ -norms, Section 5 extends the proposed method to the equations of compressible isotropic linear elasticity, and Section 6 demonstrates the optimal convergence of the error in a series of numerical tests.

## 2. Preliminaries on the shifted boundary method

This section introduces the notation and general strategy of the Shifted Boundary Method (SBM). In Section 3, we will describe the specific details of the new Gap-SBM pursued in this work.

### 2.1. Surrogate domains and boundaries

Let  $\Omega$  be a connected open set in  $\mathbb{R}^d$  ( $d = 2$  or  $3$ ) with Lipschitz boundary  $\Gamma = \partial\Omega$  and let  $\mathbf{n}$  be the outer-pointing normal to  $\Gamma$ . We consider a closed domain  $\mathcal{D}$  such that  $\text{clos}(\Omega) \subseteq \mathcal{D}$  and we introduce a family  $\mathcal{T}_h$  of admissible tessellations (i.e., grids, or meshes) of  $\mathcal{D}$ . We indicate by  $h_T$  the size of element  $T \in \mathcal{T}_h$  and by  $h$  the piecewise constant function such that  $h|_T = h_T$ . We will consider tessellations constructed on grids that are either Cartesian or the union of simplexes (triangles, tetrahedrons).

Referring to the two-dimensional sketch of Fig. 1, the SBM is based on restricting the tessellation  $\mathcal{T}_h$  (i.e., the triangular grid) to an sub-tessellation  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  (marked in red), in which the discrete variational formulation is solved. The sub-tessellation  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  is the union of only those elements that are *strictly* contained in  $\text{clos}(\Omega)$ , i.e., we form

$$\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h := \{T \in \mathcal{T}_h : T \subset \text{clos}(\Omega)\},$$

which identifies the *surrogate domain*

$$\tilde{\Omega}_h := \text{int} \left( \bigcup_{T \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h} T \right) \subseteq \Omega$$

with surrogate boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h := \partial\tilde{\Omega}_h$  and outward-oriented unit normal vector  $\tilde{\mathbf{n}}$  to  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ . Obviously,  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  is an admissible and shape-regular tessellation of  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$  (see Fig. 1a). In other words, all cut elements are removed from the active computational domain, which is now  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$  instead of  $\Omega$ . We now introduce a mapping

$$\mathbf{M} : \tilde{\Gamma}_h \rightarrow \Gamma, \quad (1a)$$

$$\tilde{x} \mapsto x, \quad (1b)$$

which associates to any point  $\tilde{x} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$  on the surrogate boundary a point  $x = \mathbf{M}(\tilde{x})$  on the physical boundary  $\Gamma$ . Whenever uniquely defined, the closest-point projection of  $\tilde{x}$  upon  $\Gamma$  is a natural choice for  $x$ , as shown e.g. in Fig. 1b. Through  $\mathbf{M}$ , a distance vector function  $\mathbf{d}_M$  can be defined as

$$\mathbf{d}_M(\tilde{x}) = x - \tilde{x} = [\mathbf{M} - \mathbf{I}](\tilde{x}). \quad (2)$$

For the sake of simplicity, we set  $\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{d}_M$  where  $\mathbf{d} = \|\mathbf{d}\|\mathbf{v}$  and  $\mathbf{v}$  is a unit vector.

**Remark 1.** If  $x = \mathbf{M}(\tilde{x})$  does not belong to vertices, corners or edges, then the closest-point projection implies  $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{n}$ , where  $\mathbf{n}$  has been defined as the outward pointing normal to  $\Gamma$ .

**Remark 2.** There are strategies for the definition of the map  $\mathbf{M}$  and distance  $\mathbf{d}$  other than the closest-point projection, such as level sets, for which  $\mathbf{d}$  is defined by means of a distance function. Other more sophisticated choices of  $\mathbf{M}$  may be locally preferable and we refer to [57] for more details.

In case the boundary  $\Gamma$  is partitioned into a Dirichlet boundary  $\Gamma_D$  and a Neumann boundary  $\Gamma_N$  with  $\Gamma = \overline{\Gamma_D \cup \Gamma_N}$  and  $\Gamma_D \cap \Gamma_N = \emptyset$ , we need to identify whether a surrogate side entity  $\tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h$  (i.e., a surrogate element edge in two dimensions or a surrogate element face in three dimensions) is associated with  $\Gamma_D$  or  $\Gamma_N$ . To that end, we partition  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  as  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h} \cup \tilde{\Gamma}_{N,h}$  with  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h} \cap \tilde{\Gamma}_{N,h} = \emptyset$  using again a map  $M$ , such that

$$\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h} = \{ \tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h : M(\tilde{s}) \subseteq \Gamma_D \} \quad (3)$$

$$\tilde{\Gamma}_{N,h} = \{ \tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h : M(\tilde{s}) \subseteq \Gamma_N \}. \quad (4)$$

We will also assume that  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{N,h} = \tilde{\Gamma}_h \setminus \tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}$ , that is, that either a surrogate side entity  $\tilde{s}$  entirely belongs to the surrogate Dirichlet boundary or to the surrogate Neumann boundary. We will then prevent the case of mixed Dirichlet/Neumann surrogate side entities. This hypothesis is realized in practice, by renouncing to the map  $M$  be the closest-point projection near Dirichlet/Neumann interfaces, as discussed in more detail in [57].

### 2.2. General notation for inner products, norms, and seminorms

Throughout this article, we denote by  $L^2(\omega)$ , for  $\omega \subset \Omega$ , the space of Lebesgue square-integrable functions on  $\omega$ . We will use the Sobolev spaces  $H^m(\omega) = W^{m,2}(\omega)$  of index of regularity  $m \geq 0$  and index of summability 2, equipped with the (scaled) norm

$$\|v\|_{H^m(\omega)} = \left( \|v\|_{L^2(\omega)}^2 + \sum_{k=1}^m \|I(\omega)^k D^k v\|_{L^2(\omega)}^2 \right)^{1/2}, \quad (5)$$

where  $D^k$  is the  $k$ th-order spatial derivative operator and  $I(\omega) = \text{meas}_2(A)^{1/2}$  is a characteristic length of the domain  $A$ . Note that  $H^0(\omega) = L^2(\omega)$  and, as usual, we use a simplified notation for norms and semi-norms, i.e., we set  $\|v\|_{m,\Omega} = \|v\|_{H^m(\omega)}$  and  $|v|_{\ell,\omega} = \|D^\ell v\|_{\ell,\Omega} = \|D^\ell v\|_{L^2(\omega)}$ .

We also introduce the definition of the  $L^2$ -inner product over  $\omega$ , namely  $\langle u, v \rangle_\omega = \int_\omega u v$ , and an analogous inner product on the subset  $\gamma \subset \partial\Omega$ , namely  $\langle u, w \rangle_\gamma = \int_\gamma u w$ . We can also restrict to  $\omega$  and  $\gamma$  the norms and seminorms initially defined on  $\Omega$  and  $\Gamma$ , that is  $\|\cdot\|_{\omega,k}$ ,  $|\cdot|_{\omega,k}$  and  $\|\cdot\|_{\gamma,0}$ , for example.

### 2.3. General strategy of the standard shifted boundary method

Consider now the Poisson problem with Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions:

$$-\Delta u = f, \quad \text{in } \Omega, \quad (6a)$$

$$u = u_D, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_D, \quad (6b)$$

$$\nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} = h_N, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_N, \quad (6c)$$

where  $\Gamma = \overline{\Gamma_D \cup \Gamma_N}$  and  $\Gamma_D \cap \Gamma_N = \emptyset$ . Note also that vectors and tensors are marked in bold, while scalars are marked with regular fonts.

As already mentioned, the SBM discretizes the governing equations in  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$  rather than in  $\Omega$ , with the challenge of accurately imposing boundary conditions on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ . To this end, boundary conditions are *shifted* from  $\Gamma$  to  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ , by performing an  $m$ th-order Taylor expansion of the variable of interest at the surrogate boundary, under the assumption that a solution variable  $u$  is sufficiently smooth in the strip between  $\Gamma_h$  and  $\Gamma$ . Let  $D_d^i$  denote the  $i$ th-order directional derivative along  $\mathbf{d}$ :

$$D_d^i u = \sum_{\alpha \in \mathbb{N}^d, |\alpha|=i} \frac{i!}{\alpha!} \frac{\partial^\alpha u}{\partial x^\alpha} d^\alpha.$$

Then, for  $\tilde{x} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$  and  $\mathbf{x} = M(\tilde{x})$  we can write

$$u(\mathbf{x}) = u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} + \mathbf{d}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})) = u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) + \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{D_d^i u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})}{i!} + (R^m(u, \mathbf{d}))(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) \quad (7)$$

where the remainder  $R^m(u, \mathbf{d})$  satisfies  $|R^m(u, \mathbf{d})| = o(\|\mathbf{d}\|^m)$  as  $\|\mathbf{d}\| \rightarrow 0$ . Assume that the Dirichlet condition  $u(\mathbf{x}) = u_D(\mathbf{x})$  needs to be imposed on the true boundary  $\Gamma_D$ . Using the map  $M_h$ , one can extend  $u_D$  from  $\Gamma_D$  to  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  as  $E u_D(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) = u_D(M_h(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}))$ . Then, the Taylor expansion can be used to enforce the Dirichlet condition on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  rather than  $\Gamma_D$ , as

$$S_d^m u - E u_D + R^m(u, \mathbf{d}) = 0, \quad \text{on } \tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}, \quad (8)$$

where we have introduced the boundary *shift* operator for every  $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}$ , namely:

$$S_d^m u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) := u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}) + \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{D_d^i u(\tilde{\mathbf{x}})}{i!}. \quad (9)$$

Neglecting the remainder  $R^m(u, \mathbf{d})$ , we obtain the final expression of the *shifted* approximation of order  $m$  of the boundary condition

$$S_d^m u \approx E u_D, \quad \text{on } \tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}. \quad (10)$$

This shifted boundary condition will be enforced weakly in what follows, and whenever there is no source of confusion, the symbol  $E$  will be removed from the extended quantities, and we would write  $u_D$  in place of  $E u_D$ .

$$S_d u \approx u_D, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_{D,h}, \quad (11)$$

with

$$S_d := S_d^0 u(\bar{x}) = u(\bar{x}) + \nabla u(\bar{x}) \cdot d(\bar{x}). \quad (12)$$

To develop a simple SBM variational formulation, consider the space

$$V_h = \left\{ v_h \in C^0(\bar{\Omega}_h) \mid v_h|_T \in P^1(T), \forall T \in \bar{\mathcal{T}}_h \right\}, \quad (13)$$

where  $C^0(\bar{\Omega}_h)$  is the space of continuous function over  $\bar{\Omega}_h$  and  $P^1(T)$  is the space of linear polynomials over the element  $T \in \bar{\mathcal{T}}_h$ , for grids made of simplexes (triangles/tetrahedrons). In the case of Cartesian grids, instead of  $P^1(T)$  one would use the space  $Q^1(T)$  of functions that are first-order polynomials in each space direction. To fix the ideas and start the discussion, we introduce here the penalty-free SBM formulation with Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions described in [58]:

Find  $u_h \in V_h$  such that,  $\forall w_h \in V_h$

$$(V u_h, \nabla w_h)_{\bar{\Omega}_h} - (V u_h \cdot \bar{n}, w_h)_{\Gamma_h} - (n \cdot \bar{n} (h_N - V u_h \cdot n), w_h)_{\Gamma_{N,h}} + (S_d u_h - u_D, V w_h \cdot \bar{n})_{\Gamma_{D,h}} - (f, w_h)_{\bar{\Omega}_h} = 0. \quad (14)$$

The variational form (14) relies on Nitsche's method to enforce Dirichlet boundary conditions, in which the shift operator  $S_d$  (i.e., a Taylor expansion in our case) is used to extend the discrete solution  $u_h \in V_h$  from  $\Gamma_{D,h}$  to  $\Gamma_D$ . Neumann conditions are imposed weakly, assuming a constant extrapolation of the gradient  $V u_h$ , because, in the case of piecewise-linear approximation, the Hessian of  $u_h$  vanishes (or is incomplete for the space  $Q^1(T)$ ) and it is not possible to construct a Taylor expansion of  $V u_h$ . It was found in numerical computations that this limitation causes the SBM to have suboptimal convergence (i.e., first- instead of second-order convergence) in the  $L^2$ -norm of the error, while the  $H^1$ -seminorm of the error converges optimally (first-order).

There are problems in which this limitation is not particularly negative, such as problems in which the solution has low regularity. For example, Li et al. found that in applications to fracture mechanics [50–52] or contact mechanics [55], a standard SBM implementation does not produce any appreciable difference in  $L^2$ -norm or  $H^1$ -norm of the error with respect to body-fitted finite elements implemented on grids of similar resolution. Fundamentally, because rough problems prevent the optimal convergence of standard finite elements, the differences with respect to SBM approximations become negligible for engineering purposes.

But there are many problems that have sufficient regular solutions, and Atallah et al. [49,56] addressed the sub-optimality of the SBM by imposing Neumann boundary conditions via a mixed shifted formulation (e.g. solving for strains and displacements in solid mechanics). In the next section, we will develop an alternative approach based on a primal formulation, which bypasses the complexities of mixed formulations.

**Remark 3** (generality of our approach). Penalty free formulations of Nitsche's method were introduced by Burman [59] and Boiveau and Burman [60,61] in the context of body-fitted finite element approximations and cutFEM approximations, respectively. The penalty-free formulation [58], which is the basis of the work described in this article, is not the only option for an implementation of an SBM formulation or the new Gap-SBM formulation described in detail in Section 3, and similar derivations apply to the SBM inspired by the symmetric Nitsche's formulation [27,44,46,62]. We will describe this option in Section 3.1.

## 3. Gap-SBM: A new conceptualization of the shifted boundary method

To cure the loss of optimality in the convergence of the  $L^2$ -norm, we propose to account for the effect on the solution  $u_h$  of the gap  $\Omega \setminus \bar{\Omega}_h$  between  $\Gamma_h$  and  $\Gamma$  (the gray-shaded region in Fig. 1a). Of course, we will avoid explicitly integrating the discrete equations over the gap region but, rather, we will derive approximate quadrature formulas that do not require integration over cut elements.

The key aspects of the proposed approach are more easily understood in two dimensions. We will describe the extension of the method to three dimensions in Section 3.2.

### 3.1. Two-dimensional setting

We define next a convenient discretization of the gap  $\Omega \setminus \bar{\Omega}_h$  in the two-dimensional case. Consider an element  $\bar{T}_e \in \bar{\mathcal{T}}_h$  with an edge  $\bar{e}$  on  $\Gamma_h$  (e.g., the element  $\bar{T}_e$  attached to edge  $\bar{e}_1$  in Fig. 2). Denoting by  $\bar{a}_e$  and  $\bar{b}_e$  the end nodes of the edge  $\bar{e}$ , it is easily seen that  $\bar{x} \in \bar{e}$  can be parametrized as

$$\bar{x} = (1 - \xi) \bar{a}_e + \xi \bar{b}_e, \quad 0 \leq \xi \leq 1.$$

Now  $\bar{a}_e$  and  $\bar{b}_e$  can be projected via  $M$  onto  $\Gamma$  (to  $a^{\text{ext}} = M(\bar{a}_e)$  and  $b^{\text{ext}} = M(\bar{b}_e)$ ). More generally, the mapping  $M$  projects  $\bar{e}$  to the possibly curved segment  $M(\bar{e})$ , the portion of the true boundary  $\Gamma$  that connects  $a^{\text{ext}}$  and  $b^{\text{ext}}$ . Since we will be considering piecewise-linear finite element approximations, it is sufficient to approximate  $M(\bar{e})$  by a straight segment  $e^{\text{ext}}$ , which interpolates  $\Gamma$  between  $a^{\text{ext}}$  and  $b^{\text{ext}}$  (see the segment  $e^{\text{ext}}$  that interpolates  $\Gamma$  between  $a^{\text{ext}}$  and  $b^{\text{ext}}$  in Fig. 2). Namely, each  $x \in e^{\text{ext}}$  satisfies

$$x = (1 - \xi) a^{\text{ext}} + \xi b^{\text{ext}}, \quad 0 \leq \xi \leq 1.$$

$$\begin{aligned} M_h(\bar{x}) &:= (1 - \xi)a^{\text{ext}} + \xi b^{\text{ext}} \\ &= (1 - \xi)M(\bar{a}_e) + \xi M(\bar{b}_e) \\ &= (1 - \xi)(\bar{a}_e + d(\bar{a}_e)) + \xi(\bar{b}_e + d(\bar{b}_e)), \quad 0 \leq \xi \leq 1. \end{aligned} \quad (15)$$

Similarly, we can define an approximate distance as

$$d_{M_h}(\bar{x}) := (1 - \xi)d(\bar{a}_e) + \xi d(\bar{b}_e), \quad 0 \leq \xi \leq 1. \quad (16)$$

which approximates  $d_M$ . The union of the edges  $e^{\text{ext}}$  is then denoted as  $\Gamma_h$ , an interpolation of  $\Gamma$ , which can be further decomposed into a Dirichlet part  $\Gamma_{D,h}$  and Neumann part  $\Gamma_{N,h}$ . The quadrilateral  $\tilde{T}_i^{\text{ext}}$  that connects  $\bar{a}_e$ ,  $a^{\text{ext}}$ ,  $b^{\text{ext}}$ , and  $\bar{b}_e$  is the element extension of the original element  $\tilde{T}_e$  to the gap between  $\Gamma_h$  and  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  (e.g., see the quadrilaterals  $\tilde{T}_1^{\text{ext}}$  or  $\tilde{T}_2^{\text{ext}}$  in Fig. 2). More precisely,

$$\tilde{T}_e^{\text{ext}} := \{x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid x = (1 - \xi)(\bar{a}_e + \zeta d(\bar{a}_e)) + \xi(\bar{b}_e + \zeta d(\bar{b}_e)), 0 \leq \xi, \zeta \leq 1\}. \quad (17)$$

Let us denote by  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h^{\text{ext}}$  the set of quadrilaterals of this type along  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ .

**Remark 4.** Our geometric constructions in the gap between  $\Gamma_h$  and  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  do not involve any cut cells, because the element extension  $\tilde{T}_e^{\text{ext}}$  is a quadrilateral, instead of a triangle. This modification to the element topology is one of the key differences between the Gap-SBM and CutFEMs, for which, instead, the underlying, background computational grid is never modified. Additionally, the SBM framework of shifted function evaluations will be extensively used to integrate the variational Gap-SBM formulation in the gap region.

**Remark 5.** In the geometric constructions presented here, the boundary  $\Omega$  is approximated as polygonal, that is  $\Omega \approx \Omega^h$ , where  $\Omega^h$  is a polygonal domain with its vertices lying on  $\Gamma$ .  $\Omega^h$  introduces a geometric error, which is however quadratic in nature, and this approximation can be made safely in the context of piecewise-linear finite element approximation spaces. One aspect that is worth mentioning is that, strictly speaking, boundary conditions are defined on  $\Gamma$  and not  $\Gamma_h$ . Hence a Neumann boundary flux of the type  $h_N(M_h(\bar{x}))$  does not make mathematical sense. In this case, when imposing boundary conditions, we will use the map  $M$  and write  $h_N(M(\bar{x}))$ . The error introduced by this procedure is again quadratic in nature and does not negatively affect the overall accuracy of piecewise-linear finite element approximations.

**Remark 6.** The same geometric constructions apply in the case of Cartesian grids. We omit them here for the sake of brevity but the proposed method will be demonstrated also in this context with specific numerical tests in Section 6.

We then define the extension  $v_h^{\text{ext}}$  of  $V_h$ , where a function  $v_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$  is obtained as the linear combination of the extensions to the gap  $\Omega \setminus \Omega_h$  of the piecewise-linear basis functions used to represent  $v_h$  in  $\Omega_h$ . More precisely,  $v_h^{\text{ext}} = v_h$  on all elements  $\tilde{T} \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$ , while the extension of  $v_h$  beyond the boundary  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  is constructed as follows. Since  $V_h$  is composed of piecewise linear functions,  $v_h \in V_h$  coincides with a first-order polynomial  $q_1$  on a boundary element  $\tilde{T} \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  such that  $\tilde{T} \cap \tilde{\Gamma}_h \neq \emptyset$  (e.g., on the triangular element  $\tilde{T}_i$  along  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  in Fig. 2) and the extension  $v_h^{\text{ext}}$  of  $v_h$  on the adjacent extended element ( $\tilde{T}_i^{\text{ext}}$  in Fig. 2) is the same polynomial  $q_1$  evaluated therein (and similarly for the extension of  $v_h$  from  $\tilde{T}_j$  to  $\tilde{T}_j^{\text{ext}}$  in the same figure).

Based on the discussion in Remark 5, we will always use interchangeably  $\Omega$  and  $\Omega^h$  in what follows. The previous geometric construction allows us to extend the shape functions defined over  $\tilde{T}_i$  to  $\tilde{T}_i^{\text{ext}}$ . This is in a nutshell the construction of a function  $v_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ .

Observe that the dimension of the function spaces  $V_h^{\text{ext}}$  and  $V_h$  are the same, since no additional degrees of freedom are added in the extension process.

We now want to derive a weak form that implements a SBM discretization of the strong form (6). For a sufficiently regular solution  $u$  of the infinite dimensional problem, the strong form (6) is multiplied by  $u_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ :

$$-(u_h^{\text{ext}}, \Delta u)_\Omega = (u_h^{\text{ext}}, f)_\Omega. \quad (18)$$

Let us introduce the following notation, for the sake of brevity:

$$(\cdot, \cdot)_{\tilde{T}_h} = \sum_{T \in \tilde{T}_h} (\cdot, \cdot)_T, \quad (19a)$$

$$(\cdot, \cdot)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} = \sum_{\tilde{T}^{\text{ext}} \in \tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} (\cdot, \cdot)_{\tilde{T}^{\text{ext}}}, \quad (19b)$$

$$(\cdot, \cdot)_{\partial \tilde{T}_h} = \sum_{T \in \tilde{T}_h} (\cdot, \cdot)_{\partial T}, \quad (19c)$$

$$(\cdot, \cdot)_{\partial \tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} = \sum_{\tilde{T}^{\text{ext}} \in \tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} (\cdot, \cdot)_{\partial \tilde{T}^{\text{ext}}}. \quad (19d)$$

Then integrating by parts Eq. (18) over all the triangles in  $\tilde{T}_h$  and the quadrilaterals in  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$ , we have

$$(\nabla w_h, \nabla u)_h - (w_h, \nabla u \cdot n_T)_{\partial \tilde{T}_h} + (\nabla w_h^{\text{ext}}, \nabla u)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} - (w_h^{\text{ext}}, \nabla u \cdot n_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}})_{\partial \tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} = (w_h, f)_{\tilde{T}_h} + (w_h^{\text{ext}}, f)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}}, \quad (20)$$

where we have used the fact that for a given  $w_h \in V_h$ , the extension  $w_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$  coincides with  $w_h$  over  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$ .

**Remark 7.** While  $w_h \in V_h$  is continuous over  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$ , the extension  $w_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$  can be discontinuous over the gap  $\Omega \setminus \tilde{\Omega}_h$ . Looking at Fig. 2, and in particular to the edge  $e_h^{\text{ext}}$  emanating from  $b_{\tilde{e}_1}$  along the distance vector  $d(b_{\tilde{e}_1})$ , it is clear that the extended shape functions from the elements  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_1}$  and  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_2}$  may not match on  $e_h^{\text{ext}}$ . This happens because the gradient over  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_1}^{\text{ext}}$  is the same as the one over  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_1}$  and the gradient over  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}$  is the same as the one over  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_2}$ , but the gradients over  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_1}$  and  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}_2}$  in general do not match.

Because of the presence of potential discontinuities, the framework of Discontinuous Galerkin Methods seems the most appropriate to proceed. The contribution from the internal element boundaries in Eq. (20) can be expanded by making use of the following definitions and identities of the jumps and averages of edge quantities:

$$\llbracket w \rrbracket = w^+ n^+ + w^- n^-, \quad (21)$$

$$\llbracket v \rrbracket = v^+ \cdot n^+ + v^- \cdot n^-, \quad (22)$$

$$\|w\| = \frac{1}{2}(w^+ + w^-), \quad (23)$$

$$\|w w\| = \|v\| \|w\| + \|v\| \cdot \|w\|. \quad (24)$$

In what follows, to simplify the notation, we will write  $w_h$  in place of  $w_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ , since the regions of integration uniquely define whether we are considering a test/trial function on  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$ , or its extension over  $\Omega \setminus \tilde{\Omega}_h$ . Hence, denoting  $\tilde{\mathcal{E}}^\circ$  the set of interior faces in  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$  and  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}$  the set of faces of elements in  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$  that do not lie on  $\Gamma$  nor  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ , we have

$$\begin{aligned} (\nabla w_h, \nabla u \cdot n)_{\partial \tilde{T}_h} + (\nabla w_h, \nabla u \cdot n)_{\partial \tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} &= \langle 1, \llbracket \nabla w_h \nabla u \rrbracket \rangle_{\tilde{\mathcal{E}}^\circ \cup \tilde{\Gamma}_h \cup \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} \\ &\quad + \langle w_h, \underbrace{\nabla u \cdot n}_{=h_N} \rangle_{\Gamma_{N,h}} + (\nabla w_h, \nabla u \cdot n)_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \\ &= \langle 1, \llbracket w_h \rrbracket \llbracket \nabla u \rrbracket + \llbracket w_h \rrbracket \cdot \llbracket \nabla u \rrbracket \rangle_{\tilde{\mathcal{E}}^\circ \cup \tilde{\Gamma}_h \cup \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} \\ &\quad + \langle w_h, h_N \rangle_{\Gamma_{N,h}} + \langle w_h, \nabla u \cdot n \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \\ &= (\llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla u \rrbracket \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} + \langle w_h, h_N \rangle_{\Gamma_{N,h}} \\ &\quad + \langle w_h, \nabla u \cdot n \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}}, \end{aligned} \quad (25)$$

where we have used the fact that  $\llbracket \nabla u \rrbracket = 0$  on edges in  $\tilde{\mathcal{E}}^\circ \cup \tilde{\Gamma}_h \cup \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}$  and  $\llbracket w_h \rrbracket = 0$  on edges in  $\tilde{\mathcal{E}}^\circ \cup \tilde{\Gamma}_h$ . Observing that  $(\nabla w_h, \nabla u)_{\tilde{T}_h} = (\nabla w_h, \nabla u)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h}$  and  $(\nabla w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} = (\nabla w_h, f)_{\tilde{T}_h}$ , we have

$$(\nabla w_h, \nabla u)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} + (\nabla w_h, \nabla u)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} - \langle \llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla u \rrbracket \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} - \langle w_h, h_N \rangle_{\Gamma_{N,h}} - \langle w_h, \nabla u \cdot n \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} = (w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} + (w_h, f)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}}. \quad (26)$$

Let us now replace the infinite dimensional exact solution  $u$  with its approximation  $u_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$  and simplify the notation with  $u_h$  in place of  $u_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ , as we did before for  $w_h^{\text{ext}}$ . Complementing the previous equation with what we weakly enforce the Dirichlet condition and the continuity of the solution across edges in  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}$ , we finally obtain the discrete weak formulation:

$$\begin{aligned} (\nabla w_h, \nabla u_h)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} + (\nabla w_h, \nabla u_h)_{\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} - \langle w_h, h_N \rangle_{\Gamma_{N,h}} \\ - \langle \llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla u_h \rrbracket \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} - \langle w_h, \nabla u_h \cdot n \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} - \theta \langle \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket u_h \rrbracket \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\circ}} - \theta \langle \nabla w_h \cdot n, u_h - u_D \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \end{aligned}$$

$$+ \langle \gamma h^{-1} \llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket u_h \rrbracket \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}} \circ} + \langle \gamma h^{-1} w_h, u_h - u_D \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} = (w_h, f)_{\mathcal{D}_h} + (w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}}, \quad (27)$$

where **Remark 7** is in order also for  $u_h$ . The terms

$$- \theta (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket u_h \rrbracket)_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}} \circ} - \theta (\nabla w_h \cdot \mathbf{n}, u_h - u_D)_{\Gamma_{D,h}} + \langle \gamma h^{-1} \llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket u_h \rrbracket \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} + \langle \gamma h^{-1} w_h, u_h \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}}$$

are typical of an interior penalty discontinuous Galerkin discretization. In particular, for  $\theta = 1$  and  $\gamma > 0$  we obtain a symmetric interior penalty Galerkin discretization, while for  $\theta = -1$  and  $\gamma = 0$  we obtain the skew-symmetric (or non-symmetric) interior penalty Galerkin discretization, which has the advantage of being penalty-free. An SBM version of the latter had been recently explored in [58].

Now, rather than computing integrals on the gap between  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  and  $\Gamma_h$ , or on  $\Gamma_{D,h}$  and  $\Gamma_{N,h}$ , we propose an approximation to these integrals using information on the  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  and appropriate rescaling of integrals. Let us start approximating the term:

$$(w_h, \nabla u_h)_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_h} \langle w_h, \nabla u_h \frac{|\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}|}{|\tilde{e}|} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}, \quad (28)$$

where,  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}$  is the quadrilateral emanating from edge  $\tilde{e}$  (see Fig. 3). In practice, we have reduced the integrals over extended elements  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}$  to integrals over edges  $\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$ , by introducing the rescaling factor  $|\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}|/|\tilde{e}|$ , which is the ratio between the measure (area) of  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}$  and the measure (length) of the edge  $\tilde{e}$ . See Fig. 3 for a sketch of the geometric construction. This simplified approach is reminiscent of using a biased left-node quadrature over an interval in one space dimension, instead of the common mid-point or Gauss quadratures. Similarly, defining

$$H_{\tilde{e}} := \frac{|\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}|}{|\tilde{e}|}, \quad (29)$$

we obtain

$$(w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_h} \langle w_h, f H_{\tilde{e}} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}. \quad (30)$$

Consider now the term

$$(w_h, h_N)_{\Gamma_{N,h}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_{N,h}} \langle S_d(w_h), h_N(M_h(\tilde{x})) j_{\tilde{e}} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}, \quad (31)$$

where we introduced the Jacobian of the map (15):

$$j_{\tilde{e}} := \frac{|dM_h(\tilde{x})|}{|d\tilde{x}|} = \frac{|e^{\text{ext}}|}{|\tilde{e}|}. \quad (32)$$

Note also that the map  $M_h$  is used to evaluate  $h_N$  on  $\mathbf{x} = M_h(\tilde{x})$ , and that the shift  $S_d(w_h)$  is used to evaluate  $w_h$  on  $e^{\text{ext}}$ . Hence, we use the reference edge  $\tilde{e}$  to perform the integration over  $e^{\text{ext}}$ , introducing an approximate formula for the change of variables under integration.

**Remark 8.** In the context of triangular finite element grids and a field  $h_N$  exactly integrable (e.g., a constant), (31) would actually be an exact formula. In the case of Cartesian grids, however, this would not be the case. It is for this reason that we prefer to keep the approximate sign, even if the discussion here refers to triangular grids.

Similarly:

$$\langle w_h, \nabla u_h \cdot \mathbf{n} \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_{D,h}} \langle S_d(w_h), (\nabla u_h \cdot \mathbf{n}) j_{\tilde{e}} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}, \quad (33)$$

$$\langle \nabla w_h \cdot \mathbf{n}, u - u_D \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_{D,h}} \langle \nabla w_h \cdot \mathbf{n}, (S_d(u_h) - u_D) j_{\tilde{e}} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}, \quad (34)$$

$$\langle \gamma h^{-1} w_h, u_h - u_D \rangle_{\Gamma_{D,h}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\mathcal{F}}_{D,h}} \langle \gamma h^{-1} S_d(w_h), (S_d(u_h) - u_D) j_{\tilde{e}} \rangle_{\tilde{e}}. \quad (35)$$

Note that in the approximations (33)–(34) we introduce a slight abuse of notation by writing the normal  $\mathbf{n}$  inside the integrals over  $\tilde{e}$ . In fact, at any point  $\tilde{x} \in \tilde{e}$ , the normal vector  $\mathbf{n}$  to the actual boundary  $\Gamma_h$  should be evaluated as  $\mathbf{n}(M_h(\tilde{x}))$ , and the same applies to  $u_D$  in (34)–(35).

**Remark 9.** Because the Taylor expansion of a polynomial function coincides with it, we have  $S_d(w_h) = w_h^{\text{ext}} \circ M_h$  and one can simply evaluate the extension  $w_h^{\text{ext}}$  of  $w_h$  at the point  $\mathbf{x} = M_h(\tilde{x})$ . In the context of the piecewise-linear approximation spaces considered here, this strategy is less straightforward to implement than the simple Taylor expansion, for a canonical finite element data structure. However, if higher-order approximations are pursued, this alternative could be advantageous since it does not require the evaluation of the possibly many derivatives of  $w_h$ . This idea was originally explored in [63].

The last three remaining terms to be approximated are:

$$(\llbracket w_h \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla u_h \rrbracket)_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}} \circ} \approx \sum_{\tilde{a} \in \mathcal{N}(\tilde{\Gamma}_h)} |d_{\tilde{a}}| \|S_{1/2} w_h\|_{\tilde{a}} \cdot \|\nabla u_h\|_{\tilde{a}}$$

In particular, the average  $\llbracket \cdot \rrbracket_{\tilde{a}}$  and jump  $\llbracket \cdot \rrbracket_{\tilde{a}}$  across the node  $\tilde{a} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$  are computed by evaluating the fields on the two edges of  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  emanating from  $\tilde{a}$ , with formulas analogous to (21) and (22), assuming that the unit vectors  $n^+, n^-$  are normal to the edge in  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}, \sigma}$  stemming from  $\tilde{a}$ , i.e. to the vector  $d_{\tilde{a}}$ . The magnitude  $|d_a|$  represents the length of each edge. Note that the formulas (36)–(37) are actually exact in the case of triangular meshes and linear finite elements since the gradients of involved functions are then constant on each cell. Approximations (36)–(38) can also be applied to the case of Cartesian grids with bi-linear elements, and should include the higher-order terms in the Taylor expansion in the case of higher-order finite element spaces.

In summary, the weak form 27 is approximated as

$$\begin{aligned} (\nabla w_h, \nabla w_h)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} &+ \sum_{\tilde{\epsilon} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \langle \nabla w_h \cdot \nabla u_h H_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \rangle_{\tilde{\epsilon}} - \sum_{\tilde{\epsilon} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \langle \mathbb{S}_d(w_h), h_N(\mathbb{M}_h(\tilde{x})) \tilde{j}_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \rangle_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \\ &- \sum_{a \in \mathcal{N}(\tilde{\Gamma}_h)} \frac{|d_a|}{2} \left( \|\nabla w_h \cdot d_a\|_{\tilde{a}} \cdot \|\nabla w_h\|_{\tilde{a}} + \theta \|\nabla w_h\|_{\tilde{a}} \cdot \|\nabla u_h \cdot d_a\|_{\tilde{a}} \right) \\ &- \sum_{\tilde{\epsilon} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \left( (\mathbb{S}_d(w_h), (\nabla u_h \cdot n) \tilde{j}_{\tilde{\epsilon}})_{\tilde{\epsilon}} + \theta \langle \nabla w_h \cdot n, (\mathbb{S}_d(u_h) - u_D) \tilde{j}_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \rangle_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \right) \\ &+ \sum_{a \in \mathcal{N}(\tilde{\Gamma}_h)} \frac{\gamma}{4h} |d_a| \|\nabla w_h \cdot d_a\|_{\tilde{a}} \cdot \|\nabla u_h \cdot d_a\|_{\tilde{a}} + \sum_{\tilde{\epsilon} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \frac{\gamma}{h} (\mathbb{S}_d(w_h), (\mathbb{S}_d(u_h) - u_D) \tilde{j}_{\tilde{\epsilon}})_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \\ &= (w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Omega}_h} + \sum_{\tilde{\epsilon} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \langle w_h, f H_{\tilde{\epsilon}} \rangle_{\tilde{\epsilon}}. \end{aligned} \quad (39)$$

**Remark 10.** Observe that the shape functions utilized by the proposed method are a partition of unity. Furthermore, the proposed formulation is exact if the solution is affine, because the gradient is globally constant and the Taylor expansion is exact in this case.

### 3.2. Generalization to the three-dimensional setting

The three-dimensional case is technically more complicated, but it is not conceptually different. We will consider the case of tetrahedral grids, but similar derivation can be applied for Cartesian grids. Looking at the sketch in Fig. 4, the element  $\tilde{T}_s \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$  is attached to the surrogate side entity (or face)  $\tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h$ . The nodes of the face  $\tilde{s}$  are indicated in the picture as  $\tilde{a}_{ij}$ , and a point  $\tilde{x}$  lying on  $\tilde{s}$  has the representation

$$\tilde{x} = \tilde{a}_{s;1} + \xi(\tilde{a}_{s;2} - \tilde{a}_{s;1}) + \eta(\tilde{a}_{s;3} - \tilde{a}_{s;1}), \quad (40)$$

where  $(\xi, \eta) \in \tilde{s}$  with  $\tilde{s}$  the reference triangle with vertices  $(0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1)$ . To avoid lengthy expressions, let us introduce the simplified notation  $\tilde{a}_i = \tilde{a}_{s;i}$  and  $d_i = d_M(\tilde{a}_i)$ . Then, the three-dimensional analog of the discrete distance  $d_{M_h}$  defined in (16) is

$$d_{M_h}(\tilde{x}) = d_1 + \xi(d_2 - d_1) + \eta(d_3 - d_1), \quad (41)$$

and the associated discrete map  $M_h$  takes the form

$$\begin{aligned} x &= M_h(\tilde{x}) := \tilde{x} + d_{M_h}(\tilde{x}) \\ &= \tilde{a}_1 + \xi(\tilde{a}_2 - \tilde{a}_1) + \eta(\tilde{a}_3 - \tilde{a}_1) + d_1 + \xi(d_2 - d_1) + \eta(d_3 - d_1), \end{aligned} \quad (42)$$

which identifies the extension face entity  $s^{\text{ext}}$  and can be generalized to define the three-dimensional extension element

$$\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}} := \left\{ x \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid x = \tilde{x} + \zeta d_{M_h}(\tilde{x}), \text{ with } \tilde{x} \in \tilde{s} \text{ and } \zeta \in (0, 1) \right\}, \quad (43)$$

as the extrusion of  $\tilde{s}$  ( $\zeta = 0$ ) into  $s^{\text{ext}}$  ( $\zeta = 1$ ).

As in the two-dimensional case, rather than computing integrals on the gap between  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  and  $\Gamma_h$ , or on  $\Gamma_{D,h}$  and  $\Gamma_{N,h}$ , we propose an approximation to these integrals using information on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$  and appropriate rescaling of integrals. We consider again the approximation of the terms

$$(\nabla w_h, \nabla u_h)_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \langle \nabla w_h, \nabla u_h H_3 \rangle_{\tilde{s}}, \quad (44)$$

$$(w_h, f)_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}} \approx \sum_{\tilde{s} \subset \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \langle w_h, f H_3 \rangle_{\tilde{s}} \quad (45)$$

where

$$H_3 := \frac{|\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}|}{|\tilde{s}|} \quad (46)$$

and, as in the two-dimensional case, the gradients  $\nabla w_h$  and  $\nabla u_h$  are constant over the element extensions  $\tilde{T}_s \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h^{\text{ext}}$ . The area  $|\tilde{s}|$  of the face  $\tilde{s}$  can easily be computed as  $|\tilde{s}| = 1/2 |(\tilde{a}_2 - \tilde{a}_1) \wedge (\tilde{a}_3 - \tilde{a}_1)|$ . The volume  $|\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}|$  of the element extension  $|\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}|$  can be computed in several ways, which will also be compared in the numerical experiments of Section 6.2:

This is a well known decomposition of a triangular prism, and the volume of the three sub-tetrahedrons would match exactly the volume  $|\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}|$  only if  $\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}$  is such geometric shape. The final three terms that need to be discussed are the two-dimensional versions of (36)–(38). We will only consider the first of these three terms, namely  $\langle \|\omega_h\|_h, \|\nabla u_h\|_h \rangle_{\partial\Omega_h}$ , since the other two can be treated very similarly. Taking as a reference the sketch on the right of Fig. 6, the extended face  $s_{ab}^{\text{ext}}$  is constructed from the surrogate boundary edge  $\tilde{e}_{ab}$ , connecting nodes  $\tilde{a}$  and  $\tilde{b}$  on the surrogate boundary  $\Gamma_h^+$ . The edge  $\tilde{e}_{ab}$  is shared by two surrogate faces  $\tilde{s}^+$  and  $\tilde{s}^-$ , attached, respectively, to the element extensions  $T_{\tilde{a}}^{\text{ext}}$  and  $T_{\tilde{b}}^{\text{ext}}$ . The “+” sides are chosen so that the vector  $(\tilde{b} - \tilde{a}) \wedge d_{\tilde{a}}$  points from the “-” side to the “+” side, where  $d_{\tilde{a}} = d_M(\tilde{a})$  (and similarly for  $d_{\tilde{b}} = d_M(\tilde{b})$ ). This can be enforced requiring that  $(\tilde{b} - \tilde{a}) \wedge d_{\tilde{a}} \cdot m > 0$ , where  $m > 0$  is the vector pointing from the barycenter of  $T_{\tilde{a}}^{\text{ext}}$  to  $\tilde{a}$ . Referring again to Fig. 6, we parametrize  $s_{ab}^{\text{ext}}$  with the intrinsic coordinates  $\xi, \zeta$  chosen so that a generic point  $x \in s_{ab}^{\text{ext}}$  is written like

$$\mathbf{x} = \tilde{\mathbf{a}} + \xi(\tilde{\mathbf{b}} - \tilde{\mathbf{a}}) + \zeta(\mathbf{d}_{\tilde{a}} + \xi(\mathbf{d}_{\tilde{b}} - \mathbf{d}_{\tilde{a}})).$$

Here  $\xi \in (0, 1)$  so that  $\tilde{a} + \xi(\tilde{b} - \tilde{a})$  runs over the edge  $e$ , and  $\zeta \in (0, 1)$  represents the movement in the direction of the shift. The unit normal to  $s_h^{\text{ext}}$  at a point  $x$  directed outward of  $T_s^{\text{ext}}$  is given by

$$n = n^- = \frac{1}{\left| \frac{\partial x}{\partial \xi} \wedge \frac{\partial x}{\partial \zeta} \right|} \frac{\partial x}{\partial \xi} \wedge \frac{\partial x}{\partial \zeta},$$

where

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \xi} = (\bar{b} - \bar{a}) + \zeta(d_{\bar{b}} - d_{\bar{a}}) \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \zeta} = d_{\bar{a}} + \xi(d_{\bar{b}} - d_{\bar{a}}).$$

The unit normal to  $\partial b^{\text{ext}}$ , exterior to  $\mathcal{T}_s^{\text{ext}}$  is then given by  $n^+ = -n^-$ . Similarly, the infinitesimal area element is  $d s_{\partial b}^{\text{ext}} = \left| \frac{\partial x}{\partial \zeta} \wedge \frac{\partial x}{\partial \xi} \right| d\zeta d\xi$ , and we briefly recall the definitions of the average and jump on  $s_{\partial b}^{\text{ext}}$ :

$$\begin{aligned}\llbracket \nabla u_h \rrbracket &= \frac{1}{2}(\nabla u_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}} + \nabla u_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}}) \\ \llbracket w_h \rrbracket &= w_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}} \mathbf{n}^- + w_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}} \mathbf{n}^+ = (w_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}} - w_h|_{\tilde{T}_s^{\text{ext}}}) \mathbf{n}.\end{aligned}$$

where  $w_h|_{\tilde{s}_h^{\text{ext}}}$  are the natural extensions from  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$  and  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$  respectively. Note that for any  $w_h \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ , we have  $\llbracket w_h \rrbracket = 0$  on  $\tilde{\varepsilon}_{\tilde{a}\tilde{b}}$ , so that at a point  $\mathbf{x} = \tilde{\mathbf{a}} + \xi(\tilde{\mathbf{b}} - \tilde{\mathbf{a}}) + \zeta(\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a))$  we have

$$\llbracket w_h \rrbracket = \zeta(\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) \cdot (\nabla w_h|_{\tilde{s}_h^{\text{ext}}} - \nabla w_h|_{\tilde{s}_h^{\text{ext}}}) \mathbf{n} = (\zeta(\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) \cdot \nabla w_h) \mathbf{n} .$$

All these preliminary definitions allow the exact computation of the jump integral term:

$$\begin{aligned} \int_{\tilde{s}_{\tilde{a}\tilde{b}}^{\text{ext}}} \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot \llbracket w_h \rrbracket \, ds_{\tilde{a}\tilde{b}}^{\text{ext}} &= \int_0^1 \int_0^1 \left( \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \xi} \wedge \frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \zeta} \right) \zeta \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) d\xi d\zeta \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int_0^1 \left( \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\tilde{\mathbf{b}} - \tilde{\mathbf{a}}) \wedge (\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) \right) \zeta \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) d\xi \\ &\quad + \frac{1}{3} \int_0^1 \left( \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a) \wedge \mathbf{d}_a \right) \zeta \llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_a + \xi(\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) d\xi \\ &= \frac{1}{8} (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\tilde{\mathbf{b}} - \tilde{\mathbf{a}}) \wedge (\mathbf{d}_a + \mathbf{d}_b)) (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_a + \mathbf{d}_b)) \\ &\quad + \frac{1}{24} (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\tilde{\mathbf{b}} - \tilde{\mathbf{a}}) \wedge (\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a)) \\ &\quad + \frac{1}{6} (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_b - \mathbf{d}_a) \wedge \mathbf{d}_a) (\llbracket \nabla w_h \rrbracket \cdot (\mathbf{d}_a + \mathbf{d}_b)) \end{aligned} \tag{53}$$

is obtained swapping  $u_h$  and  $w_h$  in the previous formula, and the term

### 3.3. Generalization to high-order discretizations

In this section we briefly describe how to extend to higher-order approximation spaces the proposed Gap-SBM. As already mentioned, this is not the focus of the present work and a forthcoming publication is under development. In the case of finite element approximations, several nodes in the addition to the vertices of the surrogate side entity  $\tilde{s}$  may be present, and a higher-order interpolation  $\Gamma_h$  of the boundary  $\Gamma$  can be constructed using the distance vectors at *all such nodes*. A similar situation will occur in the case of three-dimensional Cartesian grids in which the surrogate boundary faces are squares. In the case of Isogeometric Analysis, one would use instead the distances at the knots of the geometric discretization.

By means of these geometric constructions one can develop, for every element boundary  $\tilde{T}_h \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  along  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ , an extension element  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$ , with a curved/warped side entity  $s^{\text{ext}}$  on  $\Gamma_h$ . The extension  $v_h^{\text{ext}} \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$  of  $v_h \in V_h$  will involve the evaluation of a high-order polynomial. Namely, if  $v_h$  coincides with the  $k$ th-order polynomial  $q_k$  over the boundary element  $\tilde{T}_h$ , then the same polynomial  $q_k$  will be evaluated over  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$ .

In general,  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$  is obtained mapping a parent high-order prismatic element (e.g., a prism with a triangular base in the case of tetrahedral grids or a cube for Cartesian grids). Using the isoparametric map, it is possible then to define volume and surface Jacobians required to integrate the terms in the variational form.

Finally, higher-order quadrature rules will need to be implemented over  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$ ; this can be done first developing high-order quadrature formulas on the reference element associated with  $\tilde{T}_h^{\text{ext}}$ , and then mapping those points in physical space using the previously described geometric constructions and isoparametric map. The two dimensional case is similar, and of course simpler.

## 4. Theoretical analysis

The numerical analysis will be restricted to triangular grids in two dimensions to avoid the complex notation and technicalities of proofs for the general three-dimensional setting. At the end of this section, we will also comment on how the extension of the presented proofs to the three-dimensional case can be achieved. Similarly, with some appropriate adjustment, the analysis could be extended to uniform Cartesian grids, but we prefer to omit this step for the sake of brevity and notational simplicity. The three-dimensional tests on Cartesian grids included in Section 6 demonstrate the stability, robustness and optimality of the proposed method in some of those situations not covered by the present theory.

Throughout this section, we will assume that the triangular meshes are shape-regular and quasi-uniform, in the sense of Ciarlet.

**Remark 11.** The assumption of quasi-uniformity is not essential for the numerical analysis of the proposed methods, but it greatly simplifies the notation in the mathematical proofs.

For the sake of simplicity and without loss of generality, we shall only consider here problem (6) with homogeneous boundary conditions, that is the case of  $u_D = 0$  and  $h_N = 0$ . Problem (6) is well posed for any  $f \in L^2(\Omega)$ , and we assume that the boundary is sufficiently smooth to have elliptic regularity, that is the weak solution  $u$  is assumed in  $H^2(\Omega)$  with  $|u|_{2,\Omega} \leq C\|f\|_{0,\Omega}$ . This choice is motivated by the fact that we would ultimately like to study the  $L^2$ -optimality of the numerical approximation  $u_h$  to  $u$  in the case of Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions, and this can be done only in the case in which  $u$  is at least in  $H^2(\Omega)$ .

As already mentioned in Section 3, and in particular Remark 5, the error caused by replacing the true boundary  $\Gamma$ , represented by the mapping  $M$ , with  $\Gamma_h$ , represented by the approximate mapping  $M_h$ , should be quadratic on smooth domains. Hence, in the present theoretical study, we neglect this fine level of approximation and develop our theory as if  $\Omega^h = \Omega_h$  and  $M_h = M$ . Taking these errors into account would make the theoretical analysis much more tedious: while a necessity in the case of higher-order approximation spaces, it can be avoided for the piecewise-linear interpolation spaces considered here. In the analysis that follows we will consider the following bilinear form,

$$\begin{aligned} a_h(w, v) &= (\nabla w, \nabla v)_{\bar{\Omega}_h} + (\nabla w, \nabla v)_{\bar{\Gamma}_h} \\ &\quad - (\llbracket w \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla v \rrbracket)_{\text{ext}\Omega} - \theta \langle \llbracket v \rrbracket, \llbracket \nabla w \rrbracket \rangle_{\text{ext}\Omega} + \langle \gamma^{-1} \llbracket w \rrbracket, \llbracket v \rrbracket \rangle_{\text{ext}\Omega} \\ &\quad - \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \langle S_d(w), S_d(\nabla w) \cdot n_{\bar{j}_e} \rangle_{\bar{e}} - \theta \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \langle S_d(\nabla w) \cdot n, S_d(v) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}} \\ &\quad + \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \langle \gamma^{-1} S_d(w), S_d(v) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}}, \end{aligned} \tag{54}$$

which is an “intermediate step” between (27) and (39), in the sense that it corresponds to (39) but, to simplify the notation, the terms (36), (37), and (38) are not approximated. Note also that in the present case of piecewise-linear finite element approximations over triangular grids, the representation formulas (33), (34), and (35) are exact. Here, we interpret the shift operator  $S_d$  as

$$S_d(v^h) = v^h(M_h(\bar{x})), \tag{55}$$

an idea we already explored in [58,64]. When applied to a piecewise linear function  $v_h \in V_h^{\text{ext}}$ , this definition of  $S_d$  is consistent with (i.e., identical to) definition (12), which is based on Taylor expansions. Moreover, for  $v_h \in V_h$ ,  $S_d(\nabla v_h) = \nabla v_h$  on any edge  $\bar{c} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}$ , since  $\nabla v_h$  is piecewise constant. Thus the SBM solution  $u_h \in V_h$  of problem (6) with homogeneous boundary conditions satisfies

$$a_h(v_h, w_h) = (v_h, f)_{\Omega_h} + \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_h} \langle v_h, f H_{\bar{e}} \rangle_{\bar{e}}, \quad \forall v_h \in V_h. \tag{56}$$

Interpreting the shift operator  $S_d$  as in (55) allows us to write that the exact solution  $u \in H^2(\Omega)$  to the same problem satisfies

$$a_h(v_h, u) = (v_h, f)_{\Omega}, \quad \forall v_h \in V_h, \tag{57}$$

where we also have neglected errors in the geometric approximation of the boundaries according to (32), so that

$$\begin{aligned} \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \langle S_d(v_h), S_d(\nabla u) \cdot n_{\bar{j}_e} \rangle_{\bar{e}} &= \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \int_{\bar{e}} S_d(v_h) S_d(\nabla u) \cdot n \frac{|dM_h(\bar{x})|}{|d\bar{x}|} \\ &= \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \int_{M_h(\bar{e})} v_h \nabla u \cdot n \\ &= \langle v_h, \nabla u \cdot n \rangle_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}}. \end{aligned} \tag{58}$$

An important assumption is now in order:

**Assumption 1.** The term  $j_e$  is bounded above, that is  $|j_e| \leq C_D$ .

Observe that Assumption 1 is normally satisfied in engineering computations, even the ones with the most challenging geometries. This is because the grids utilized in practical computations produce a surrogate boundary that broadly captures the shape of the true domain, for any grid resolution.

The first results in our analysis are two lemmas on the stability of the bilinear form (56), followed by an error estimate in the natural norm.

**Lemma 1 (stability of the penalty-based formulations).** The bilinear form  $a_h$  is coercive on  $V_h$  for any  $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$  and  $\gamma > 0$  sufficiently large. More precisely, there exist  $\alpha > 0$  and  $\gamma_0 > 0$ , depending only on the regularity of the mesh and on  $\theta$ , such that for all  $\gamma \geq \gamma_0$  and all  $v_h \in V_h$

$$a_h(v_h, v_h) \geq \alpha \|v_h\|_h^2$$

with

$$\|v_h\|_h^2 := |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \frac{1}{h} \|\llbracket v_h \rrbracket\|_{0,\text{ext}\Omega}^2 + \frac{1}{h} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_D^{\text{ext}}}^2 \tag{59}$$

$$\|w\|_{0,\Gamma_D^{\text{ext}}}^2 := \sum_{\bar{e} \subset \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \|w\|_{0,\bar{e}}^2, \tag{60}$$

$$\|w\|_{0,\bar{e}} := \|w\sqrt{j_e}\|_{0,\bar{e}}. \tag{61}$$

###### **Proof.**

We start by stating a trace inverse inequality (which implicitly relies on Assumption 1)

$$\| \nabla v_h \|_{0, \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}, \partial}}^2 + \| \nabla v_h \|_{0, \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}}^2 \leq \frac{C_{\text{inv}}^2}{h} |v_h|_{1, \Omega}^2$$

and we conclude

$$\begin{aligned} a_h(v_h, v_h) &\geq |v_h|_{1, \Omega}^2 - (1 + \theta) \langle \|v_h\|, \| \nabla v_h \| \rangle_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}, \partial}} + \langle \gamma h^{-1} \|v_h\|, \|v_h\| \rangle_{0, \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}, \partial}} \\ &\quad + (1 + \theta) \sum_{e \in \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}} \langle \mathbf{S}_d(v_h), \nabla v_h \cdot \mathbf{n} j_e \rangle_{0, \bar{e}} + \sum_{e \in \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}} \langle \gamma h^{-1} \mathbf{S}_d(v_h), \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{0, \bar{e}} \\ &\geq \left( 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{2} C_{\text{inv}}^2 \right) |v_h|_{1, \Omega}^2 + \left( \frac{\gamma}{h} - \frac{(1 + \theta)^2}{2\epsilon h} \right) \left( \|v_h\|_{0, \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}, \partial}}^2 + \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}}^2 \right). \end{aligned}$$

This gives the announced estimate taking  $\epsilon$  sufficiently small, e.g.  $\epsilon = C_{\text{inv}}^{-2}$ , and  $\gamma_0 = \frac{1 + \theta^2}{2} C_{\text{inv}}^2$ , so that  $\alpha = \frac{1}{2}$ .  $\square$

The above lemma does not cover the antisymmetric penalty-free case, i.e.  $\theta = -1$ ,  $\gamma = 0$ . Indeed, the bilinear form  $a_h$  is coercive with respect to the  $H^1$  seminorm, but not the norm  $\| \cdot \|_h$ . However, we can replace the notion of coercivity by the inf-sup property, as stated in the following lemma. This can be done under additional assumption about the Dirichlet part of the surrogate boundary:

**Assumption 2.** There exist positive constants  $c_D$  and  $C_D$  such that on any edge  $\bar{e} \subset \hat{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  we have  $c_D \leq j_e \leq C_D$  and  $\bar{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{n} \geq c_D$ , where, at any point  $\bar{\mathbf{x}} \in \bar{e}$ ,  $\bar{\mathbf{n}}$  is the normal to  $\bar{e}$  that points outside of  $\hat{\Omega}_h$ , while  $\mathbf{n}$  is the normal to the actual boundary  $\Gamma$  evaluated at  $\mathbf{M}_h(\bar{\mathbf{x}})$ .

Observe that condition  $\bar{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{n} \geq c_D$  in Assumption 2 can be violated, but typically only on isolated edges. We conjecture that a finer analysis could then help to prove the upcoming result even without this assumption.

**Lemma 2 (Stability of the penalty-free formulation).** Assume  $\theta = -1$ ,  $\gamma = 0$ . There exists  $\alpha > 0$ , depending only on the regularity of the mesh, such that

$$\inf_{v_h \in V_h} \sup_{w_h \in V_h} \frac{a_h(w_h, v_h)}{\|w_h\|_h \|v_h\|_h} \geq \alpha.$$

###### **Proof.**

Taking  $w_h = v_h$ , we immediately obtain the coercivity of  $a_h$  with respect to the  $H^1$  seminorm:

$$a_h(v_h, v_h) \geq |v_h|_{1, \Omega}^2, \quad \forall v_h \in V_h.$$

Now, for any  $v_h \in V_h$ , introduce  $g_h$  as the piecewise-linear function on  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  taking the same values as  $v_h$  at the boundary nodes on  $\hat{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  and set to zero on all the other nodes of the mesh. Reexamining the proof of [58, Lemma 1], we observe that, for any boundary edge  $\bar{e} \subset \hat{\Gamma}_{D,h}$ ,

$$\langle \nabla g_h \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \rangle_{\bar{e}} \geq \frac{c_1}{h} \|v_h\|_{0, \bar{e}}^2 - c_2 |v_h|_{1, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e}^2, \quad (62)$$

where we recall that  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e$  denotes the element of  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  attached to  $\bar{e}$ . This bound corresponds to equation (14a) in the proof of [58, Lemma 1], taken in an element-wise version (prior to summing over all the boundary edges). In order to adapt (62) to the needs of the current article, in particular to pass from the normal vector  $\bar{\mathbf{n}}$  to  $\mathbf{n}$ , we need to invoke Assumption 2. Introducing the unit tangent vector  $\bar{\tau}$  on  $\bar{e}$  alongside the normal vector  $\bar{\mathbf{n}}$ , we can derive from (62):

$$\begin{aligned} \langle \nabla g_h \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}} &= \langle \nabla g_h \cdot \bar{\mathbf{n}} \bar{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}} + \langle \nabla g_h \cdot \bar{\tau} \bar{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}} \\ &\geq c_D \frac{c_1}{h} |v_h|_{\sqrt{j_e}}^2 - c_D c_2 C_D |v_h|_{1, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e}^2 - |\langle \nabla v_h \cdot \bar{\tau} \bar{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}}|. \end{aligned} \quad (63)$$

In the last term above we have replaced  $\nabla g_h \cdot \bar{\tau}$  by  $\nabla v_h \cdot \bar{\tau}$  since  $g_h = v_h$  on  $\bar{e}$ . By scaling arguments and Young inequality, we can further bound this term as

$$|\langle \nabla v_h \cdot \bar{\tau} \bar{\tau} \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}}| \leq \frac{c}{\sqrt{h}} |v_h|_{1, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e} \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, j_e} \leq \frac{C}{h} |v_h|_{1, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e}^2 + c_D \frac{c_1}{4h} \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, j_e}^2 \quad (64)$$

with  $h$ -independent constants  $c$  and  $C$ . Similarly,

$$\|v_h\|_{0, j_e}^2 \geq (\| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, j_e} - \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) - v_h \|_{0, j_e})^2 \geq \frac{3}{4} \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, j_e}^2 - C h |v_h|_{1, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_e}^2. \quad (65)$$

Putting (64) and (65) into (63) and summing over  $\bar{e} \subset \hat{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  we arrive at

$$\sum_{e \in \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}} \langle \nabla g_h \cdot \mathbf{n}, \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) j_e \rangle_{\bar{e}} \geq \frac{\tilde{c}_1}{h} \| \mathbf{S}_d(v_h) \|_{0, \mathcal{F}_{D,h}^{\cup j}}^2 - \tilde{c}_2 |v_h|_{1, \hat{\Omega}_h}^2 \quad (66)$$

with  $\tilde{c}_1 = c_D c_1 / 2$  and  $\tilde{c}_2$  regrouping the constants in (63), (64) and (65).

We shall also need to bound some norms of  $g_h$  by those of  $v_h$ . To this end, we start from the following bound, analogous to equation (14b) in the proof of [58, Lemma 1]:

$$|g_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \frac{1}{h} \sum_{e \in \Gamma_{D,h}} \|S_d(g_h)\|_{0,e}^2 \leq \frac{c_3}{h} \sum_{e \in \Gamma_{D,h}} \|v_h\|_{0,e}^2. \quad (67)$$

The original version of this bound is slightly different and does not include the factors  $j_{\bar{e}}$ , nor the shift operator  $S_d$ . The present version is easily proven by scaling, having in mind Assumption 2. Note that

$$\frac{1}{h} \|g_h\|_{0,\text{ext}\omega}^2 \leq c_4 |g_h|_{1,\Omega}^2. \quad (68)$$

Indeed, consider the edge  $e_b^{\text{ext}} \in \mathcal{E}^{\text{ext}\omega}$  in the sketch of Fig. 2, which is adjacent to a surrogate boundary node  $\bar{b} \in \mathcal{N}(\Gamma_h)$  and is shared by the element extensions  $T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}}, \bar{T}_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}} \in \bar{\mathcal{T}}_h^{\text{ext}}$ . Note that  $T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}}$  and  $\bar{T}_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}$  emanate from the surrogate edges  $\bar{e}_1$  and  $\bar{e}_2$ , with  $\bar{e}_1 \cap \bar{e}_2 = \bar{b}$ . Hence, we have

$$|V g_h|_{T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}}} - V g_h|_{T_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}} \leq \frac{c_4}{h} |g_h|_{1,T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}} \cup T_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}}^2$$

with an  $h$ -independent constant  $c_4$ . Since  $\|g_h\|(a) = 0$ , we can estimate

$$\|g_h\|_{e_b^{\text{ext}}}^2 \leq h^2 \|V g_h|_{T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}}} - V g_h|_{T_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}}\|_{e_b^{\text{ext}}}^2 \leq c_4 h |g_h|_{1,T_{\bar{e}_1}^{\text{ext}} \cup T_{\bar{e}_2}^{\text{ext}}}^2.$$

Summing this over all such edges gives (68). Thanks to (68) and (65) taken in the opposite sense (still valid by scaling), we can rewrite (67) as

$$\|g_h\|_h^2 + h \|V g_h\|_{\text{ext}\omega}^2 \leq \frac{\bar{c}_3}{h} \sum_{e \in \Gamma_{D,h}} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,e}^2. \quad (69)$$

Take any  $\lambda > 0$ , and observe, using (66) and (69),

$$\begin{aligned} a_h(v_h + \lambda g_h, v_h) &\geq (1 - \bar{c}_2 \lambda) |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \bar{c}_1 \frac{\lambda}{h} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}}^2 + \lambda (V g_h, V v_h)_\Omega \\ &\quad - \lambda \|g_h\|_h \|V v_h\|_{\text{ext}\omega} + \lambda \|v_h\|_h \|V g_h\|_{\text{ext}\omega} - \lambda \sum_{e \in \Gamma_{D,h}} (S_d(g_h), S_d(V v_h) \cdot n j_{\bar{e}})_e \\ &\geq (1 - \bar{c}_2 \lambda) |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \bar{c}_1 \lambda \frac{1}{h} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}}^2 \\ &\quad - \lambda \sqrt{\frac{\bar{c}_3}{h}} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}} \times \\ &\quad \times \left( |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + h \|V v_h\|_{\text{ext}\omega}^2 + \frac{1}{h} \|v_h\|_h^2 + h \|S_d(v_h) \cdot n\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}}^2 \right)^{1/2}. \end{aligned}$$

All the terms in the parentheses in the last line can be bounded by  $C_f^2 |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2$  with an  $h$ -independent constant  $C_f$  thanks to trace inverse inequalities and, in particular, inequality (68) applied to  $v_h$  instead of  $g_h$ . This gives with the help of Young inequality for any  $\varepsilon > 0$ , followed again by (68),

$$\begin{aligned} a_h(v_h + \lambda g_h, v_h) &\geq \left( 1 - \bar{c}_2 \lambda - \frac{C_f^2 \lambda}{2\varepsilon} \right) |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \left( \bar{c}_1 - \frac{\bar{c}_3 \varepsilon}{2} \right) \lambda \frac{1}{h} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}}^2 \\ &\geq \left( \frac{1}{2} - \bar{c}_2 \lambda - \frac{C_f^2 \lambda}{2\varepsilon} \right) |v_h|_{1,\Omega}^2 + \frac{1}{2c_4 h} \|v_h\|_{0,\text{ext}\omega}^2 + \left( \bar{c}_1 - \frac{\bar{c}_3 \varepsilon}{2} \right) \lambda \frac{1}{h} \|S_d(v_h)\|_{0,\Gamma_{D,h}^{\text{fuj}}}^2 \\ &\geq c_5 \|v_h\|_h^2 \end{aligned}$$

with

$$c_5 = \min \left( \frac{1}{2} - \bar{c}_2 \lambda - \frac{C_f^2 \lambda}{2\varepsilon}, \frac{1}{2c_4}, \left( \bar{c}_1 - \frac{\bar{c}_3 \varepsilon}{2} \right) \lambda \right)$$

assuming that  $\varepsilon$  and  $\lambda$  are chosen small enough so that  $c_5 > 0$ . Having fixed  $\lambda$  as above, we deduce from (69)

$$\|v_h + \lambda g_h\|_h \leq c_6 \|v_h\|_h.$$

This gives the announced inf-sup with  $\alpha = c_5/c_6$  taking  $w_h = v_h + \lambda g_h$ .  $\square$

**Theorem 1 (convergence in the natural norm).** Let the parameters  $\theta, \gamma$  be chosen either as in Lemma 1 or in Lemma 2. Suppose also that  $f \in H^1(\Omega)$  and  $u \in H^2(\Omega)$ . Then the following  $H^1$ -error estimate holds:

$$|u - u_h|_{1,\Omega} \leq \|u - u_h\|_h \leq Ch(|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{1,\Omega}).$$

###### **Proof.**

Subtracting (57) from (56) yields the “modified Galerkin orthogonality” statement

$$a_h(v_h, u - u_h) = \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \left( (v_h, f)_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} - \langle v_h, f \rangle_{\tilde{e}} H_{\tilde{e}} \right), \quad \forall v_h \in V_h. \quad (70)$$

Introducing, on any edge  $\tilde{e}$ , the averages  $\bar{f}$  and  $\bar{v}_h$  of  $f$  and  $v_h$  on that edge, we have that  $\int_{\tilde{e}} f = \bar{f} |\tilde{e}|$  and  $\int_{\tilde{e}} v_h = \bar{v}_h |\tilde{e}|$ , and we continue the above calculations as

$$\begin{aligned} a_h(v_h, u - u_h) &= \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \left( (v_h - \bar{v}_h, f)_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} + (\bar{v}_h, f - \bar{f})_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} + (\bar{v}_h, \bar{f})_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} - (v_h - \bar{v}_h, f)_{\tilde{e}} H_{\tilde{e}} - \bar{v}_h \bar{f} |\tilde{e}| \right) \\ &= \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h} \left( (v_h - \bar{v}_h, f)_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} + (\bar{v}_h, f - \bar{f})_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} - (v_h - \bar{v}_h, f)_{\tilde{e}} H_{\tilde{e}} \right) \\ &\leq Ch |v_h|_{1,\Omega} \|f\|_{0,\Omega} + Ch \|v_h\|_{0,\Omega} |f|_{1,\Omega} + Ch^2 |v_h|_{1,\tilde{\Gamma}_h} \|f\|_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h}. \end{aligned} \quad (71)$$

To derive the last bound, we have used the following inequalities involving any boundary edge  $\tilde{e} \in \tilde{\Gamma}_h$ , shared by the element  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}} \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  and the quadrilateral  $\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}} \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h^{\text{ext}}$  (see Fig. 3 for an illustration):

$$\|v_h - \bar{v}_h\|_{\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}}} \leq Ch |v_h|_{1,\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}}, \quad \|\bar{v}_h\|_{\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}}} \leq C |v_h|_{\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}}, \quad (72)$$

$$\|f - \bar{f}\|_{\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}}} \leq Ch |f|_{1,\tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}} \cup \tilde{T}_{\tilde{e}}^{\text{ext}}}, \quad \|v_h - \bar{v}_h\|_{\tilde{e}} \leq Ch |v_h|_{1,\tilde{e}}. \quad (73)$$

All of these can be easily proven by scaling arguments and Poincaré-type inequalities. We have also used the fact that  $H_{\tilde{e}} \leq Ch$ . Now, adding to these the trace inverse inequality  $|v_h|_{1,\tilde{\Gamma}_h} \leq \frac{C}{\sqrt{h}} |v_h|_{1,\tilde{\Omega}_h}$  and the trace inequality  $\|f\|_{\tilde{\Gamma}_h} \leq \|f\|_{1,\tilde{\Omega}_h}$  yields

$$a_h(v_h, u - u_h) \leq Ch \|v_h\|_{1,\Omega} \|f\|_{1,\Omega} \leq Ch \|v_h\|_{1,h} \|f\|_{1,\Omega}. \quad (74)$$

The passage from the full  $H^1$  norm of  $v_h$  to its triple norm in the last line, is justified by the Poincaré-type inequality (valid since  $\Gamma_D$  is assumed of positive measure): for any  $v \in H^1(\Omega)$

$$\|v\|_{0,\Omega} \leq C_P \left( \|\nabla v\|_{0,\Omega} + |\Gamma_D|^{-1/2} \|v\|_{0,\Gamma_D} \right).$$

From this and the fact that the boundary term on  $\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}$  in the triple norm (59) can be interpreted as the norm on  $\Gamma_D$  when applied to  $v_h \in \tilde{V}_h^{\text{ext}}(\Omega)$ , we deduce that

$$\|v_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq C_P \left( \|\nabla v_h\|_{0,\Omega} + h^{-1/2} \|v_h\|_{0,\tilde{\Gamma}_{D,h}} \right) \leq C_P \|v_h\|_{1,h}. \quad (75)$$

Introducing the interpolant  $I_h u \in V_h$  (constructed by nodal interpolation over the mesh  $\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  inside  $\tilde{\Omega}_h$ ), we derive from (74) using either the coercivity of the form  $a_h$  from Lemma 1, or the inf-sup property from Lemma 2, depending on the choice of parameters  $\theta$ ,  $\gamma$ :

$$a_h \|I_h u - u_h\|_h \leq \sup_{w_h \in V_h} \frac{a_h(w_h, I_h u - u_h)}{\|w_h\|_h} \leq \sup_{w_h \in V_h} \frac{a_h(w_h, u - I_h u)}{\|w_h\|_h} + Ch \|f\|_{1,\Omega}. \quad (76)$$

Using the continuity of the form  $a_h$  in the triple norm (evident by Cauchy-Schwarz and scaling), we arrive at:

$$\|I_h u - u_h\|_h \leq Ch \|f\|_{1,\Omega} + C \|u - I_h u\|_h.$$

By the Bramble–Hilbert lemma [65,66] and scaling arguments, we have

$$\|u - I_h u\|_h \leq C |u - I_h u|_{1,\Omega} \leq Ch |u|_{2,\Omega}.$$

This allows us to obtain the announced error estimate, thanks to the triangle inequality

$$\|u - u_h\|_h \leq \|u - I_h u\|_h + \|I_h u - u_h\|_h \leq Ch \|f\|_{1,\Omega} + C \|u - I_h u\|_h \leq Ch (|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{1,\Omega}). \quad (77)$$

□

We now turn to the  $L^2$  error estimate, and we initially focus on the symmetric interior penalty method (i.e. for  $\theta = 1$  and  $\gamma$  sufficiently large). This variant possesses the adjoint consistency property required for optimal convergence. To treat the errors introduced by the approximations in the gap  $\Omega \setminus \tilde{\Omega}_h$ , we enlarge it to the set  $\Omega_h^{\Gamma,\text{fat}}$ , which contains  $\Omega \setminus \tilde{\Omega}_h$  together with all elements  $\tilde{T} \in \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_h$  adjacent to  $\tilde{\Gamma}_h$ . Note that  $\Omega_h^{\Gamma,\text{fat}}$  is a thin layer of width of order  $h$ , a fact that will be used via the following lemmas:

**Lemma 3.** For any  $u \in H^1(\Omega)$

$$\|u\|_{0,\Omega_h^{\Gamma,\text{fat}}} \leq C \sqrt{h} \|u\|_{1,\Omega}.$$

This lemma is proved in [67, Lemma 4.10]. The idea of the proof is to cover the band  $\Omega_h^{\Gamma,\text{fat}}$  by curves  $S_\eta = \{x \in \Omega : \text{dist}(x, \partial\Omega) = \eta\}$  for  $\eta \in (0, \delta)$  with  $\delta$  of order  $h$ , apply the trace inequality on each  $S_\eta$ , and then integrate on  $\eta$ .

**Lemma 4.** For any  $u_h \in V_h$

$$\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} \leq C\sqrt{h}(\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega} + |u_h|_{2,h,\Omega})$$

with

$$|u_h|_{2,h,\Omega}^2 = \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_h^{\text{internal}}} \frac{1}{h} \|\nabla u_h\|_{0,e}^2.$$

**Proof.** First, observe first that the norm  $|u_h|_{2,h,\Omega}$  would in principle contain also the term  $\sum_{T \in \mathcal{T}_h \cup \mathcal{T}_h^{\text{ext}}} |u_h|_{2,T}^2$ , which is however zero for functions in  $\mathcal{P}^1(\mathcal{T})$ . The idea of the proof is to apply the preceding lemma to  $\nabla u_h$ , but the difficulty is that  $\nabla u_h$  is not in  $H^1(\Omega)$ , because of the discontinuities that arise in the gap  $\Omega \setminus \Omega_h$ . To circumvent this issue, we can construct a continuous counterpart  $U_h$  of  $\nabla u_h$ . For example, we can set  $U_h = I_h \nabla u_h$  where  $I_h$  is a Clément interpolation operator to  $\mathcal{P}^1$  functions. By scaling arguments, it is possible to prove that the Clément interpolation operator satisfies  $\|U_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq C\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega}$  (stability),  $\|\nabla u_h - U_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq Ch|u_h|_{2,h,\Omega}$  (accuracy), and  $\|\nabla U_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq C|u_h|_{2,h,\Omega}$ . We conclude using Lemma 3:

$$\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} \leq \|\nabla u_h - U_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} + \|U_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} \leq \|\nabla u_h - U_h\|_{0,\Omega} + C\sqrt{h}\|U_h\|_{1,\Omega}.$$

With the interpolation estimates from above, this gives

$$\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} \leq C(h + \sqrt{h})(\|\nabla u_h\|_{0,\Omega} + |u_h|_{2,h,\Omega}).$$

□

**Theorem 2 (optimal  $L^2$ -error estimate for the symmetric interior penalty method).** Suppose  $\theta = 1$ ,  $\gamma$  sufficiently large,  $f \in H^2(\Omega)$  and  $u \in H^2(\Omega)$ . The following  $L^2$  error estimate holds then

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq Ch^2(|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{2,\Omega}).$$

**Proof.**

We proceed by the Aubin-Nitsche trick. Let  $w \in H^2(\Omega)$  be the solution to

$$-\Delta w = u - u_h, \quad \text{in } \Omega, \tag{78a}$$

$$w = 0, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_D, \tag{78b}$$

$$\nabla w \cdot n = 0, \quad \text{on } \Gamma_N. \tag{78c}$$

Then

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 = a_h(u - u_h, w) = a_h(w, u - u_h),$$

by the symmetry of  $a_h$  for the symmetric variant of the method with  $\theta = 1$ . Take  $w_h = I_h w \in V_h$  as the nodal interpolant of  $w$ . Then, using the “modified Galerkin orthogonality” (70) we rewrite

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 = a_h(w - w_h, u - u_h) + \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \mathcal{E}_h} \left( (w_h - \tilde{w}_h, f)_\varepsilon^{\text{fext}} - (\tilde{w}_h, f)_\varepsilon H_{\tilde{e}} \right).$$

Proceeding as in the proof of the  $H^1$  error by introducing the averages  $\tilde{f}$  and  $\tilde{w}_h$  of  $f$  and  $w_h$  on every edge  $\tilde{e}$ , we continue the above calculations as

$$\begin{aligned} \|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 &\leq \|w - w_h\|_{1,\Omega} \|u - u_h\|_h + \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \mathcal{E}_h} \left( (w_h - \tilde{w}_h, f)_\varepsilon^{\text{fext}} + (\tilde{w}_h, f - \tilde{f})_\varepsilon^{\text{fext}} \right) \\ &\quad - \sum_{\tilde{e} \in \mathcal{E}_h} ((w_h - \tilde{w}_h, f)_\varepsilon H_{\tilde{e}}) \\ &\leq Ch^2 |w|_{2,\Omega} (|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{1,\Omega}) + Ch (|w_h|_{1,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} \|f\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} + \|w_h\|_{0,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}} |f|_{1,\Omega_h^{f,\text{ext}}}) \\ &\quad + Ch^2 |w|_{1,\Gamma_h} \|f\|_{0,\Gamma_h}. \end{aligned}$$

Here, we have used the already proven error estimate (77), and bounds (72). To gain another  $h$  in the terms multiplied by the first power of  $h$ , we use Lemmas 3 and 4:

$$\begin{aligned} \|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 &\leq Ch^2 |w|_{2,\Omega} (|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{1,\Omega}) + Ch^2 (|w_h|_{1,\Omega} + |w_h|_{2,h,\Omega}) \|f\|_{1,\Omega} + |w_h|_{1,\Omega} \|\nabla f\|_{1,\Omega} \\ &\quad + Ch^2 |w_h|_{1,\Gamma_h} \|f\|_{0,\Gamma_h}. \end{aligned}$$

By interpolation estimates,  $|w_h|_{1,\Gamma_h} + |w_h|_{1,\Omega} + |w_h|_{2,h,\Omega} \leq C\|w\|_{2,\Omega}$ . By the trace inequality,  $\|f\|_{0,\Gamma_h} \leq C\|f\|_{1,\Omega}$ . Thus, the estimate above leads to

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 \leq Ch^2 (|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{2,\Omega}) \|w\|_{2,\Omega}$$

and we conclude by recalling  $\|w\|_{2,\Omega} \leq C\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}$ . □

**Remark 12.** For other choices of parameters, i.e.  $\theta \neq 1$  with  $\gamma$  sufficiently large, or  $\theta = -1, \gamma = 0$ , we can prove the sub-optimal  $L^2$  error estimate

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega} \leq Ch^{3/2}(|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{2,\Omega}) .$$

This result should not be surprising, since it also holds for the unsymmetric Nitsche's method on body-fitted grids. To this end, we proceed again by the Aubin-Nitsche trick, starting by introducing  $w \in H^2(\Omega)$  as in the proof of Theorem 2. This time, however, the bilinear form is not symmetric, and some adjustments need to be made. Then

$$\begin{aligned} \|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 &= a_h(u - u_h, w) \\ &= a_h(w, u - u_h) - (1 - \theta) (\langle u - u_h, \rangle, [\nabla w])_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}} \\ &\quad - (1 - \theta) \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_{D,h}^+} \langle S_d(\nabla w) \cdot n, S_d(u - u_h) \rangle_e \\ &= a_h(w - w_h, u - u_h) + \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_h^+} (w_h, f)_{\bar{r}_h^{\text{ext}}} - (w_h, f)_e H_e + c_\theta . \end{aligned} \quad (79)$$

where

$$c_\theta = -(1 - \theta) (\langle u - u_h, \rangle, [\nabla w])_{\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}} - (1 - \theta) \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_{D,h}^+} \langle S_d(\nabla w) \cdot n, S_d(u - u_h) \rangle_e .$$

Other than  $c_\theta$ , all the terms in the right-hand side of (79), can be treated as in the proof of Theorem 2. For  $c_\theta$ , we proceed as follows

$$c_\theta \leq C\sqrt{h} \|u - u_h\|_h (\|\nabla w \cdot n\|_{0,\bar{r}_h^{\text{ext}}} + \|\nabla w \cdot n\|_{0,\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}}) .$$

To treat the contributions on the edges in  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}$ , we apply the following inverse inequality

$$\|\nabla w \cdot n\|_{0,\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}} \leq C \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{h}} \|\nabla w\|_{0,\Omega_h^{\text{ext}}} + \sqrt{h} |\nabla w|_{1,\Omega_h^{\text{ext}}} \right) \leq C \|\nabla w\|_{1,\Omega} ,$$

derived applying Lemma 3, and thinking about the edges in  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}$  as part of quadrilaterals in  $\bar{r}_h^{\text{ext}}$ . Collecting all these contributions and using the estimate for  $\|u - u_h\|_h$  gives

$$\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}^2 \leq Ch^2(|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{2,\Omega}) \|w\|_{2,\Omega} + Ch^{3/2}(|u|_{2,\Omega} + \|f\|_{1,\Omega}) (\|\nabla w \cdot n\|_{0,\bar{r}_h^{\text{ext}}} + \|\nabla w \cdot n\|_{1,\Omega})$$

and we conclude by recalling  $\|w\|_{2,\Omega} \leq C\|u - u_h\|_{0,\Omega}$ .

**Remark 13** (Extension to three dimensions of the stability and convergence proofs). The proofs developed above for the two-dimensional case of triangular grids extend with minimal changes to three-dimensional tetrahedral grids. Obvious changes include replacing  $H_e$  in (30) with  $H_e$  in (40). Analogous modifications apply to the boundary terms over  $\bar{r}_h$  and  $\mathcal{E}^{\text{ext},\partial}$ , already discussed in Section 3.2. Scaling arguments carry over practically unaltered and many derivations in the two-dimensional proofs above are carried out in a dimension-agnostic way. Also in the three-dimensional case, one relies on the assumption that the difference between  $\Omega$  and  $\Omega_h$  and  $\Gamma$  and  $\Gamma_h$  do not alter the convergence rates, as it would be the case when affine interpolations of the boundary are used in combination with  $P^1$ -elements.

$$-\nabla \cdot (\sigma(u)) = b \quad \text{in } \Omega , \quad (80a)$$

$$u = u_D \quad \text{on } \Gamma_{D,h} , \quad (80b)$$

$$\sigma n = t_N \quad \text{on } \Gamma_{N,h} , \quad (80c)$$

where  $u$  is the displacement field,  $u_D$  its value on the Dirichlet boundary  $\Gamma_{D,h}$ ,  $t_N$  the normal traction along the Neumann boundary  $\Gamma_{N,h}$ , and  $b$  a body force. We of course assume that  $\partial\Omega = \bar{\Gamma}_{D,h} \cup \bar{\Gamma}_{N,h}$  and  $\bar{\Gamma}_{D,h} \cap \bar{\Gamma}_{N,h} = \emptyset$ . ## 6. Numerical results

In this section, we present results from a series of numerical experiments for problems in two and three dimensions, which demonstrate the theoretical findings of Section 4. Our general approach relies on the method of manufactured solutions and involves embedding geometries (both analytic and discrete) on a series of meshes of increased refinement. A schematic representation of the experimental setup is provided in Fig. 7, depicting an embedded circular shape on a unit square domain.

The primary motivator for the proposed method was, by and large, the development of Neumann boundary conditions that guarantee optimal convergence rates. Although a majority of the experiments involve only embedded Neumann boundaries, the array of tests was expanded to also include embedded Dirichlet boundary conditions.

### 6.1. Two-dimensional tests

We present a series of numerical experiments for Poisson and linear elasticity problems. The numerical experiments on the Poisson equation encompass both the symmetric and anti-symmetric Nitsche formulations for the weak enforcement of Dirichlet conditions, along with the inclusion of a “patch test.” Likewise, a simple bending beam test was performed for linear elasticity, which included both embedded homogeneous displacement and embedded homogeneous traction boundary conditions. Furthermore, optimal convergence was also achieved with quadrilateral elements, demonstrating the flexibility of the method beyond standard triangular finite elements.

#### 6.1.1. Patch test for the Poisson problem

A patch test experiment was performed to assess the ability of the method to match an affine exact solution. In general, passing a patch test does not guarantee convergence nor stability of a numerical method. Yet, it is an important sanity check for the proposed conceptualization of the SBM, which involves solution extensions and approximate integration over the gap region. Affine solutions are relevant in engineering applications, since they imply a constant flux state in the case of the Poisson problem or a constant strain or stress state in the case of linear elasticity.

The proposed SBM variant possesses the partition of unity property and passes the patch test, since affine solutions can be exactly represented in the gap region. We considered the Poisson problem with three simple geometries (circle, rotated square, and star) embedded on a unit square domain with a regular background mesh, as seen in Fig. 8. The solutions are  $u = x + y$ ,  $u = x$ , and  $u = y$  for the circle, rotated square, and star respectively. A Dirichlet boundary condition is strongly enforced on the outer perimeter of the unit square, while the shifted Neumann boundary condition is applied to the surrogate boundary in red. A visualization of the surrogate extensions are provided in Fig. 9, along with contours of the nodal error between the approximate and exact solutions  $u_h - u_{exact}$ . It is easily seen that the numerical error is within machine precision.

#### 6.1.2. Poisson problem with manufactured solutions

The aim of these numerical experiments is to analyze convergence properties of the method for a high number of extension arrangements. Rotating the background mesh, while fixing the embedded geometry and manufactured solution, isolates effects imparted on the quality of the solution by the geometry of the element extensions  $T_{\Gamma}^{ext}$ . Both a smooth, analytical shape (circle) and a concave, polygonal shape (star) were immersed into triangular background meshes. #### 6.1.4. Cantilever beam

The cantilever beam test is a classic elastostatics problem consisting of a loaded beam that is clamped on one end subject to a distributed load. This test is a good candidate for assessing the performance of the proposed variant of SBM in the presence of mixed displacement/traction boundary conditions. An analytical solution can be derived using the Euler-Bernoulli beam theory. The beam has length  $L = 20$  and height  $H = 1$  and is subject to a uniformly distributed load  $q = 1e-3$ . A zero displacement Dirichlet boundary condition is applied at  $x = 0$ , with a stress-free Neumann boundary condition applied everywhere else. The material Young's modulus is  $E = 1e5$  and the Poisson's ratio is  $\nu = 0.3$ . The analytical solution for the vertical tip displacement is calculated as

$$u_{y_{max}} = \frac{qL^4}{8ET}. \tag{91}$$

Simulations were performed with both the embedded (non-symmetric Nitsche) SBM and compared with a boundary-fitted standard primal formulation.

## 7. Summary

We proposed a new conceptualization of the SBM framework, which provides optimal error estimates in both the  $H^1$ -norm and  $L^2$ -norm in the presence of Neumann or Dirichlet boundary conditions. This achievement closes a long-standing issue in the literature of SBM methods, without resorting to mixed formulations. The proposed approach is based on approximate integration of the variational formulation in the gap between the surrogate and true boundaries. The method we presented still falls in the class of SBMs, since no cut cells are created: the construction of the integration procedure on the gap relies on changing the element topology and defining extension fields through distances and Taylor expansions. Because, no cut-cell are present, none of the related pathologies are present, such as cumbersome integration rules in physical space, numerical instability or poor matrix conditioning related to the small-cut cell problem. A series of numerical experiments demonstrated the consistency, stability, robustness, and optimal accuracy of the proposed approach, in both two and three dimensions.

The present work explores the application of the method to piece-wise linear finite element approximation spaces, where the benefits of optimal (quadratic) convergence in the  $L^2$ -norm are more easily felt. However, the proposed methodology could be extended to higher-order discretization and immersed Iso-Geometric Analysis. Further developments in the context of nonlinear mechanics, contact mechanics and CFD coupled with thermal flows are also envisioned.
