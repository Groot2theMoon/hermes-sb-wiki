## ABSTRACT

Multiple-porosity models are commonly used to describe the poromechanical behavior of complex porous solids occurring in earth and life sciences. From the characterization standpoint, the poroelastic constants of such materials may be determined from the poroelastic constants of their single-porosity constituents. The uniqueness principle of the poroelastic energy density function is herein used to construct and solve a system of equations between the described sets of poroelastic constants in a multiple-porosity material. The method systematically applies to an arbitrary number of co-existing porosities. The presented energy-based approach resolves the perceived closure problem that was reported for solids with more than two porosity systems.

## 1. Introduction

Biot's theory of poroelasticity (Biot, 1941) postulates a phase-independent, macroscopic representation of deformation in porous, fluid-saturated, and elastic solids. The theory does so by introducing the kinematic variable,  $\zeta$ , that accounts for increments in the fluid mass content per unit representative elementary volume (REV) of the bulk material. The fluid content increment, together with dilatational strain,  $\epsilon$ , correspond to the related dynamic variables, i.e., the pore pressure,  $p$ , and confining stress,  $-p_0$ . Linear formulation of the relationship between the described sets of variables may be expressed in terms of three material constants, e.g., the bulk modulus  $\bar{K}$ , Biot-Willis effective stress coefficient  $\bar{\alpha}$  (Biot & Willis, 1957), and Skempton's pore fluid pressure coefficient  $\bar{B}$  (Skempton, 1954), as follows.

$$\begin{pmatrix} \bar{\epsilon} \\ \bar{\zeta} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\bar{K}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \bar{\alpha} \\ \bar{\alpha} & \bar{s} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p_0 \\ p \end{pmatrix} \quad (1)$$

where  $\bar{s} = \bar{K}\bar{S} = \bar{\alpha}/\bar{B}$  is the dimensionless storage coefficient (Wang, 2000). Eq. (1) presents the compliance form of the poroelastic constitutive relation owing to appearance of the kinematic variables on the left side of the equation, and dynamic variables on the right side.

The double-porosity and double-permeability poroelastic model (Aifantis, 1977, 1980) stems from integration of poroelasticity and dual-continuum theory (Barenblatt, Zheltov, & Kochina, 1960) to address certain types of pore structure heterogeneity at microscale that are commonly observed in earth and life sciences. Examples include fractured rocks (Aguilera, 2014; Moench, 1984), or biological tissues such as bone (Cowin, Gailani, & Benalla, 2009), and brain (Mehrabian, Abousleiman, Mapstone, & El-Amm, 2015). In particular, modeling the storage and transport porosities of naturally fractured rocks is among the early engineering adoptions of the theory (Warren & Root, 1963). Alternative formu-

lations of double-porosity poroelasticity are available (Beskos & Aifantis, 1986; Elsworth & Bai, 1992; Khalili & Selvadurai, 2003; Wilson & Aifantis, 1982), and a number of applied solutions to the related engineering problems have been published (Lewallen & Wang, 1998; Nguyen & Abousleiman, 2010). More recently, the double-porosity poroelastic models are enhanced to account for the coupled processes of species and ionic transport in porous media (Nguyen & Abousleiman, 2009; Liu, Hoang, & Abousleiman, 2018; Liu, Mehrabian, & Abousleiman, 2018).

A generalization of the theory to arbitrary number of coexisting porosities, namely, multiple-porosity and multiple-permeability poroelasticity, along with related analytical solutions, is provided in (Mehrabian & Abousleiman, 2014, 2015, 2018). In particular, generalization of Eq. (1) for multiple-porosity poroelastic media takes the following form:

$$\begin{pmatrix} \varepsilon \\ \zeta_1 \\ \zeta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \zeta_N \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{K} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \alpha_1 & \alpha_2 & \cdots & \alpha_N \\ \alpha_1 & s_{11} & s_{12} & \cdots & s_{1N} \\ \alpha_2 & s_{12} & s_{22} & \cdots & s_{2N} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \alpha_N & s_{1N} & s_{2N} & \cdots & s_{NN} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p_0 \\ p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_N \end{pmatrix} \quad (2)$$

Eq. (2) consists of  $(N+1)(N+2)/2$  material constants including  $N$  Biot-Willis coefficients pertaining to  $N$  porosity systems,  $N(N+1)/2$  dimensionless storage coefficients  $s_{ij}$ , together with the overall bulk modulus of the multiple-porosity material,  $K$ .

The focus of this work is on characterization aspects of multiple-porosity and multiple-permeability poroelastic media. The specific aim is to provide a systematic method for obtaining constants  $\alpha_i$ ,  $s_{ij}$ , and  $K$  in terms of the three poroelastic constants of individual constituents, i.e.,  $\bar{\alpha}_i$ ,  $\bar{s}_i$ , and  $\bar{K}_i$  in a system with an arbitrary number of  $N$  porosities. A phenomenological approach to solving the stated problem for double-porosity materials has been developed and documented through a series of publications (Berryman & Milton, 1991; Berryman & Wang, 1995; Berryman and Pride 2002; Berryman, 2002, 2006, 2011, 2012). The approach involves constructing a system of equations between the poroelastic constants of the bulk double-porosity solid and those of the single-porosity constituents by simultaneous implementation of Eqs. (1) and (2) on stress and pore pressure perturbations associated with a number of deliberately designed, thought experiments, e.g., self-similar expansion (Berryman, 2002). Despite its usefulness and simplicity, the method falls short of providing the necessary number of equations required to solve for all poroelastic constants when three or more co-existing porosities are present in the poroelastic system. For instance, the self-similar expansion experiment provides  $2(N+1)$  equations to be solved for  $(N+1)(N+2)/2$  poroelastic constants (Berryman, 2002). Therefore, the system of equations would become underdetermined for  $N \geq 3$ .

This paper presents a method to resolve the described closure problem. Section 2 introduces the potential energy density function of the multiple-porosity poroelasticity. The uniqueness condition on the obtained formulation of the potential energy density is exploited to extract sufficient number of equations for obtaining the material constants in an  $N$ -porosity material. The cases of double porosity and multiple-porosity poroelasticity are presented in Sections 2.1 and 2.2, respectively. In particular, it is shown that the systematic nature of the proposed method does not require design of ad-hoc thought experiments. Sections 3 and 4 discuss alternative manifestations of Eq. (2), namely, the stiffness and mixed formulations of the multiple-porosity poroelastic constitutive relation.

## 2. Potential energy density

A general equilibrium state of uniform confining load  $p_{0i}$  and pore pressure disturbance  $p_i$  applied to a sample of the  $i^{\text{th}}$  single-porosity constituent of an  $N$ -porosity poroelastic composite is considered. The potential energy density associated with the considered volumetric deformation is written as follows (Biot, 1941; Coussy, 2004):

$$\bar{U}_i = \frac{1}{2} (p_{0i} \bar{\epsilon}_i + p_i \bar{\zeta}_i) \quad (3)$$

Substitution from Eq. (1) yields:

$$\bar{U}_i = \frac{1}{2K_i} (p_0^2 + 2\bar{\alpha}_i p_0 p_i + \bar{s}_i p_i^2) \quad (4)$$

Likewise, a general state of confining load  $p_0$  and pore pressure disturbances  $p_i$  ( $i = 1, \dots, N$ ) applied to an  $N$ -porosity poroelastic sample is considered. The potential energy density associated with linear volumetric deformations of the multiple-porosity material is written as follows (Coussy, 2004):

$$U = \frac{1}{2} \left( p_0 \epsilon + \sum_{i=1}^N p_i \zeta_i \right) \quad (5)$$

Substitution from Eq. (2) yields:

$$U = \frac{1}{2K} \left( p_0^2 + \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{j=1}^N s_{ij} p_i p_j + 2 \sum_{j=1}^N \alpha_j p_0 p_j \right) \quad (6)$$

The uniqueness condition on the potential energy density function calls for the following identity.

$$U - \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \bar{U}_i \equiv 0 \quad (7)$$

In the following subsections, Eq. (7) will be used to obtain the composite material constants,  $\alpha_i$ ,  $s_{ij}$ , and  $K$ , in terms of the constituents poroelastic constants,  $\bar{\alpha}_i$ ,  $\bar{s}_i$ , and  $\bar{K}_i$ . The double-porosity case will be treated in the next subsection. Results will be compared with the published solutions on the subject. Next, the multiple-porosity problem will be discussed as a generalization of the double-porosity poroelasticity.

### 2.1. Double-porosity poroelasticity

The case of  $N = 2$  is addressed in this section. Suppose a general state of confining load,  $p_0$ , and independent pore pressure disturbances,  $p_1$  and  $p_2$ , is applied to a double-porosity poroelastic sample. The undrained response of the sample instantaneously after applying the loads ( $t = 0^+$ ) is considered, so that the effect of inter-porosity fluid exchange is eliminated from the analysis. Under the described loading conditions, the fluid content increment values calculated from the single-porosity and multiple-porosity constitutive relations must be identical, i.e.,  $\zeta_i = v_i \bar{\zeta}_i$ . Substitution from the second row of Eq. (1), as well as the second and third (last) rows of Eq. (2) yields.

$$\frac{1}{K} (\alpha_1 p_0 + s_{11} p_1 + s_{12} p_2) = \frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} (\bar{\alpha}_1 p_0, + s_1 p_1) \quad (8a)$$

$$\frac{1}{K} (\alpha_2 p_0 + s_{12} p_1 + s_{22} p_2) = \frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} (\bar{\alpha}_2 p_0, + s_2 p_2) \quad (8b)$$

The linear system presented in Eqs. (8a) and (8b) can be solved for  $p_{01}$  and  $p_{02}$ , as follows.

$$p_{01} = \frac{1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1 K} \left[ \bar{K}_1 \alpha_1 p_0 - K \bar{s}_1 v_1 p_1 + \bar{K}_1 (s_{11} p_1 + s_{12} p_2) \right] \quad (9a)$$

$$p_{02} = \frac{1}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2 K} \left[ \bar{K}_2 \alpha_2 p_0 - K \bar{s}_2 v_2 p_2 + \bar{K}_2 (s_{12} p_1 + s_{22} p_2) \right] \quad (9b)$$

Substitution of Eqs. (4), (6), (9a) and (9b) in Eq. (7) yields the following quadratic polynomial equation involving  $p_i p_j$  ( $i, j = 0, 1, 2$ ) terms:

$$\begin{aligned} & \left( \frac{\bar{K}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2 v_1} \alpha_1^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2 v_1} \alpha_2^2 - K \right) p_0^2 + \left[ \frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} \left( \frac{\bar{s}_1^2}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} - \bar{s}_1 \right) K^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{11}^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{12}^2 + \left( 1 - \frac{2\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} \right) K s_{11} \right] p_1^2 + \\ & \left[ \frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} \left( \frac{\bar{s}_2^2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} - \bar{s}_2 \right) K^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{22}^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{12}^2 + \left( 1 - \frac{2\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} \right) K s_{22} \right] p_2^2 + \left( \frac{2\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} \alpha_1 s_{11} + \frac{2\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} \alpha_2 s_{12} - \frac{2\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} K \alpha_1 \right) p_0 p_1 + \left( \frac{2\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} \alpha_1 s_{12} + \frac{2\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} \alpha_2 s_{22} - \frac{2\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} K \alpha_2 \right) p_0 p_2 + s_{12} \left[ \left( 2 - \frac{2\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} - \frac{2\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} \right) K + \frac{2\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{11} + \right. \\ & \left. \frac{2\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{22} \right] p_1 p_2 \equiv 0 \end{aligned} \quad (10)$$

The undrained response ( $t = 0^+$ ) of the sample to arbitrary disturbances in the confining stress  $p_0$ , as well as the pore fluid pressures,  $p_1$  and  $p_2$ , is considered. Because the identity shown Eq. (10) should hold for any choice of  $p_0$ ,  $p_1$ , and  $p_2$ , the coefficients of each quadratic term must vanish. That is,

$$\frac{\bar{K}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2 v_1} \alpha_1^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2 v_1} \alpha_2^2 - K = 0 \quad (11a)$$

$$\frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} \left( \frac{\bar{s}_1^2}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} - \bar{s}_1 \right) K^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{11}^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{12}^2 + \left( 1 - \frac{2\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} \right) K s_{11} = 0 \quad (11b)$$

$$\frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} \left( \frac{\bar{s}_2^2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} - \bar{s}_2 \right) K^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{22}^2 + \frac{\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{12}^2 + \left( 1 - \frac{2\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} \right) K s_{22} = 0 \quad (11c)$$

$$\frac{2\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} \alpha_1 s_{11} + \frac{2\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} \alpha_2 s_{12} - \frac{2\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} K \alpha_1 = 0 \quad (11d)$$

$$\frac{2\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} \alpha_1 s_{12} + \frac{2\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} \alpha_2 s_{22} - \frac{2\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} K \alpha_2 = 0 \quad (11e)$$

$$\left[ \left( 1 - \frac{\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1^2} - \frac{\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2^2} \right) K + \frac{\bar{K}_1}{v_1 \bar{\alpha}_1^2} s_{11} + \frac{\bar{K}_2}{v_2 \bar{\alpha}_2^2} s_{22} \right] s_{12} = 0 \quad (11f)$$

The described condition establishes a number of six second-order, algebraic equations. Of the obtained six equations, however, only four are independent. If the joints between the constituents in the multi-porosity composite material remain intact, the strain compatibility condition can be used to obtain the extra needed equations. This condition may be expressed as follows.

$$v_1 \bar{\epsilon}_1 + v_2 \bar{\epsilon}_2 = \epsilon \quad (12)$$

Substitution from the first rows of Eqs. (1) and (2) in Eq. (12) gives:

$$\begin{aligned} & \left( \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_1 + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_2 - 1 \right) p_0 + \left[ \frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} \left( \bar{\alpha}_1 - \frac{\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \right) K + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_{11} + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_{12} - \alpha_1 \right] p_1 + \left[ \frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} \left( \bar{\alpha}_2 - \frac{\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \right) K + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_{12} \right. \\ & \left. + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_{22} - \alpha_2 \right] p_2 \equiv 0 \end{aligned} \quad (13)$$

Likewise, the arbitrary choice of  $p_0$ ,  $p_1$  and  $p_2$  requires that their coefficients must vanish. That is,

$$\frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_1 + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_2 - 1 = 0 \quad (14a)$$

$$\frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} \left( \bar{\alpha}_1 - \frac{\bar{s}_1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \right) K + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_{11} + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_{12} - \alpha_1 = 0 \quad (14b)$$

$$\frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} \left( \bar{\alpha}_2 - \frac{\bar{s}_2}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \right) K + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_1} \alpha_{12} + \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_2} \alpha_{22} - \alpha_2 = 0 \quad (14c)$$

Simultaneous solution of Eqs. (11a)–(11f) and (14a)–(14c) yields five solution sets for the composite material constants of which only one set provides physically meaningful values. This solution set is presented in below.

$$\alpha_1 = \frac{v_1 K}{\bar{K}_1} \bar{\alpha}_1 \quad (15a)$$

$$\alpha_2 = \frac{v_2 K}{\bar{K}_2} \bar{\alpha}_2 \quad (15b)$$

$$s_{11} = \frac{v_1 K}{\bar{K}_1} \bar{s}_1 \quad (15c)$$

$$s_{22} = \frac{v_2 K}{\bar{K}_2} \bar{s}_2 \quad (15d)$$

$$s_{12} = 0 \quad (15e)$$

$$K = \left( \frac{v_1}{\bar{K}_1} + \frac{v_2}{\bar{K}_2} \right)^{-1} \quad (15f)$$

Eqs. (15a)–(15f) agree with Eqs. (29), (30), (32), and (33) in (Berryman, 2002), as well as Eqs. (95)–(100) in (Berryman & Pride 2002).

### 2.2. Multiple-porosity poroelasticity

The systematic method that was introduced for double-porosity poroelasticity can be applied to the multiple-porosity problem. Suppose a general state of confining load,  $p_0$ , and pore pressure disturbances,  $p_1, p_2, \dots$ , and  $p_N$  is applied to a multiple-porosity poroelastic sample. The undrained response of the sample at  $t = 0^+$  is considered, so that the effect of inter-porosity fluid exchange is eliminated from the analysis. Under the described loading conditions, the fluid content increment values calculated from the single-porosity and multiple-porosity constitutive relations must be identical, i.e.,  $\zeta_i = v_i \epsilon_i$ . Substitution from the second row of Eq. (1) and second-to-last rows of Eq. (2) yields:

$$\frac{1}{K} \left( \alpha_i p_0 + \sum_{j=1}^N s_{ij} p_j \right) = \frac{v_i}{\bar{K}_i} (\bar{\alpha}_i p_0 + \bar{s}_i p_i) \quad i = 1, \dots, N \quad (16)$$

The linear system presented in Eq. (16) can be solved for  $p_0$  as follows.

$$p_{0i} = \frac{1}{\bar{\alpha}_i \bar{K} v_i} \left( \bar{K}_i \alpha_i p_0 - K \bar{s}_i v_i p_i + \bar{K}_i \sum_{j=1}^N s_{ij} p_j \right) \quad (17)$$

Substitution of Eqs. (4), (6) and (17) in Eq. (7) yields quadratic polynomials involving  $p_i p_j$ , ( $i, j = 0, 1, 2, \dots, N$ ) terms in the following form.

$$\sum_{i=0}^N \sum_{j=0}^N f_{ij}(\alpha_m, s_{mn}, K, \bar{\alpha}_i, \bar{s}_i, \bar{K}_i) p_i p_j = 0 \quad (18)$$

with the indication that coefficients  $f_{ij} = f_{ij}(\alpha_m, s_{mn}, K, \bar{\alpha}_i, \bar{s}_i, \bar{K}_i)$  are functions of the composite ( $\alpha_m, s_{mn}, K$ ) and constituents ( $\bar{\alpha}_i, \bar{s}_i, \bar{K}_i$ ) material constants. Detailed expressions of these functions are provided in Appendix A. Because the identity shown Eq. (18) should hold for any choice of  $p_0, p_1, p_2, \dots$ , and  $p_N$ , the functions  $f_{ij}$  must vanish, i.e,

$$f_{ij} = 0, \quad i, j = 0, 1, \dots, N \quad (19)$$

Assuming the joints between the constituents in the multi-porosity composite material remain intact, the strain compatibility condition can be used to obtain extra equations between the poroelastic constants. This condition may be expressed as follows.

$$\sum_{i=1}^N v_i \bar{\epsilon}_i = \epsilon \quad (20)$$

Substitution from the first rows of Eqs. (1) and (2) in Eq. (20) gives:

$$\sum_{i=1}^N g_i(\alpha_m, s_{mn}, K, \bar{\alpha}_i, \bar{s}_i, \bar{K}_i) p_i \equiv 0 \quad (21)$$

The functions  $g_i$  are provided in Appendix A. Likewise, the arbitrary choice of  $p_0, p_1, \dots$ , and  $p_N$  requires that the coefficient functions  $g_i$  must vanish, i.e.,

$$g_i = 0, \quad i = 0, 1, \dots, N \quad (22)$$

Simultaneous solution of the system comprising Eqs. (19) and (22) yields the following solution set.

$$\alpha_i = \frac{v_i \bar{\alpha}_i K}{\bar{K}_i} \quad (23a)$$

$$s_{ij} = \begin{cases} \frac{v_i \bar{s}_i K}{\bar{K}_i} & i = j \\ 0 & i \neq j \end{cases} \quad (23b)$$

$$K = \left( \sum_{k=1}^N \frac{v_k}{\bar{K}_k} \right)^{-1} \quad (23c)$$

Substitution of Eqs. (23a)–(23c) in Eqs. (19) and (21) would verify the validity of the solution. These equations extend the formulae for poroelastic constants of double-porosity materials to the general case of multiple-porosity poroelasticity.

## 3. The stiffness formulation

A result of the described analysis is that the cross-storage coefficients,  $s_{ij}$  ( $i \neq j$ ), must vanish. This finding is in agreement with published results from material property measurements of double-porosity rocks suggesting a value identical to zero (Berea Sandstone and Westerly Granite), or close to zero (Chelmsford Granite and Weber Sandstone) for  $s_{12}$  (Berryman & Wang, 1995). In fact, a small cross-storage coefficient verifies the underlying distinction between the two porosity systems. That is, an otherwise strong coupling between disturbances in the pore fluid pressure of one system, e.g.,  $p_i$ , with the kinematic response of the other, e.g.,  $\zeta_j$  ( $i \neq j$ ), under unconfined conditions ( $p_0 = 0$ ) would be an indication of little or no disparity between the considered porosity systems. In such a case, the use of a double-porosity or multiple-porosity model would not be justified.

Conversely, the described coupling does exist under constrained confinement of the sample ( $\epsilon \neq 0$ ). To elucidate the matter, the inverse matrix relation corresponding to the stiffness formulation of multiple-porosity poroelasticity is obtained, as follows.

$$\begin{pmatrix} p_0 \\ p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_N \end{pmatrix} = K_u \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\bar{B}_1 & -\bar{B}_2 & \dots & -\bar{B}_N \\ -\bar{B}_1 & 1/s_{11}^s & 1/s_{12}^s & \dots & 1/s_{1N}^s \\ -\bar{B}_2 & 1/s_{12}^s & 1/s_{22}^s & \dots & 1/s_{2N}^s \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \dots & \vdots \\ -\bar{B}_N & 1/s_{1N}^s & 1/s_{2N}^s & \dots & 1/s_{NN}^s \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon \\ \zeta_1 \\ \zeta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \zeta_N \end{pmatrix} \quad (24)$$

where  $K_u$  is the undrained bulk modulus of the multiple-porosity composite. It is expressed in terms of known material constants, as follows.

$$K_u = \frac{K}{(1 - \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i \bar{B}_i)} \tag{25}$$

recalling that  $\bar{B}_i = \bar{\alpha}_i/\bar{s}_i$  is the Skempton's pore pressure coefficient of the  $i^{\text{th}}$  porosity constituent. Correspondingly, the first row and column of the material constants matrix consists of  $N$  Skempton's pore pressure coefficients,  $B_i = \bar{B}_i$ . The submatrix  $s_{ij}^\epsilon$  is the generalized and dimensionless form of the constrained storage coefficient (Wang, 2000). This parameter is obtained in terms of previously-described material constants, as follows.

$$s_{ij}^\epsilon = K_u S_{ij}^\epsilon = \begin{cases} \frac{s_{ij}}{(1 - \sum_{k=1, k \neq i}^N \frac{\alpha_k^2}{\bar{\alpha}_k^2})} & i = j \\ \frac{1}{\bar{B}_i \bar{B}_j} & i \neq j \end{cases} \tag{26}$$

An implication of Eq. (26) is that under constrained conditions ( $\epsilon = 0$ ), disturbances in the pore fluid pressure of one porosity system, e.g.,  $p_i$ , would affect the fluid content of the other,  $\zeta_j$ . The coupling parameter  $s_{ij}^\epsilon = 1/(\bar{B}_i \bar{B}_j)$  is of the same order of magnitude as  $s_{ii}^\epsilon$ . Therefore, it cannot be neglected in mathematical treatment of double-porosity models. The necessity of including the cross-porosity mechanical coupling coefficients in double-porosity models is previously discussed in (Berryman & Wang, 1995; Lewallen & Wang, 1998; Berryman and Pride 2002).

## 4. The mixed formulations

A mixed formulation of the multiple-porosity poroelastic constitutive relation may be suggested by describing the volumetric strain,  $\epsilon$ , and pore pressures,  $p_i$ , as the control variables while allowing the confining stress,  $p_0$ , and the fluid content increments,  $\zeta_i$ , to be the dependent variables. A rearrangement of Eq. (2) or (24) provides the described mixed formulation of the constitutive behavior, as follows.

$$\begin{pmatrix} p_0 \\ \zeta_1 \\ \zeta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \zeta_N \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} K & -\alpha_1 & -\alpha_2 & \dots & -\alpha_N \\ -\alpha_1 & 1/M_{11} & 1/M_{12} & \dots & 1/M_{1N} \\ -\alpha_2 & 1/M_{12} & 1/M_{22} & \dots & 1/M_{2N} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \dots & \vdots \\ -\alpha_N & 1/M_{1N} & 1/M_{2N} & \dots & 1/M_{NN} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon \\ p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_N \end{pmatrix} \tag{27}$$

The first row and column of the material constants matrix include  $N$  Biot-Willis effective stress coefficients,  $\alpha_i$ . The submatrix  $M_{ij}$  consists of the generalized Biot Moduli (Wang, 2000). These parameters are obtained in terms of previously-obtained material constants, as follows.

$$M_{ij} = \begin{cases} \frac{K \bar{B}_i}{\alpha_i(1 - \alpha_i \bar{B}_i)} & i = j \\ \frac{K}{\alpha_i \alpha_j} & i \neq j \end{cases} \tag{28}$$

Likewise, an implication of Eq. (28) is that under constrained conditions ( $\epsilon = 0$ ) disturbances in the pore fluid pressure of one porosity system, e.g.,  $p_i$ , would affect the fluid content of the other,  $\zeta_j$ . The coupling parameter  $M_{ij} = \frac{K}{\alpha_i \alpha_j}$  is of the same order of magnitude as the  $M_{ii}$ . Therefore, it cannot be neglected in mathematical treatment of multiple-porosity models (Mehrabian & Abousleiman, 2017).

A conspicuous distinction of the multiple-porosity case is that unlike the single-porosity solids,  $S_{ij}^\epsilon \neq \frac{1}{M_{ij}}$ . The reason underlies the condition of other porosity systems while the pore fluid pressure of the  $i^{\text{th}}$  porosity system in the constrained sample is disturbed. The specific storage coefficients are obtained when the fluid contents in all but one of the porosity systems are held constant, i.e.,  $\zeta_j = 0$  ( $j \neq i$ ). However, the definition of Biot's moduli requires that the pore fluid pressures of other porosity systems remain undisturbed, i.e.,  $p_j = 0$  ( $j \neq i$ ). Indeed, the two scenarios converge to identical mathematical descriptions,  $M = \frac{K}{\alpha_i^2} \epsilon_i$ , for single-porosity materials.

An alternative manifestation of the mixed constitutive relation describing the volumetric deformation of a multiple-porosity poroelastic material involves selecting the confining stress,  $p_0$ , as well as the fluid content increments,  $\zeta_i$ , as the control variables while the volumetric strain,  $\epsilon$ , as well as the pore pressures,  $p_i$ , are the dependent variables. The described mixed formulation of the constitutive behavior can be made by obtaining the inverse matrix relation of Eq. (27). The result would be as follows.

$$\begin{pmatrix} \epsilon \\ p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \vdots \\ p_N \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/K_u & B_1 & B_2 & \dots & B_N \\ B_1 & 1/S_{11}^\sigma & 1/S_{12}^\sigma & \dots & 1/S_{1N}^\sigma \\ B_2 & 1/S_{21}^\sigma & 1/S_{22}^\sigma & \dots & 1/S_{2N}^\sigma \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \dots & \vdots \\ B_N & 1/S_{N1}^\sigma & 1/S_{N2}^\sigma & \dots & 1/S_{NN}^\sigma \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} p_0 \\ \zeta_1 \\ \zeta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \zeta_N \end{pmatrix} \tag{29}$$

The first row and column of the material constants matrix consists of  $N$  Skempton's pore pressure coefficients,  $B_i = \bar{B}_i$ . The submatrix  $S_{ij}^{\sigma}$  comprises the generalized constant-stress storage coefficients (Wang, 2000). These parameters are obtained in terms of previously-described material constants, as follows.

$$S_{ij}^{\sigma} = \begin{cases} \frac{s_{ij}}{K} & i = j \\ 0 & i \neq j \end{cases} \quad (30)$$

Similar to the case of storage coefficient  $S_{ij}$ , the cross-porosity components of constant-stress storage coefficients matrix vanish.

## 5. Conclusion

The uniqueness condition on the potential energy density function of multiple-porosity poroelasticity is used to obtain the poroelastic constants of a material characterized by the theory. The compliance, stiffness, and mixed formulations of the constitutive relations describing the volumetric deformation of such materials are presented and discussed. It is shown that the mechanical coupling between two porosity systems in a multiple-porosity solid cannot be neglected under constrained strain conditions.
