# Detonative propulsion

Piotr Wolański\*

*Institute of Heat Engineering, Warsaw University of Technology, Nowowiejska 21/25, 00-665 Warsaw, Poland*

Available online 27 November 2012

## Abstract

A survey of propulsion based on detonation of chemical systems is provided in this paper. After a short historical review, basic schematics of engines utilizing detonation as the combustion mechanism are described. Possible improvement of propulsive efficiency due to detonative combustion which results in a significant pressure increase is presented, and a comparison of deflagrative and detonative combustion is discussed. Basic research on Pulsed Detonation Engines (PDE) and rotating detonations in cylindrical and disk-like chambers for different mixtures is presented. Basic principles of engines utilizing Standing Detonation Waves as well as Ram Accelerators are also provided. Detailed descriptions of PDE as well as Rotating Detonation Engines (RDE) are given. Different implementations of the PDE concept are presented and experimental and theoretical results to date are reviewed. Special attention is given to RDE, since rotating detonation can be applied to all kinds of propulsive engines including rocket, ramjet, turbine, and combined-cycle engines. A survey of detonative propulsion research carried out at different laboratories is presented, and possible future applications of such propulsion systems are discussed. A short note on detonative propulsion using non-chemical energy sources is also given.

## 1. Introduction

The possibility of improving a thermodynamic cycle by the application of detonative combustion, instead of deflagration, was first proposed by Zel'dovich [1]. The first suggestion of a possible implementation of detonation to a propulsion system, however, originated from the University of Michigan, where Nicholls et al. proposed and built the first pulsed detonation engine, which utilized detonation of a hydrogen–air mixture to produce thrust [2]. Also at the same university, Adamson et al. analyzed the possibility of the application

of rotating detonation waves to improve the efficiency of rocket propulsion [3,4]. At the same time, the idea of the application of standing detonation for propulsion systems was examined by many authors both for ramjet as well as rocket engines [5–7]. In the meantime at Novosibirsk Institute of Hydrodynamics, stationary spinning detonation was successfully achieved by Voitsekhovskii [8,9]. Later attempts to develop a continuous detonation propulsion system were undertaken at the University of Michigan, but unfortunately no successful operation of such a system was achieved. In the conclusion of their report, the researchers clearly stated [10]: “Nothing has been found that makes the concept not feasible but important questions... remain unanswered. It is believed that much is to be gained from further studies of rotating detonation waves

In the 1980's, Hertzberg et al. from the University of Washington in Seattle proposed development of a novel system of integrated propulsion – called Ram Accelerator. Such a system is based on a tube filled with detonable mixtures in which a projectile is continuously accelerated by products of combustion that is initiated by the moving projectile. In this paper, a survey of all propulsion systems based on detonation will be given.

## 2. Deflagration versus detonation

It has been well known for more than a century that combustion processes can be performed in two different modes: deflagration and detonation. The detonation process was first described by Berthelot, Vieille, Mallard, and Le Chatelier in 1881, and nearly 20 years later the zero-dimensional theory of detonation was independently presented by Chapman and Jouguet and subsequently described in many basic texts on combustion [11–13]. In the case of deflagration, a combustion wave can propagate with subsonic velocity and results in a decrease in pressure and density, while in the case of detonation, it produces a strong shock wave followed by a reaction front which propagates with sonic velocity as related to the leading front. In the case of detonation, the final products have higher density and pressure than the mixture at the initial conditions. In both cases of deflagration and detonation, the temperatures of the combustion products are much higher than the initial temperature of the mixture, but are not the same for the two different processes. For the same mixture composition, the maximum temperatures of the detonation products are higher than the maximum temperature of deflagrative combustion. Also significant differences exist in the velocity of these two combustion waves. During deflagration, the velocity of the reaction front is typically in the range of cm/s for laminar flames and m/s (even in

the order hundreds m/s) for turbulent flames, while a detonation front in gaseous or hybrid mixtures always propagates with a velocity of km/s. For direct initiation of detonation, much higher energy is needed than for initiation of deflagration. The detonation process can also be initiated by self-acceleration of a flame in relatively long channels or tubes.

Since combustion of the same mixture can result in very different parameters of the products, depending upon if the process has a deflagrative or detonative nature, application of different modes of combustion to a propulsion system can also result in different performance of the propulsion system considered. A comparison of ideal thermodynamic cycles for different combustion processes applied in an engine is presented in Fig. 1 [14]. In all cases, combustion starts at point 2. ## 3. Detonative engines

There are many different possible ways of implementing detonative combustion in propulsion systems. In engines, detonation can be stationary relative to the engine frame, stationary or quasi-stationary relative to a moving coordinate system, transient with variable parameters during engine operation, or pulsating in nature. Application of a stationary detonation is related to the concept of an engine with a standing detonation wave, while quasi-stationary detonation propulsion (stationary relative to the rotating frame of reference) is linked to the Rotating Detonation Engine (RDE), which is alternately

called the Continuous Detonation Wave Engine (CDWE). In Ram Accelerators (RAMAC), detonative propulsion can be a transient process in the superdetonative mode or in the mode of a condensed-phase explosive-lined launch tube. Since in the RAMAC system, the projectile should undergo constant acceleration, detonation parameters must be carefully tailored to the local velocity to ensure continuous thrust. Finally, Pulsed Detonation Engines (PDE) use the process of flame acceleration to detonation in specially designed tubes. Detonation lasts a relatively short time and produces impulsive thrust. After evacuation of the combustion products from the detonation tube (thrust tube), the tube is refilled with fresh detonative mixture and detonation is reinitiated. Such a system can work with the frequency ranging from a few to tens of hertz.

All engines based on detonative combustion offer a higher rate of energy release, higher thermodynamic efficiency, and a more compact heat release chamber as compared with conventional engines using deflagration. They can be applied to propel subsonic and supersonic aircraft as well as to space planes. Detonative combustion could also be used for transient propulsion in Ram Accelerators. The specific impulse of all engines utilizing detonative combustion may be significantly greater than that of conventional engines. Theoretically predicted impulses for PDE engines as a function of flight Mach number are compared to conventional propulsion systems in Fig. 2. [15].

Many papers dealing with different aspects of engines or propulsion systems, which use detonation for generation of thrust, have been previously published [16–22].

### 3.2. Ram Accelerator

The idea of the Ram Accelerator, or so-called RAMAC, was first proposed at the end of the 1980's by a group from the University of Washington working under the direction of A. Hertzberg [31–33]. It is based on acceleration of a projectile in a tube filled with combustible mixture. Unlike classical ramjets, no fuel is carried on the projectile, but rather in the RAMAC, fuel and air (or a different oxidizer) is placed in the relatively long tube and the projectile is injected into the tube at supersonic velocity. The specially designed shape of the projectile and mixture composition can compress and initiate oblique detonation in a designated area of the projectile, thus producing a pressure increase and positive thrust, which accelerates the projectile. ### 3.3. Pulse detonation engine

Pulsed detonation engines (PDE) are the class of detonative propulsion systems which have received the most attention during the last quarter of a century. This resulted in development of many different kinds of PDE, as well as in different types of engine applications and of varying scale. Depending on the type of PDE, they may range from very simple single-tube to multi-tube systems, valve-less or with mechanical valves, they can be used as combustion chambers for gas turbine and turbofan engines, and can also be applied to rocket propulsion. PDE can range in size from micro-engines up to very large systems developed for gas turbine or turbojet engines.

PDE typically consists of a sufficiently long tube, which is filled with fresh fuel-oxidizer mixture and ignited by a sufficiently strong energy source. The flame initiated by ignition must, in a relatively short time, accelerate to the detonation velocity, such that the transition from deflagration to detonation happens in a relatively small distance. Detonative combustion produces high pressure, which is converted to thrust. After all of the combustible mixture is consumed by detonation, combustion products have to be evacuated from the tube and fresh mixture must be quickly resupplied, and the cycle is repeated. PDE can operate over a wide envelope of flight Mach number, ranging from rest and up to greater than Mach

4, but the engine operates in a pulsed mode, so the thrust varies in time and the detonation must be initiated with each cycle. Typical frequency of such engine operation is usually in the range of a dozen Hertz. The system is complex because fast purging and refilling are required. In addition, the engines usually operate at stoichiometric conditions (due to the necessity of fast initiation of detonation), and the frequency is relatively low. If pulsed detonation were to be applied to turbojet combustion chambers, it would likely be necessary to add extra air to decrease the temperature before the first turbine stage.

#### 3.3.1. Detonation initiation

One of the basic aspects of successful PDE operation is very reliable and fast initiation of detonation. As was already mentioned, detonation can be initiated either directly or a deflagration initiated in a tube could eventually accelerate to detonation. Since direct initiation of detonation usually requires large initiation energy, more practical means of initiation of detonation in the PDE is by self-acceleration of a flame. In this case, the energy necessary to initiate combustion is very low, so low power ignition systems can be used. The process of self-acceleration to detonation, deflagration to detonation transition (DDT), has been studied by many authors and information on this topic can be found elsewhere, but the most detailed pictures of the transition into detonation in gaseous mixtures were taken by Oppenheim et al. [52]. In these pictures (Fig. 11 [52]) one can clearly see local, self-generated explosions, which trigger transition to detonation.

To speed-up the transition into detonation in tubes or channels, Schelkin [53] proposed to put a spiral into the tube, which intensifies turbulence induced by the accelerating flame and thus significantly reduces the transition distance to detonation. Even more rapid transition into detonation can be obtained by placing obstacles in the tube. Detailed study of such a process was first conducted at the Warsaw University of Technology [54]. In Fig. 12 [54] one can clearly see how obstacles accelerate a flame to a detonation. Since this finding, much new research (both experimental and numerical modeling) has been conducted on the influence of obstacles on the process of acceleration of flames into detonations. Some examples of DDT studies can be found in [17–19,55–68].

#### 3.3.3. PDE with turbine

A hybrid propulsion system that incorporates a pulsed detonation combustor (PDC) within a conventional gas turbine engine or even turbojet engine offers the potential of a large increase in operating efficiency of the overall system for both propulsion and stationary power generation applications. This is due to the improvement of the thermodynamic cycle PDC and gas-turbine too. Such a large scale hybrid system which includes a gas turbine with pulsed detonation combustor (PDC) was already built and evaluated at a GE facility [20,105]. The system consisted of eight PDC tubes arranged in an annular can configuration integrated with a single-stage axial turbine nominally rated for about 4 kg/s, 25,000 rpm, and 1000 hp. It has already been successfully tested. The PDC was operated with the frequency up to 30 Hz (per tube) using stoichiometric  $C_2H_4$ -air mixture and it operated at a mass flow rate of about 4.5 kg/s while the turbine and compressor operated at 18,500 rpm with a power of 350 hp. Besides PDC, there are some other differences between this hybrid system and classical gas turbines. As it can be seen from Fig. 20a, after the compressor, there is a collector, which distributes compressed air through special valves to the individual PDC tubes. Unlike in a classical gas turbine, there is an extra compressor (called

the Bypass Pump), which provides extra air for cooling the PDC as well as for adding extra air to detonation products from the PDC. Extra compressed air is necessary, since at the output of the PDC, pressure is higher than at the exit of the main compressor. So for this reason, a special mixing plenum chamber is required before the first stage of the high-pressure turbine (HPT) to create products with a temperature acceptable for the first stage of the turbine. When sufficient data from this research facility has been collected, the real efficiency gain from the system can be evaluated.

Smaller demonstrators of such technology were also tested at Hiroshima University and Tokyo Metropolitan University independently [106,107]. In both cases, a small laboratory scale PDC connected to a small gas turbine was tested. In Fig. 21a schematic diagram of the Hiroshima University demonstrator equipped with a PDC connected to an automotive turbocharger is presented, while in Fig. 21b typical measured pressures at the turbine inlet and outlet are shown. Also a small hydrogen-fueled pulse detonation combustor with a length of 200 mm and a volume of 120 cm<sup>3</sup> was developed for a 1 kW-class micro gas turbine at Tokyo Metropolitan University [107]. The combustor was designed based on thermodynamic cycle analysis. The visualization of the fuel injection and concentration measurements were conducted. The tests revealed that detonation occurred in the combustor, so the basic idea of operation of such a system was confirmed.

In the study performed at the University of Cincinnati, the performance of a hybrid propulsion system integrating an axial flow turbine with multiple pulse detonation combustors was investigated [108]. The main goal of that work was to estimate the performance of the turbine component of a hybrid PDC-turbine engine system (Fig. 22). The integrated PDC-turbine system performance was quantified by measuring the amount of power generated by the turbine section. Initial research was conducted for the case with steady flow, constant pressure combustors (classical solution) and then for the PDC-turbine system. This allowed direct comparison of turbine performance operating with PDC and with a steady combustor as well as investigation of the interaction of detonation products with the axial flow turbine. It was found that the peak shock overpressure caused by detonation in a neighboring tube corresponds only to approximately 3% of the Von Neumann spike pressure. It was also found that the efficiency of the PDC driven turbine was comparable to that of the steady driven turbine in all tested ranges of system operation. It was found, however, that the effect of non-uniform filling decreased the turbine efficiency.

### 3.4. Rotating detonation engines

In the early 1960's, Voitkekhovskii, Mitrofanov, and Topchiyan performed experiments on continuously rotating detonations [8,9]. They were able to stabilize rotating detonations in a cylindrical chamber for a relatively long duration. Experiments were conducted for stoichiometric oxy-acetylene mixtures at relatively low pressure in a disk-shaped chamber connected directly to a low-pressure dump chamber. A schematic diagram of this research facility is shown in Fig. 27 [9]. Detonation was initiated in a cylindrical channel by an electrical spark. The channel geometry and parameters of the mixture were specially selected. Registration of the process was made with a rotating drum camera, so motion-compensated pictures of the propagating wave could be obtained. A picture of a propagating, rotating detonation with six heads is presented in Fig. 27b. This experiment conducted at the Institute of Hydrodynamics of the Siberian Division of the Soviet Academy of Sciences in Novosibirsk must be considered as the first fundamental step to developing the Rotating Detonation Engine.

#### 3.4.1. Basic research on continuously rotating detonation

Continuously rotating detonation (CRD), or sometimes called continuous spin detonation, is the basic process for all RDE. It is most often initiated and run in cylindrical chambers, but occasionally in disk-like chambers or other configurations. Typical shapes of chambers and other arrangements in which a CRD can be studied are presented in Fig. 28 [116] and Fig. 30 [117]. Cylindrical chambers with an internal cylinder (Fig. 30a) or cylindrical chambers with an internal cylinder-cone insertion (Fig. 30b) are most often used for research, but sometimes disk-like chambers with fuel and oxidizer supplied from a central wall or from an outside wall are also used (Fig. 30c and d). Only occasionally can a CRD be initiated at the outside wall of the cylinder (Fig. 30e), but in such a configuration, the detonation most often propagates in both directions around half of the cylinder's outside perimeter, and then both waves are reflected and propagate backward. Other geometries are also possible, such as dual conical walls (inner and outer) or even more complex geometries. For each case, a special fuel and oxidizer supply system is designed. Most often, supply of both gaseous fuel and gaseous oxidizer is at critical or supercritical conditions. Less frequently, one of the components may be in liquid form. In the case of systems where air is used as the oxidizer, kerosene can be injected in liquid form. Up to now, liquid oxygen has only been used with gaseous hydrogen, since for rocket engines, liquid hydrogen is used for cooling and is preheated to such a degree that it is injected into the thrust chamber in gaseous form. #### 3.4.2. Numerical calculation of continuously rotating detonation

The first calculation of rotating detonation structure was performed by Adamson et al. [4]. They examined the possibility of using a CRD in a rocket motor. Calculations were performed for a rocket motor utilizing a heterogeneous propellant. Since, at that time, computer power was

significantly less than today's computers, many simplifying assumptions had to be made, but despite this, the calculations revealed the basic structure of such a detonation (Fig. 42). With today's computer power, more detailed calculations of the flow structure in a CRD can be performed. In order to solve the flow field in a CRD chamber, the two or three dimensional unsteady Euler equations, with an additional conservation equation for the reactants and a chemical source term, are used. Since the effect of transport phenomena is usually small compared to the convection terms in a detonation propagating in a duct, transport properties such as the viscosity, thermal conduction, and mass diffusion are ignored in the calculations. In different approaches, one-step, two-step, or detailed chemical reaction models are used for the simulations. The details of the methods used can be found in many papers related to this problem [118–120,130,132,134–137,143–157]. Initially, in many papers, the general structure of the propagating CRD was calculated, but later, more detailed calculation of the structure was performed. ## 5. Nonchemical detonation engines

Application of nuclear detonation to rocket propulsion systems was considered by Everett and Ulam [172]. The idea of such a propulsion system was presented soon after the first nuclear bomb was detonated. The Orion propulsion system was supposed to detonate a series of small nuclear bombs to give propulsion to orbital and interplanetary missions, but it never went beyond conceptual studies.

Another propulsion system that would use nonchemical detonation is laser-based propulsion systems, proposed by Kantrowitz [173]. In this idea, a powerful laser, placed on the ground, could supply energy to the rocket. Periodically produced high power laser pulses can initiate optical breakdown and produce so-called “optical detonations” in the rocket nozzle and therefore provide propulsive energy to the vehicle. Such an optical discharge could heat air (or an ablative

material) in the rocket nozzle, producing thrust. Research in this direction has been basically limited to small demonstrators of such propulsion systems or theoretical analysis of such propulsion. More about laser detonation propulsion can be found in many publications devoted to the subject [174–176].

## 6. Conclusions

The idea of applying chemical detonation to propulsion systems has been known for more than a half century. The concept is very attractive, since it offers a significant increase of engine efficiency over the conventional systems that use the deflagrative combustion mode. Many different systems based on detonative combustion have been proposed for jet propulsion applications, offering improvement in engine subsystems as well as in overall efficiency and simplification of the engine design. The improvements basically derive from the detonation density and speed of reaction being very high and the fact that pressure rises significantly above the initial chamber pressure, such that combustion occurs over a very small distance. The high power density allows the combustion chamber to be compact, and as a consequence of increasing the operating pressure, the thermal efficiency of the system is also increased.

Most of the recent research has been focused on development of PDE (Pulse Detonation Engines) and RDE (Rotating Detonation Engines). There are already many single-tube or multi-tube PDE air-breathing or rocket-type propulsion systems under development. Work on the application of PDE to improve the cycle efficiency of gas turbine power or propulsion systems is also under way.

In RDE, beside basic theoretical and experimental research, most effort has been focused on studies of the application of that system to rocket propulsion, since it offers the greatest advantage of continuous rotating detonation when coupled to an aerospace nozzle. The application of CRD (continuous rotating detonation) to gas turbines is also underway.

Presently, the development of the PDE and RDE is carried out in many countries including: China, France, Japan, Korea, Poland, Russia, Singapore, and the USA among others. The experiments are conducted for a number of possible configurations of the PDE and RDE. Let us hope that in the near future, chemical detonation will be effectively utilized in many propulsion and power generation systems, but before this happens still many crucial problems remain, related to all aspects of these systems, which must be solved.
