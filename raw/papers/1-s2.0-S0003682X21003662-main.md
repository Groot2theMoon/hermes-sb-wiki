## ABSTRACT

The acoustic modelling of fibrous materials is fundamental for the design of a sound-absorbing acoustic treatment. Several analytical formulas have been proposed to calculate the acoustic performance of materials made of fibres with a homogeneous diameter and a mono, bi, or three-dimensional distribution, while only a few studies have been carried out on materials made up of a mixture of fibres with non-homogeneous diameter. Fibrous materials for automotive applications, developed both for acoustic and thermal applications, are commonly obtained by moulding mixtures of recycled fibres of different nature, bonded together and compacted with a wide range of variability in density and thickness. The acoustic modelling of the mixtures is thus relevant for the simulation of the performance of the final component.

The main goal of the study was to determine the physical properties of a given mixture of fibres to compute its acoustic performance, based on the knowledge of the characteristic of each kind of fibre in the blend composition. A homogenisation process is proposed to determine the effective density and radius of a given mixture according to its composition and the characteristics of each kind of fibre. The normal incidence sound absorption coefficient of the investigated mixtures was computed according to the Johnson-Champoux-Allard-Lafarge (JCAL) model, by using the physical parameters, analytically computed from the effective properties of the mixture, as input data. The reliability of the approach was validated by comparing the computed results with the experimental airflow resistivity and the normal incidence sound absorption coefficient measured for five different fibre mixtures, at different compression rates.

## 1. Introduction

During the last fifty years, successful research has been carried out to characterise the acoustic behaviour and the performance of homogeneous fibrous materials. Several models can be found in the literature to analyse the performance of the fibrous materials, either based on numerical approaches or semi-analytical solutions [1–3]. Reliable knowledge of the material physical properties, which are required as input data for prediction models, is of fundamental importance to obtain accurate results. Numerous models were proposed to compute the physical properties of fibrous materials, such as airflow resistivity, open porosity, tortuosity, viscous and thermal characteristic of lengths, and thus their acoustical performances. A thorough and comprehensive overview of such mod-

els can be found in the work recently published by Pompoli and Bonfiglio [4]. They can be grouped in three main categories: *i*. empirical models, which are based on relationship derived from regression of experimental data [5,6]; *ii*. geometrical models, based on the knowledge of the structure of the fibres, and, in particular, of their radius and their orientation [7,8]; *iii*. hybrid models, which use experimental values measured at a certain density combined with analytical models to evaluate the non-acoustic properties for any compression rate [9,10]. Empirical models require experimental tests to be performed on each material, for each density, to obtain the coefficients from polynomial or power fit. Moreover, they do not allow for an optimization of the material by tweaking its properties. On the other hand, geometrical models require the microstructural characterisation of the material. Even though it is well known that the transport and the acoustic properties of fibrous materials are dependent on the fibres radii distribution [11,12] and their orientation [13], the determination of these

properties is rather complex. For fibrous materials, which are designed and tailored for a specific purpose, it is reasonable to assume a unique radius of the fibres. On the other hand, the size of the fibres of natural or recycled materials is described by a complex statistical distribution of radii, which can be approximated by computing an effective radius [14]. The determination of the distribution of radii of a certain kind of fibre requires a statistical analysis on a significant number of SEM (scanning electronic microscopy) images of the fibres. Moreover, it should be noted that fibres are not aligned parallel one to another but they likely exhibit a two-dimensional or three-dimensional orientation. Such a rather complicated analysis may be worthless considering the great variability of the dimensional properties of the recycled fibres, due to the different origin of the materials. Alternatively, an effective radius can be evaluated from the nominal linear density of the fibre, expressed in *d tex*, when this is known. It should be noted that such information is generally available for industrially produced fibres, but not for recycled fibres of different origins, like cotton for example.

The use of recycled fibre mixtures is very common to produce components for acoustic treatment in the automotive field [15–17]. The composite fibrous mixture comprises both coarse and fine fibres kept together with a binding agent, which can also be fibrous. The mixtures are vacuum moulded into the designated tool with a heat treating. The different kinds of fibres within the mixture may provide either acoustic or mechanical benefits according to their nature. Coarser fibres have a greater influence on the mechanical behaviour of the mixture, since an increase of the diameter, or the length, is likely to increase the overall stiffness, which is desirable in a carpet system to improve resilience. Typically, coarser fibres constitute from 10% to 30% of the mixture. Finer fibres improve the acoustic absorption performance of the mixture, which generally comprises from 25% to 70% of finer fibres, depending on the application of the material and the acoustical target of the component. Within the composite, the fibres are bonded together via thermobonding [18]. This process provides the structure of the mixture and determines the mechanical and acoustic properties of the final component. It is important to note that in some cases the size of the fibres can be controlled (PET fibres are obtained from crushed bottles, melted and extruded again) while for other materials (e.g. cotton-based) the fibres come from industrial processing waste, or the recycling of clothes and can have a rather variable size. With all these variables, it is not an easy task to predict the acoustical properties of the different mixtures and their densities.

This research aims to analytically determine the physical properties of a given fibre mixture, to predict its acoustic behaviour, based on the evaluation of an effective radius, from the knowledge of the blend composition and the characteristics of each kind of used fibre. In fact, in contrast with mono-fibre materials, a non-trivial issue is to define a relationship between the random individual fibres, which constitute the mixture, and the performance of the final component. This paper considers an indirect approach to evaluate the effective radius of loose fibres, based on the determination of fibre's characteristics from airflow resistivity measurements, performed at different compression rates, combined with the use of an analytical model. Once the radius has been determined, transport and acoustic properties can be calculated for any arbitrary fibre radius and open porosity. The accuracy of such an analytical approach was assessed through experimental measurements, performed on the different kinds of loose fibres. A weighted average is proposed to determine the effective density and radius of a given mixture according to its composition and the characteristics of each fibre of the blend. From these effective properties, it was possible to analytically compute the six physical parameters required as input data in the Johnson-Champoux-

Allard-Lafarge (JCAL) model, which was then applied to evaluate the normal incidence sound absorption coefficient of each investigated mixture. The results were validated by comparing the predicted results with the measured airflow resistivity and the normal incidence sound absorption coefficient of five different mixtures of fibres, for different compression rates.

The next section provides a brief overview of the analytical model used in this work to evaluate the physical parameters required to describe a fibrous material as an equivalent fluid according to the JCAL model. The investigate materials and their characterisation is described in Section 3. The proposed method to evaluate the acoustic properties of fibre mixtures is validated by comparison with experimental results in Section 4.

## 2. Analytical model overview

The acoustical behaviour of the fibrous material can be analysed on a macroscopic scale, by assuming its solid matrix to be motionless and perfectly rigid, through the well-known 6-parameter phenomenological JCAL model. Such equivalent fluid model describes the material using the effective density  $\rho$  and the effective bulk modulus  $K$ , which depend on 6 physical parameters: the airflow resistivity  $\sigma$ , the open porosity  $\phi$ , the tortuosity  $\alpha_\infty$ , the viscous  $\Lambda$  and thermal  $\Lambda'$  characteristic lengths, and the static thermal permeability  $k_0$ . For a given fibrous material these non-acoustic parameters can be computed using specific models; in this study, the model proposed by Pompoli and Bonfiglio [4] was adopted. It can be applied to materials with an open porosity  $\phi$  between 0.80 and 0.99, and a randomly assembled symmetric or asymmetric radii distribution of the fibres. The model computes the airflow resistivity of the material according to the formulation proposed by Tamayol et al. [8], in which the values of the coefficients were conveniently modified:

$$\sigma = \frac{\eta}{(2a)^2} \frac{\sqrt{1 - (1 - \phi)}}{0.21 \left( \frac{0.71}{1 - \phi} - 3 \sqrt{\frac{0.71}{1 - \phi}} + 3 - \sqrt{\frac{1}{0.71}} \right)} \quad (1)$$

where  $a$  represents the effective radius of the fibres and  $\eta$  is the viscosity of the air. The tortuosity  $\alpha_\infty$  is determined from a well-known empirical model, known as Archie's law, with the coefficient  $\Gamma = 0.9574$ :

$$\alpha_\infty = \left( \frac{1}{\phi} \right)^\Gamma = \left( \frac{1}{\phi} \right)^{0.9574} \quad (2)$$

The viscous characteristic length is determined by using the formulation proposed by Umnova et al. [19], even though, in here, the open porosity  $\phi$  is raised to the power of a different coefficient:

$$\Lambda = a \frac{(2 - \phi)\phi^{2.732}}{2(1 - \phi)} \quad (3)$$

while the thermal characteristic length can be determined by introducing a fit coefficient into the model proposed by Umnova et al. [19] and by Hirotsawa and Nakagawa [10]:

$$\Lambda' = a \frac{\phi}{(1 - \phi + 0.0023)} \quad (4)$$

Finally, the thermal permeability can be obtained by modifying the model proposed by Umnova et al. [19] as:

$$k_0' = a^2 \frac{3.107\eta}{\sigma\phi^{2.506}} \quad (5)$$

The effective complex density of a given fibrous material can be expressed according to the JCAL model:

$$\rho(\omega) = \frac{\alpha_\infty \rho_0}{\phi} \left[ 1 + \frac{\sigma \phi}{j\omega \rho_0 \alpha_\infty} \sqrt{1 + j \frac{4\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 \Lambda^2 \phi^2} \frac{\eta \rho_0 \omega}{\phi}} \right] \quad (6)$$

and its effective complex bulk modulus is defined as:

$$K(\omega) = \frac{\gamma \frac{P_0}{\phi}}{\gamma - (\gamma - 1) \left[ 1 - j \frac{\phi \kappa}{k_0 C_p \rho_0 \omega} \sqrt{1 + j \frac{4\kappa^2}{\kappa^2 \Lambda^2 \phi^2} \frac{C_p \rho_0 \omega}{\phi}} \right]^{-1}} \quad (7)$$

where  $\rho_0$  is the density of the air,  $\gamma$  the specific heat ratio,  $C_p$  the molar heat capacity,  $\kappa$  the thermal conduction coefficient and  $P_0$  the static pressure. For non-homogeneous fibres, the effective radius can be calculated by pursuing a homogenization process. Some authors [11,12] have proposed two different approaches for the calculation of the equivalent radius. In [11] the authors investigated the use of an effective fibre diameter for polydisperse fibrous medium calculated as the weighted averaged diameter of the fibre

### 3.2. Modelling and characterisation of loose fibre mixtures

To evaluate the acoustic properties of a given fibre mixture, knowing only its composition and the properties of the loose fibres, it is necessary to determine the mixture equivalent density, as the average of the density of every single kind of fibre  $\rho_{f,i}$ , weighted by its percentage weight  $X_i$ :

$$\rho_{mix} = \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{X_i}{\rho_{f,i}}} \quad (9)$$

Analogously, an equivalent effective radius of the mixture can be computed from the effective radius of each kind of fibre composing the blend, considering that the solid part of the porous material contributes all of the mass of the bulk porous material [12]:

$$a_{mix} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\rho_{mix} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{X_i}{\rho_{f,i} a_{eff,i}^2}}} \quad (10)$$

$$\phi_{mix} = 1 - \frac{\rho}{\rho_{mix}} \quad (11)$$

From the equivalent radius  $a_{mix}$  and the open porosity  $\phi_{mix}$ , the physical parameters of each fibre-mixture considered in this study were determined according to the analytical model presented in Section 2, and then the normal incidence sound absorption was computed using the JCAL model. It should be mentioned, that the equivalent radius of each mixture was evaluated from the effective radius of each set of loose fibres according to eq. (10), which was affected by experimental errors, expressed in Table 2 in terms of standard deviation  $\sigma_{eff}$ . (10) the standard rule of propagation of uncertainties, is also provided. To validate the proposed approach, each of the five fibre-mixtures was experimentally characterised, in terms of airflow resistivity and normal incidence sound absorption coefficient, for different compression rates. The airflow resistivity was measured, on samples with a diameter of 100 mm, according to the alternate airflow approach described in the standard ISO 9053-2:2020 [21]. The normal incidence sound absorption coefficient was measured using the transfer function method according to the ISO 10534-2 [22], in the frequency range between 100 Hz and 4200 Hz, by using a 45 mm diameter impedance tube. Different compression rates of the investigated materials (either loose fibres, or mixtures) were obtained by inserting the samples between a metal wire grid and either a movable rigid plate, in the case of sound absorption coefficient measurement, or a second metal wire grid for the airflow resistivity measurements, as shown in Fig. 2. The comparison between computed and measured data is presented in the next section.

## 4. Results

### 4.1. Experimental survey on loose fibres

The airflow resistivity was measured for all the different kinds of fibre employed in the composition of the five mixtures. The

accuracy and the uncertainty associated with the proposed approach, based on the evaluation of an effective radius from the experimental airflow resistivity, were analysed by characterising the samples of loose fibres at different compression rates. ## 5. Conclusion

In this paper, an approach for the prediction of acoustical properties of complex mixtures of recycled fibres was proposed, based on the characteristics of each kind of fibre present in the composition. At first, an indirect approach, based on the experimental airflow resistivity and open porosity evaluated for different compression rates, was used to determine the effective radius of each single set of loose recycled fibres composing the considered mixtures. From the effective radius, the transport and acoustic properties of the loose fibres were computed using the JCAL model. A good agreement was found between the experimental normal incidence sound absorption coefficient and the computed results. For all the investigated fibres a mean deviation within 0.02 and 0.05 was found between the experimental and computed sound absorption coefficient, mostly associated with resonance effects which are not taken into account by the JCAL model. In the proposed methodology an equivalent density and effective radius for a given blend were computed from the characteristics of each set of loose fibres, weighted for their percentage in the mixture composition. The airflow resistivity of the mixture analytically computed showed a good accuracy compared to the experimental

data. Moreover, the physical parameters analytically computed from the equivalent effective characteristics of the mixture allowed to compute its normal incidence sound absorption coefficient. Once again, the predicted results were found in good agreement with the experimental data for all the investigated mixtures at different compression rates, with mean deviations not exceeding 0.03. This method can be used to acoustically design any mixture of fibres, including recycled ones, whose dimensional characteristics are not known a priori, due to a high variability caused by their production process. In addition, the method of determining the effective radius of the loose fibres starting from the measurement of airflow resistivity can be used to carry out quality control on the recycled loose fibres used in the mixtures.
