## **The effect of visual and acoustic factors on the sound preference for waterscapes in urban public spaces** 

Xiaoyin Yang[a,*] and Jian Kang[b,*] 

- _a. Key Laboratory of Cold Region Urban and Rural Human Settlement Environment Science and Technology, Ministry of Industry and Information Technology, School of Architecture, Harbin Institute of Technology, Harbin, China_ 

- _b. Institute for Environmental Design and Engineering, The Bartlett, University College London (UCL), London, UK_ 

**Abstract:** Waterscape has been found to have a positive impact on preference in public space. This study evaluated three fountains in Harbin, China, to explore whether their dynamic visual form and corresponding sound rhythm changes would affect the waterscapes’ sound preference evaluation. In total, 150 visitors were selected to answer a questionnaire on-site regarding the fountain’s perception, including its visual and acoustic environment. This field experiment revealed that people have different preferences for the sound of waterscapes. Each waterscape was then classified and subjectively rated for its visual and auditory aspects in a laboratory experiment. The results showed that there was little difference in the rating of water rhythm when there was no visual stimulus. However, participants preferred a dynamic visual form with distinct rhythm changes to a continuous steady-state form. The higher the score of the visual form, the better the rating of the waterscape. In this study, a significant positive correlation was found between the dynamic visual form of fountains and the preference for waterscapes. When adding music to a fountain, combining the continuous steady-state fountain with light music improved the evaluation of the waterscape; furthermore, the combination of the dynamic fountain and strong rhythm music was more popular among the participants. In general, the evaluation of a waterscape is affected by its form, environment, and music. 

**Keywords:** Waterscape, audio-visual interaction, sound preference, urban public space

## **1. Introduction** 

In the 1960s, musician R.M. Schafer first drew people's attention to the acoustic environment, pioneering work and research in soundscapes [1]. The soundscape is at the same time a physical and social environment in which sound is one of the essential elements[2]. Soundscape research has been carried out in a variety of disciplines[3]. M. Southworth described the acoustic characteristics in urban spaces from an urban planning perspective[4]. Current research focuses on the relationship between soundscape and landscape. While different kinds of sounds influence the patterns of urban soundscapes, landscape features influence the spatially varying patterns of urban soundscapes[5]. Landscapes and soundscapes are closely related and significantly affect the experience of park users. Several landscape studies of urban parks have used the concept of soundscape proposed by Schafer[6]. 

Creating urban open spaces has changed people’s lifestyles while also promoting harmony between society and nature[7]. The acoustic environment is a key factor affecting the overall comfort of urban open spaces[8]. Fountains, water sculptures, and other water features are commonly used in such spaces[9]. A field study conducted in a city square demonstrated an extremely positive attitude towards nature sounds, with 75% of the participants responding positively to the sound of the waterscape[10]. Another questionnaire survey showed that nature sounds received very positive feedback in terms of urban soundscape elements. Water sounds were the second-most favoured after wind sounds[11]. Research on water sounds initially focused on the suppression of urban traffic noise, as they were considered more soothing than other masking sounds[12]. It was found that there are differences between water sounds, both in terms of masking and their subjective effect on silence[13]. In a laboratory study combining sounds from streams and lakes with traffic and construction noise, people rated them as significantly better than other natural soundscapes[14]. However, compared to steady water sounds, people prefer a faster frequency change, such as fountains. Aspects such as water velocity, flow velocity, water drop height, jet frequency, and impact area are the main factors that influence this[15]. Previous experiments have proven that fountain sounds can effectively mask the volume of traffic noise, thereby improving the environmental quality of areas near them[16][17]. 

Many studies on masking traffic noise using natural sound elements have focused on auditory factors. Still, there is little research on the effect of visual stimuli on the evaluation of urban soundscapes. Research on multisensory interactions in urban public space mainly focuses on audio-visual interactions. The visual and auditory perception of landscape is not independent of each other[18][19]. In urban public spaces, the consistency of sounds and visuals can influence people’s perception of environmental quality; in a previous study, audio and visual stimulus combined resulted in higher environmental preference scores[20]. In an audio-visual environment, attention to the visual landscape can decrease focus on the sounds and vice versa[21]. As for the influence of visual stimuli on auditory perception, the more urban the landscape, the more complex are the elements that influence auditory perception[22]. In a comprehensive audio-visual environment, the influence of visual parameters on sound is more significant[23]. Some experimental audio-visual studies have shown that auditory ratings are strongly influenced by visual images[24]. In one study, pictures of beaches and highways were used as visual stimulus sources. It was found that people perceived the same sound differently under different conditions with visual information, explaining the effect of visual information on auditory perception from the perspective of auditory differences[25]. Laboratory studies of the sounds and images of various water features were able to determine that the evaluation of the soundscape was influenced by the

In the study of the audio-visual interaction and perception of waterscape by road traffic noise, natural-looking features tended to increase preference scores (compared to audio-only paired comparison scores). In contrast, artificial-looking features decreased them[27]. Studies on audio-visual interaction of waterscapes have found that the evaluation of waterscapes is influenced not only by water sounds and images, but also by other elements of the soundscape. The influence of music is significant. Music is considered by people as an ideal soundscape or an imitation of the soundscape of the environment[28]. Therefore, the properties of music and the psychoacoustic parameters of music perception can also be applied to the study of the soundscapes[29]. In music psychology and psychoacoustics, auditory perception is generally studied in terms of aspects of loudness, pitch, rhythm, and timbre[30][31][32][33]. Nowadays, most water landscapes in cities combine artificial water features with different music, such as large musical fountains and water curtain shows, in addition to some natural landscapes, which invite people to linger and enjoy. 

In the design of waterscapes, the preference for waterscapes is one of the most important elements to be considered in the creation of urban public spaces. Therefore, this study aims to investigate the relationship between visual form and sound preference for water features, the influence of environment on sound preference, and the evaluation of sound preference for water sounds and music with different rhythms. This study of water features, environment, and background music was conducted in the city square of Harbin, China, considering both visual and auditory aspects. 

## **2. Methods** 

In addition to physical characteristics, the following factors must also be considered when determining water sounds: the region, the people, and the history. Therefore, this study used a combination of subjective on-site questionnaires and laboratory testing to investigate sound preferences for water features in plazas. 

## _2.1 Field study_ 

## _2.1.1 Selection of urban parks_ 

Since most of the water features in Harbin are fountains, three sampling sites were selected according to the degree of enclosure of the square. Fig.1 shows the aerial view of the three squares: (a) The fountain square of Saint Sophia Cathedral is semi-open, surrounded by buildings, and adjacent to the city’s main traffic artery. It is mainly affected by traffic and ambient noise from the surrounding commercial area. The crowd consists mainly of foreign tourists and residents passing through; (b) flood control monument square is located near Songhua; there is flowing water on one side and a commercial area on the other side. The building complex is some distance away and the fountain square is mainly affected by traffic noise. The crowd is mainly tourists and local residents; (c) the central square of Qun Li is located in a residential area. Although there are traffic arteries all around, traffic noise cannot be heard because of the large square area and dense planting. The crowd consists mainly of local residents.

## _2.1.2 Measurement of the audio-visual environment_ 

Water sounds, ambient sounds, and music in urban public spaces were selected as data collection objects. At each measurement point, a ten-channel high-fidelity recorder was used to record the sounds of the waterscape and the environment for 30s[18]. The length of the sound should allow participants to see the changes in the fountain. The device is placed vertically on the ground at a height of 1.6 metres. At the same time, a Canon EOS 5D camera was used to record video and capture images of the waterscape and the fountain site environment at the three locations. As the sounds of the fountain changed rhythmically, we captured the various dynamic effects in the audio and video recordings. BSWA801 sound vibration analyzers were used to record the sound pressure level of the scene. 

Fig.2 shows the spectra of the water sounds and music recorded during the experiment. After adding the music, the fountain is dynamic. Therefore, the water sound matches the rhythm of the music, so some parts of the sound component in the high frequency band that appeared in the existing water sound disappear. Studies have shown that 40-70 bpm is defined as slow speed, 85-110 bpm as medium speed, and above 120 bpm as fast speed[34]. As can be seen in the picture, the frequency of the water sound is quite stable, and the variability over time is not obvious. However, the changes in frequency and variability over time become more apparent when overlaid with different musical rhythms.

## _2.1.3 Participants_ 

While measurements were being taken at the three locations, questionnaires were randomly administered to the people at the site, with 50 valid questionnaires for each site, totalling 150 participants (65.3% females) [35][36]. The average age (QL) 34 (SD = 13.8; Min = 20; Max = 62), the average age (FCMS) 31 (SD = 13.2; Min = 17; Max = 62) the average age (SSC) 31 (SD = 11.3; Min = 17; Max = 55). 

The evaluation questionnaire was divided into two main parts: The first part concerned demographic information, including gender, age, and purpose of visit; the second part assessed participants’ perception of the soundscape. A Likert scale was used to identify and rate different sources of sound in each location: -2: inaudible, -1: hear little, 0: hear moderately, 1: hear a lot, and 2: hear all the time. The main types of noise sources included: natural sounds (water, wind, and animal sounds), human sounds (speech, footsteps, and activities), and other sounds (music, traffic noise, and mechanical noise). 

The field research questionnaire found that (1) the dominant sounds in the fountain plazas were those of water and music, followed by traffic noise and the sound of human activity; these sounds influenced

## _2.2 Laboratory study_ 

## _2.2.1 Laboratory settings_ 

The laboratory volume was 186 m[3] and the background noise was 11 dBa. As shown in Fig.3, a 75inch LCD TV (UA75F6400) was used for video playback, and participants were asked to sit in designated seats. Audio playback was provided by an RME FireFace UFX sound card and Genelec sound. There were no items in the lab other than the necessary equipment, allowing participants to focus on the test[37]. Sometimes a sound could seem too loud and cause discomfort when the same sound pressure level was simulated in the laboratory as in the field; this is due to the difference between their background sound pressure levels. Therefore, the actual sound pressure level in the laboratory experiment was attenuated and calibrated and measured using a HMS IV artificial head to simulate a human ear. The adjustment of sound pressure level was processed by Adobe Audition software.

The sound environment of the three squares is as follows: Qun Li Central Square is mainly composed of water and music; Saint Sophia Cathedral Square is mainly composed of water, music, traffic noise, and human activities; and Flood Control Monument Square is mainly composed of water, music, traffic noise, mechanical noise, human conversation, and activities. Spearman correlations were performed to test the correlations between the evaluation indicators. The higher the complexity of the environment, the lower the acoustic preference.

## **4.  Discussion** 

## _4.1. Applications_ 

When designing water features in urban public spaces, the visual factors of the environment must be considered. Pleasantness dimension is dependent on visual scenery [43]. In significant places, such as famous tourist attractions, the surrounding traffic and pedestrian flows are usually more complex. Therefore, it may be beneficial to consider the use of dynamic and varied water features in combination with a rhythmic design that can effectively increase people’s preference for the water features. In plazas that are more open and have fewer disturbances, large and varied water features can be used to encourage visitors to pause and enjoy the sight; steady waterscapes combined with light music can also be used in such plazas. While this may not necessarily grab people’s attention, it can make the plaza a favourite place to relax. 

## _4.2. Limitations and future research directions_ 

The results showed the preference for waterscapes, the influence of the environment on the preference for waterscape sounds, and the influence of music on the preference for waterscape sounds from visual and auditory perspectives. As for auditory factors, there are many other sounds that can be used in addition to water sounds and music in urban public spaces. After the COVID-19 lockdown the number of noise complaints increased significantly [44]. The relationship between different sounds mixed with water sounds and their influence on the evaluation of preferences for waterscapes can be investigated in future studies. This is also of relevance in terms of improving the comfort of the acoustic environment when people are working from home. 

Visual factors have a greater influence on the evaluation of waterscape preferences than auditory factors; changes in the waterscape environment influence the overall evaluation of sound preferences. However, a relationship between visual form and environment was not demonstrated. A significant difference (p < 0.05) between the visual shape of the waterscape and the sound preference of the environment was found with ANOVA. In future research, the water elements and the environment can be analysed using eye-tracking devices. 

## **5.  Conclusions** 

The purpose of this research was to conduct a field and laboratory study of water features in three parking lots in Harbin by comparing different audio and visual combinations to evaluate sound

preferences for water features. It also investigated the influence of visual form, environment, and background music on the evaluation of sound preferences. 

In evaluating the visual form and overall sound preference for the waterscape, this study found that compared to the steady, continuous fountain, the fountain with dynamic and irregular changes was more popular. Preference for waterscape with visual shapes was positively correlated with general sound preference for water features, with correlation coefficients of R(SF)=0.47 (p<0.01), R(DF)=0.44 (p<0.01), and R(DFR)=0.77 (p < 0.01). 

Thus, it can be concluded that the environment of the waterscape influences the overall evaluation of the sound, with the complex visual environment being significantly related to the preference for the waterscape. However, the influence of the surrounding acoustic environment on the overall rating is not significant. 

Regarding the influence of music on the evaluation of waterscapes, light music is more effective in improving the evaluation of stationary waterscapes, such as ordinary continuous fountains. The rhythm of intense and varied music can improve the evaluation of dynamic waterscapes through certain rhythmic changes, such as in the case of a musical fountain.
